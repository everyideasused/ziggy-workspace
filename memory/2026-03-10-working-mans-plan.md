Critical Facts & Governing Rules What Is ASE Master Automobile Technician ? ASE (Automotive Service Excellence) is the nationally recognized nonprofit certification body for the automotive service industry, active since 1972. Master Automobile Technician status is earned by passing all eight A-series certification exams: A1 through A8. Over 240,000 professionals hold ASE certification; roughly 58,000 hold Master Technician status. Critical clarification: A9 (Light Vehicle Diesel Engines) is not required for Master Technician status. Passing A1–A8 earns the credential. A9 is a valuable supplemental certification, and this plan includes it, but the Master designation is awarded upon completion of A1–A8. Eligibility Requirements ASE requires one of the following before you can sit for exams: Path A: 2 years of relevant hands-on work experience in automotive repair, OR Path B: 1 year of relevant hands-on experience + a 2-year degree or equivalent formal training program Your EET degree coursework combined with documented garage hours will satisfy Path B.

Start logging every repair from April 2026 forward. By the time you sit for your first exam in Fall 2027, you will have 18 months of documented hands-on work, which — combined with your active EET enrollment — positions you well. Important: ASE’s experience verification is self-certified during registration. You attest that you meet the requirement. However, ASE reserves the right to audit. Keep your experience log meticulous and honest. Exam Format All A-series exams are computer-based, multiple-choice, and administered at Prometric test centers. Each exam includes 10 additional unscored “research” questions mixed in with scored questions — you will not know which ones they are, so answer every question. Results are reported as pass/fail on a scaled scoring system, and you receive instant results at the test center. Floor jacks are lifting devices, not support devices. They can fail, leak, or tip. Always place rated jack stands under the frame or designated lift points before putting any part of your body under the vehicle. Use a minimum of 3-ton rated stands for your Jeeps. Wear safety glasses for every task. Battery acid, brake fluid, coolant, rust particles, and spring-loaded clips can all reach your eyes. Make this automatic — glasses go on when you walk into the garage. Wear nitrile or mechanic’s gloves. Brake dust contains metals and formerly contained asbestos. Used motor oil is a known carcinogen with prolonged skin contact. Coolant (ethylene glycol) is toxic. Gloves protect you and keep your grip clean. Keep a fire extinguisher rated for Class B (flammable liquids) and Class C (electrical) within arm’s reach. A dry chemical ABC extinguisher ( $ 25–40 at any hardware store) covers all garage scenarios. Mount it on the wall near your primary work area, not buried behind tools. Work in a ventilated space. Running engines produce carbon monoxide — colorless, odorless, and lethal in an enclosed garage. Open the garage door fully or use an exhaust extraction hose whenever the engine is running. If you feel lightheaded, shut the engine off and get fresh air immediately. Tell someone what you’re doing. Working alone means nobody knows if something goes wrong.
