# Construction Safety & OSHA Residential

> **Purpose:** Safety on construction sites — the regulations, the hazards, and the practices that prevent injuries and fatalities. Construction is consistently one of the most dangerous industries. The four leading causes of fatalities (OSHA's "Focus Four") account for over 60% of construction deaths annually. Every builder, GC, and PM needs to understand these hazards and the minimum standards for managing them. For personal projects, safety discipline is even more critical — there's no safety officer watching, and the nearest hospital may be a long drive from a rural job site.

---

## OSHA's Focus Four — The Leading Killers

### 1. Falls (38% of construction fatalities)

The number one killer in construction. Falls from roofs, ladders, scaffolding, floor openings, and unprotected edges.

**OSHA residential fall protection standard (Subpart M — 1926.501):**
- Fall protection is required at **6 feet** above a lower level
- Options: guardrail systems, safety net systems, or personal fall arrest systems (harness + lanyard + anchor)
- **Residential construction exception (STD 03-11-002):** Conventional fall protection may be infeasible on certain residential tasks (steep-slope roofing, leading-edge framing). In those cases, a compliant fall protection plan with alternative measures is acceptable — but the plan must be site-specific, written, and supervised.

**Practical fall protection for residential builders:**

| Situation | Protection Method |
|-----------|------------------|
| Roof work (pitch ≤ 8/12) | Guardrails at eaves, or harness with roof anchor |
| Roof work (pitch > 8/12) | Harness with roof anchor + toe boards. Very steep pitches require additional measures. |
| Floor openings (stairwells, duct chases) | Cover (secured, labeled "HOLE") or guardrails on all exposed sides |
| Wall openings (before windows installed) | Guardrails or covers if opening is 6'+ above lower level |
| Scaffold work | Guardrails on all open sides and ends above 10'. Access ladder for each scaffold level. |
| Ladder use | 3-point contact always. Top of ladder extends 3' above landing. 4:1 pitch ratio (1' out for every 4' up). |
| Unprotected edges (deck framing, floor sheathing) | Guardrails or personal fall arrest at 6'+ above grade |

**Anchor points for harness systems:** Must support 5,000 lbs per person attached. Ridge anchors, truss anchors, and retractable lifelines are commercially available for residential roofing. Never tie off to a vent pipe, railing, or anything not rated as an anchor.

### 2. Struck-By (9% of fatalities)

Injuries from falling objects, swinging loads, moving vehicles, or ejected materials.

**Prevention:**
- Hard hats in multi-story work or anytime overhead hazards exist
- Secure tools and materials on elevated work surfaces (tool lanyards, toeboards on scaffolds)
- Never stand under a crane load, suspended materials, or workers above
- High-visibility vests around heavy equipment
- Barricade areas below overhead work
- Never operate a nail gun pointed toward anyone, even if the trigger isn't engaged

### 3. Caught-In/Between (1.4% of fatalities, but horrific injuries)

Workers caught in or compressed by equipment, materials, or collapsing structures.

**Prevention:**
- Never enter an unshored trench deeper than 5 feet. Trenching and excavation are regulated under OSHA 1926 Subpart P. Soil type determines the maximum allowable slope or the required shoring system.
- Keep hands and body clear of pinch points on equipment
- Lock out/tag out (LOTO) on any equipment being serviced
- Never work between a vehicle/equipment and a fixed object without the operator's awareness
- Secure stacked materials to prevent collapse (lumber, drywall, brick — all can shift and crush)

### 4. Electrocution (7% of fatalities)

Contact with overhead power lines, energized circuits, or faulty equipment.

**Prevention:**
- Assume all wires are energized until confirmed dead by the utility company
- Maintain 10-foot minimum clearance from overhead power lines (for equipment, ladders, scaffolding, and materials)
- GFCI protection on all temporary construction power (job site outlets, extension cords)
- Inspect cords and tools for damage before each use — replace frayed cords immediately
- Never use metal ladders near electrical work
- Qualified electricians only for work on energized panels and circuits
- Call 811 before any excavation to locate underground utilities

## Scaffolding Safety

Scaffolding is involved in a disproportionate number of construction injuries. Key requirements:

**Erection and use:**
- Scaffolds must be erected on firm, level footing (base plates and mudsills on soft ground)
- Planking must be scaffold-grade lumber or manufactured scaffold planks — never random 2x lumber
- Guardrails (top rail at 38-45", midrail, and toeboard) on all open sides above 10'
- Access by ladder or built-in stair — never climb the cross-braces
- Maximum platform span between supports depends on plank type (typically 7' for standard 2x10 scaffold plank)

**Capacity:**
- Light duty: 25 psf (designed for workers + hand tools only)
- Medium duty: 50 psf (workers + materials)
- Heavy duty: 75 psf (workers + stored materials)
- Never exceed the rated capacity. A full mortar pan, a stack of bricks, and two workers can exceed light-duty capacity quickly.

**Inspection:** A competent person must inspect scaffolding before each shift and after any event that could affect structural integrity (high wind, impact, modification).

## Ladder Safety

Ladders are the most commonly used and most commonly misused access equipment.

**Stepladders:**
- Never stand on the top two steps (or the top cap)
- Never use a stepladder as a leaning ladder (unless it's rated for that use)
- Fully open and lock the spreaders
- Weight rating must exceed your weight plus tools and materials

**Extension ladders:**
- Set at 4:1 angle (base out 1 foot for every 4 feet of height)
- Extend 3 feet above the landing or roof edge
- Secure at the top (tie off) to prevent lateral movement
- Both feet on firm, level ground (leg levelers for uneven ground)
- Face the ladder when climbing — three points of contact at all times (two hands + one foot, or two feet + one hand)

**Duty ratings:**

| Type | Duty Rating | Max Load |
|------|------------|----------|
| Type IAA | Special Duty | 375 lbs |
| Type IA | Extra Heavy Duty | 300 lbs |
| Type I | Heavy Duty | 250 lbs |
| Type II | Medium Duty | 225 lbs |
| Type III | Light Duty | 200 lbs |

**Remember:** Max load includes your body weight PLUS tools and materials. A 200-lb person carrying 30 lbs of tools exceeds a Type III ladder's rating.

### Silica Dust — The Hidden Killer

Crystalline silica dust (from cutting concrete, masonry, stone, and fiber cement products) causes silicosis — a fatal, irreversible lung disease. OSHA's silica standard (1926.1153) requires:
- Wet cutting for concrete, masonry, and stone (water suppresses dust)
- Vacuum attachment on grinders and saws when wet cutting isn't feasible
- Respiratory protection (minimum N95, but half-face respirator with P100 filters recommended for regular exposure)
- Exposure monitoring for workers regularly cutting silica-containing materials

**This applies to DIY builders too.** Cutting a concrete slab with a dry saw produces silica levels hundreds of times above the safety limit. Always use water or a vacuum-equipped saw.

## Electrical Safety on Construction Sites

**Temporary power requirements:**
- GFCI protection on ALL temporary outlets and extension cords (either GFCI-protected outlet or portable GFCI device)
- Daily inspection of cords and tools — remove damaged equipment from service
- No modified plugs (three-prong plug with the ground pin removed)
- Extension cords are temporary — they are not permanent wiring and should not be fastened to structure, run through walls, or used as a substitute for permanent circuits
- Cord sets must be rated for the load (most 16-gauge cords max at 13A; use 12-gauge for high-draw tools)

**Working around existing electrical:**
- Before cutting into any wall, floor, or ceiling, locate electrical with a stud finder or non-contact voltage tester
- Turn off and verify dead before working on or near any circuit (breaker off + verified with a tester at the point of work)
- Never assume a circuit is dead because the breaker is labeled — labels are frequently wrong

## First Aid and Emergency Preparedness

Every construction site (including personal projects) should have:

- **First aid kit** — stocked and accessible (bandages, gauze, antiseptic, eye wash, tweezers, burn gel, tourniquet)
- **Emergency contact list** — posted and in phone (911, nearest hospital, poison control: 1-800-222-1222)
- **Fire extinguisher** — at least one 10-lb ABC extinguisher, accessible and serviced
- **Emergency plan** — know the route to the nearest hospital from the job site. Know where the utility shutoffs are (water main, electrical panel, gas meter).

**For solo work:** Tell someone where you are and when you expect to be done. Keep your phone charged and accessible. Solo work at heights, in trenches, or with heavy equipment carries significantly higher risk because there's no one to call for help if you're incapacitated.

## The Safety Culture Principle

Safety isn't a set of rules — it's a mindset. The safest job sites share a common trait: everyone feels empowered to stop work when something doesn't look right, and no one gets punished for raising a safety concern. Whether you're a GC running a crew or a homeowner working alone on a Saturday, the principle is the same: **slow down when something feels wrong.** The five minutes you spend repositioning a ladder, tying off a safety line, or putting on a respirator is never wasted time.
