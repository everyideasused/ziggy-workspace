# Framing & Structural Carpentry

> **Purpose:** The skeleton of a building — wall framing, floor systems, roof systems, structural connections, and layout. This is the core trade skill of a carpenter and the system that every other building component depends on. Understanding framing means understanding why buildings stand up, how loads transfer, and where structural failures originate.

---

## Wall Framing

### Components

**Bottom plate (sole plate)** — The horizontal member at the base of the wall. Pressure-treated if on concrete (slab or foundation). Anchored to the foundation with anchor bolts or powder-actuated fasteners. Layout marks for stud spacing start here.

**Studs** — Vertical members, typically 2x4 or 2x6, spaced at 16" or 24" on center (OC). Stud spacing is measured from the center of one stud to the center of the next. 16" OC is standard for load-bearing walls and anywhere drywall or sheathing panels need support at their edges (4' wide panels land on studs at 16" but not at 24" for horizontal installation).

**Top plate and double top plate** — Two horizontal members at the top of the wall. The double plate ties intersecting walls together and bridges gaps at stud spacing. Top plate splices must be offset from the lower plate splice by at least 48". Overlapping at corners and intersections is what connects walls to each other structurally.

**King studs** — Full-height studs on each side of an opening. They carry the double top plate continuously across the opening.

**Jack studs (trimmers)** — Shortened studs that support the header at each side of an opening. They transfer the header load down to the bottom plate. The number of jacks required depends on the header span (one jack per side for spans up to 4', two for wider spans — check local code and span tables).

**Headers** — Horizontal members spanning door and window openings in load-bearing walls. They carry the load from above the opening to the jack studs. Common header materials: doubled 2x lumber with plywood spacer, engineered LVL beams, or steel flitch plates. Header sizing depends on span, tributary load area, and snow load. In non-bearing walls, a single flat 2x4 is sufficient as a header (it's just holding up the cripples, not structural load).

**Cripple studs** — Short studs above headers (carrying top plate to header) and below window sills (carrying sill to bottom plate). Must maintain the same spacing as the full studs to support sheathing.

**Rough sill** — Horizontal member at the bottom of a window opening, supported by cripple studs below. Not structural in the same way as a header — it's primarily a nailing surface for the window unit.

### Wall Layout Process

1. **Snap chalk lines** on the floor deck for all plate locations (inside and outside faces of walls)
2. **Mark stud layout** starting from the same corner of the building. Consistency matters — sheathing and drywall panels must land on studs predictably.
3. **Transfer layout** to both top and bottom plates simultaneously (lay them side by side, mark both at once)
4. **Mark all openings** with king stud, jack stud, header, sill, and cripple positions
5. **Cut all components** before assembly (production cutting is faster than one-at-a-time)
6. **Assemble on the deck** — lay bottom plate, studs, and top plate flat, nail together
7. **Raise the wall** — tip up into position, brace, plumb, and secure
8. **Install the double top plate** after walls are raised and aligned (laps tie walls together)

### Corners and Intersections

**Corners** must provide nailing surface for drywall on both sides and structural connection between the two walls. Traditional method: three studs in an L-shape. Modern/efficient method: two studs with drywall clips or a flat 2x nailer on the inside face (saves lumber and reduces thermal bridging).

**T-intersections** (partition wall meeting exterior wall): Traditional method: three studs in a channel. Efficient method: ladder blocking — flat 2x blocks between studs to provide nailing surface.

### Advanced Framing (OVE — Optimum Value Engineering)

Uses 2x6 studs at 24" OC with single top plates (using metal connector plates at intersections), two-stud corners, and right-sized headers. Benefits: less lumber, more insulation cavity space, less thermal bridging. Tradeoffs: requires careful planning, not all sheathing/cladding systems span 24" well, some drywall installers resist 24" spacing. More common in energy-efficient construction.

## Floor Systems

### Dimensional Lumber Floors

**Joists** — 2x8, 2x10, or 2x12 spanning between bearing walls or beams. Span depends on species, grade, spacing, and load. Common spans (40 psf live load, Southern Pine #2):
- 2x8 at 16" OC: ~12' span
- 2x10 at 16" OC: ~15' span  
- 2x12 at 16" OC: ~18' span

**Rim joist (band joist)** — Closes the perimeter of the floor system, sitting on top of the sill plate. Critical for air sealing and insulation. Also transfers vertical loads from above walls to the foundation.

**Blocking** — Short pieces installed between joists at bearing points, mid-span (for stiffness), and at edges where walls above run parallel to joists. Required where joists bear on supports to prevent rotation.

**Bridging** — Cross-bracing between joists to prevent lateral movement and distribute point loads. Required for joist depths exceeding certain thresholds. Can be wood cross-bridging, solid blocking, or metal bridging.

### Engineered Floor Systems

**I-joists (TJI)** — Engineered members with a plywood/OSB web and LVL or solid lumber flanges. Advantages: longer spans, lighter weight, pre-punched knockout holes for MEP routing, consistent dimensions (no crowning, twisting, or shrinking). Disadvantages: must not be notched or cut (except at pre-engineered knockouts), require web stiffeners at bearing points, and are more susceptible to fire (thinner web burns through faster).

**Floor trusses** — Open-web trusses allow MEP routing through the open spaces without any drilling or notching. Excellent for long spans and complex MEP layouts. Typically 12"-24" deep. Require engineer's design.

**LVL and PSL beams** — Laminated veneer lumber and parallel strand lumber are engineered products for beams and headers. Much stronger than dimensional lumber of the same size. Used for carrying beams, long-span headers, and point-load applications. Never notch or drill engineered beams without engineering approval.

### Subfloor

**Material:** 3/4" tongue-and-groove OSB or plywood (APA-rated Sturd-I-Floor). Plywood is more moisture-resistant and feels stiffer underfoot; OSB is cheaper and more commonly used.

**Installation:** Glue AND screw for squeak-free performance. Construction adhesive (PL Premium or equivalent) on every joist. Ring-shank nails or screws at 6" OC on edges, 12" OC in the field. Stagger end joints. Leave 1/8" gap at panel edges for expansion.

**Squeak prevention:** Squeaks come from fastener movement and panel edges rubbing. Glue eliminates 90% of squeaks. Screws hold better than nails over time. Ensuring joists are crowned consistently (crown up) prevents high spots that cause subfloor bounce.

## Roof Systems

### Stick Framing vs. Trusses

**Stick-framed roofs** — Built in place from individual rafters, ridge boards, collar ties, and ceiling joists. More labor-intensive but allows complex geometries, vaulted ceilings, and attic space. The carpenter cuts each rafter to fit, which requires understanding rafter layout, birdsmouth cuts, and roof geometry.

**Truss roofs** — Pre-engineered, factory-built assemblies delivered to site and set by crane. Faster, often cheaper (less site labor), and engineered for exact loads. However, trusses cannot be modified in the field — no cutting, notching, or removing members. Attic space is typically unusable. Truss design must account for all loads, bearing conditions, and any special requirements (piggyback trusses for steep pitches, scissor trusses for vaulted ceilings, girder trusses for carrying other trusses).

### Key Roof Framing Concepts

**Pitch** — The slope of the roof, expressed as rise/run. A 6/12 pitch rises 6 inches for every 12 inches of horizontal run. Common residential pitches: 4/12 to 12/12. Steeper pitch = more material and labor but better water shedding, more attic space, and greater design impact.

**Rafter span** — The horizontal distance from the bearing wall to the ridge. Rafter size depends on span, spacing, pitch, species, grade, and snow/wind loads. Span tables in the IRC provide prescriptive sizing.

**Birdsmouth cut** — The notch in the rafter where it sits on the top plate. Creates a flat bearing surface. The seat cut (horizontal part) should be at least 1.5" to fully bear on the plate. The plumb cut (vertical part) should not exceed 1/3 of the rafter depth (to avoid weakening the rafter at the bearing point).

**Ridge board vs. ridge beam** — A ridge board is a non-structural member that rafters bear against for alignment. It requires opposing rafters to push against each other, with collar ties or ceiling joists to prevent the walls from spreading. A ridge beam is a structural member that carries the rafter loads (like a header turned on its side). Required when collar ties or ceiling joists cannot provide the necessary restraint (vaulted ceilings with no attic ties).

**Collar ties** — Horizontal members connecting opposing rafters, typically in the upper third of the rafter span. They resist ridge separation under wind uplift but do NOT prevent wall spreading (that's the ceiling joist's or rafter tie's job). Common misconception: collar ties are not the same as rafter ties.

**Rafter ties** — Horizontal members at the bottom of the rafter span (at or near the wall plate level) that resist the outward thrust of the rafters. Ceiling joists often serve this function. If ceiling joists are removed or run perpendicular to the rafters, separate rafter ties are required.

**Hurricane / uplift connections** — Metal connectors (Simpson H2.5, H10, etc.) tying rafters or trusses to the top plate. Required by code in most areas. Resist uplift forces that try to lift the roof off the walls during high winds. The load path must be continuous from the roof through the walls to the foundation — straps, hold-downs, and anchor bolts all contribute.

## Structural Connections — The Hardware

Modern residential framing relies heavily on engineered metal connectors. The most common:

| Connector | Purpose | Typical Product |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| Joist hanger | Supports joist ends at beams or headers | Simpson LUS, HUS series |
| Hurricane tie / rafter tie | Resists roof uplift at wall plate | Simpson H2.5, H10 |
| Hold-down | Resists shear wall overturning at foundation | Simpson HDU, STHD series |
| Strap tie | Connects members across a joint (plate to plate, stud to plate) | Simpson LSTA, CS series |
| Post base | Connects post to concrete foundation/pier | Simpson ABU, CB series |
| Post cap | Connects beam to top of post | Simpson BC series |
| Ledger connector | Secures deck ledger to house framing | Simpson DSTC series |

**Critical rules:**
- Use the correct fasteners specified by the manufacturer (not random nails or screws)
- Install in the correct orientation (many connectors are directional)
- Do not omit fastener holes — every hole in the connector gets a nail or bolt
- Simpson Strong-Tie publishes load ratings based on full fastener installation — missing fasteners proportionally reduce capacity

## Notching and Boring Rules

Framing members have strict limits on where and how much material you can remove for MEP routing:

### Studs (Bearing Walls)

- **Notching:** Maximum 25% of stud depth. For a 2x4 (3.5" actual), max notch is 7/8". For a 2x6 (5.5" actual), max notch is 1-3/8". Notches must be in the middle third of the stud length.
- **Boring:** Maximum 40% of stud depth. Holes must be at least 5/8" from the edge. For a 2x4, max bore is 1-3/8". For a 2x6, max bore is 2-3/16".
- **Nail plates** are required when holes or notches are within 1-1/4" of the face of the stud (to protect from drywall nails/screws hitting pipes or wires).

### Joists

- **Notching:** Only in the outer 1/3 of the span. Maximum depth = 1/6 of the joist depth. For a 2x10 (9.25" actual), max notch is 1-9/16". No notching in the middle third of the span — this is the maximum bending stress zone.
- **Boring:** Holes must be at least 2" from the top and bottom edges. Maximum diameter = 1/3 of the joist depth. For a 2x10, max hole is 3-1/16".

### Engineered Members (I-joists, LVL, PSL)

**Never notch, cut, or bore engineered members** unless the manufacturer explicitly allows it at that location. I-joists have pre-punched knockout holes in the web — use only those. LVL beams and PSL beams should never be modified in the field. Violating this voids the engineering and can cause catastrophic failure.

## Plumb, Level, and Square — The Three Fundamentals

Every measurement in framing comes down to these three things:

**Plumb** — Perfectly vertical. Checked with a level held against the member. Walls must be plumb before sheathing — sheathing locks in any error.

**Level** — Perfectly horizontal. Checked with a level or a laser. Floor systems must be level before walls go up. A 1/4" variance in 10' is generally acceptable for rough framing. Finish work demands much tighter tolerances.

**Square** — Corners at exactly 90 degrees. Checked with the 3-4-5 triangle method (or any multiple: 6-8-10, 9-12-15). Measure 3' along one wall, 4' along the other — the diagonal should be exactly 5'. Also checked by measuring diagonals of rectangular spaces — if both diagonals are equal, the rectangle is square.

**Why this matters:** An out-of-plumb wall creates cascading problems: sheathing doesn't fit, siding looks wrong, cabinets don't hang flat, doors don't swing properly. An out-of-level floor makes every finish trade's life harder. An out-of-square room means tile patterns won't work, cabinets have gaps, and countertops require scribe cuts.
