# 💊 Vegan Supplementation Protocol

> **Purpose:** Evidence-based supplement stack for a vegan athlete on a lean bulk. Separates non-negotiable supplements from beneficial additions, and beneficial additions from marketing noise. No supplement replaces a well-planned whole food diet.

---

## The Evidence Hierarchy

**Tier 1 — Non-Negotiable (deficiency inevitable without supplementation):**
B12, Vitamin D3, Algae EPA/DHA

**Tier 2 — Highly Beneficial for Athletic Performance (strong evidence):**
Creatine monohydrate

**Tier 3 — Beneficial for Optimization (moderate evidence, context-dependent):**
Iodine (if no seaweed), Zinc, Vitamin K2, Beta-alanine, Protein powder (as food tool)

**Tier 4 — Situational (test-and-supplement only):**
Iron, Calcium, Magnesium, Selenium (unless not eating 2 Brazil nuts/day)

**Tier 5 — Marketing Noise (insufficient evidence for vegans who eat well):**
Most "superfoods," antioxidant mega-doses, most individual amino acids (if eating adequate protein), most herbal supplements for "performance"

## Daily Supplement Stack — Nathan's Protocol

### Morning (with breakfast)

| Supplement | Dose | Form | Why |
|-----------|------|------|-----|
| Vitamin B12 | 1,000mcg | Methylcobalamin sublingual | Irreversible nerve damage risk if deficient |
| Vitamin D3 (lichen) | 2,000 IU (winter: 4,000 IU) | Softgel with fat meal | Bone, immune, mood — near-universal insufficiency |
| Vitamin K2 (MK-7) | 100–200mcg | Softgel | Directs calcium to bones, complements D3 |
| Algae EPA/DHA | 250–500mg combined | Softgel | Only reliable vegan omega-3 source; anti-inflammatory |
| Iodine (as potassium iodide) | 150mcg | Tablet OR iodized salt | Thyroid function — often missing in vegan diet |

### With Workout or Post-Workout

| Supplement | Dose | Form | Why |
|-----------|------|------|-----|
| Creatine monohydrate | 3–5g | Powder mixed in liquid | ATP replenishment, strength gains, cell volumization — vegans respond most strongly |
| Beta-alanine | 1.6g (split doses, 4x) | Powder | Muscle carnosine → buffering → delayed fatigue in 8-15 rep range |

### As Needed / Weekly

| Supplement | Dose | Trigger |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Zinc picolinate | 15–25mg | If pumpkin seeds + hemp seeds are not consistently in diet |
| Magnesium glycinate | 200–400mg (evening) | If sleep quality is poor or muscle cramping |
| Iron (bisglycinate) | Per lab result | ONLY if ferritin <20 ng/mL on bloodwork |

### Food-Based "Supplements" (Best Form)

| "Supplement" | Food Form | Protocol |
|-------------|----------|---------|
| Selenium | 2 Brazil nuts | Daily — most reliable dose |
| Choline | Soy foods + cruciferous veg | 3–4 servings soy + broccoli/Brussels daily |
| Vitamin A (as beta-carotene) | Orange/yellow vegetables | Sweet potato, carrot, squash daily |
| Vitamin E | Sunflower seeds, almonds, EVOO | Daily with fat-containing meals |

## Supplement Quality Standards

### What to Look For

**Third-party testing (critical for supplements):**
- NSF Certified for Sport
- Informed Sport / Informed Choice
- USP Verified
- ConsumerLab tested

**Why it matters:** FDA does not regulate supplements like drugs. Third-party testing verifies actual contents and purity.

**Heavy metal testing:** Particularly important for:
- Protein powders (especially rice and hemp — can accumulate arsenic, lead)
- Algae/seaweed products (iodine + heavy metal accumulation)
- Green powders

### Forms That Matter

| Nutrient | Best Form | Avoid |
|---------|----------|-------|
| B12 | Methylcobalamin or cyanocobalamin | — |
| D3 | Lichen-derived cholecalciferol | D2 (ergocalciferol) — less potent |
| K2 | MK-7 (menaquinone-7) | MK-4 (shorter half-life) |
| Magnesium | Glycinate, malate, or threonate | Oxide (poorly absorbed, laxative) |
| Zinc | Picolinate, bisglycinate | Oxide (poor absorption) |
| Iron | Bisglycinate, ferrous sulfate | Ferric forms (lower absorption) |
| Omega-3 | Algae oil (triglyceride form) | Ethyl ester algae (less stable) |
| Creatine | Monohydrate (micronized) | Any other form — no evidence advantage |

## Protein Powder — Using Food Strategically

### When Protein Powder Is Appropriate
- Post-workout when whole food isn't accessible
- As a meal component (not meal replacement)
- When daily protein targets are difficult to hit from whole foods alone
- Smoothie base for caloric surplus

### When Protein Powder Is NOT a Replacement
- As a substitute for whole food meals (missing fiber, micronutrients, phytonutrients)
- As the primary protein source (whole foods should dominate)
- When whole food is available (always prefer whole food)

### Best Vegan Protein Powders (Evidence-Based)

**Recommended:**
- **Pea + Rice blend (70:30):** Near-complete amino profile, high leucine, clean taste
  - Look for: Third-party tested, minimal additives, stevia or no sweetener
- **Soy protein isolate:** Truly complete protein, high digestibility
  - Best for those not eating enough whole soy
- **Pea protein alone:** Excellent if supplementing with other lysine-rich foods

**Skip:**
- Plant protein "blends" with 8+ protein sources (marketing complexity, not better nutrition)
- High-sugar flavored powders
- Proprietary blends with undisclosed amounts

### Post-Workout Shake Formula

```
Base: 2 scoops pea/rice protein (40–50g protein)
Carbs: 1 ripe banana (27g carbs, potassium)
Slow carbs: ¼ cup rolled oats (20g carbs, beta-glucan)
Fat: 1 tbsp almond butter (healthy fat, calories for surplus)
Liquid: 1 cup fortified soy milk (7g protein, calcium, B12-fortified)
Optional: 3 tbsp hemp seeds (+10g protein, omega-3)
Optional: 1 tbsp nutritional yeast (+3g protein, B12 if fortified)
1 tsp creatine monohydrate (include here for convenience)

Total: ~55–72g protein | 50–75g carbs | 15–20g fat | 550–720 cal
```

## Supplements to Avoid

### Marketing Products with Limited Vegan-Specific Evidence

| Product | The Claim | The Reality |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| BCAA supplements | "Maximizes MPS" | Useless if eating adequate complete protein — you already get BCAAs |
| Most glutamine supplements | "Gut healing, muscle building" | Redundant if eating adequate protein; gut produces glutamine |
| Most "fat burner" supplements | Weight loss | Minimal effect; often contain stimulants; irrelevant for bulk |
| Collagen supplements | "Joint health, skin" | Animal-derived; plant "collagen boosters" have no evidence of equivalent |
| Spirulina as B12 | "Complete nutrition" | Pseudovitamin B12 analogues — inactive; dangerous substitution |
| Chlorella as iron | "Natural iron" | Some iron content but not a reliable strategy |
| Sea vegetables as iodine replacement (in large amounts) | "Natural iodine" | Extremely variable content; kelp risk of excess iodine |
| Most "superfood" green powders | "Fills nutrition gaps" | Real whole vegetables are better and far cheaper |
| Testosterone boosters | "Natural hormone support" | No evidence; some ingredients unsafe |

### When to Stop a Supplement

Stop if:
- Lab results show levels in optimal range (retest in 6 months to confirm food-only maintenance)
- Adverse reaction occurs (discontinue immediately, consult physician)
- Third-party testing reveals contamination
- No longer aligned with need (e.g., move to sunnier location → less D3 needed)

## Supplement Schedule Summary

### Daily (Non-Negotiable)
- AM with food: B12 (1,000mcg), D3 (2,000–4,000 IU), K2 (100mcg), Algae DHA/EPA (300mg)
- Daily food: 2 Brazil nuts (selenium), iodized salt or nori

### Daily (Athletic Performance)
- Post-workout or with any meal: Creatine monohydrate (5g)
- Optional: Beta-alanine (1.6g × 4 doses spread through day)

### Weekly/As Needed
- Zinc: 15–25mg if not consistently eating pumpkin + hemp seeds
- Magnesium glycinate: 300–400mg at night if sleep quality poor

### Annually
- Blood panel: B12, MMA, D, ferritin, zinc, omega-3 index, CBC
- Adjust supplementation based on results

## Cost-Effective Supplement Strategy

### Priority Order If Budget Is Tight

1. **B12** — Cheapest supplement, absolute non-negotiable (~$5–10/3 months)
2. **D3** — Cheap, critical impact (~$10–15/3 months)
3. **Algae EPA/DHA** — More expensive but non-negotiable (~$20–30/month)
4. **Creatine monohydrate** — Cheap, massive performance ROI (~$15–20/3 months)
5. **K2 MK-7** — Inexpensive, D3 synergy (~$10–15/3 months)
6. **Iodine** — Essentially free via iodized salt

### Skip Until Budget Allows
- Beta-alanine (beneficial but not essential)
- Zinc (get from pumpkin seeds and hemp seeds first)
- Protein powder (whole food protein is better; use only for convenience)
