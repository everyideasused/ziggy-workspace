# Delay Documentation & Claims

> Cross-cutting reference for [[Construction PM Knowledge Base]]
> Related: [[Schedule Recovery & Acceleration]], [[Risk Management Framework]], [[07 - Construction Execution]], [[Contract Types & Structures]]

---

## Purpose

When a project is delayed, the question isn't just "how do we recover?" — it's "who pays for this?" The answer depends entirely on the quality of the contemporaneous documentation. A PM with good records can protect the client from unwarranted claims, support legitimate time extension requests, and resolve disputes before they become litigation. A PM with poor records is guessing — and guessing costs money. This note provides the documentation standards, claim procedures, and evidence frameworks Atlas needs to advise on delay-related disputes.

---

## The Documentation Principle

**Contemporaneous records win disputes. Retrospective reconstructions lose them.**

The single most important habit a PM can develop is documenting events as they happen — not a week later, not when the claim is filed, not when the lawyer asks. A daily report written the afternoon of the event is worth more than a detailed memo written three months later. Courts, arbitrators, and mediators consistently give greater weight to contemporaneous records because they're less susceptible to bias, memory distortion, and after-the-fact rationalization.

## Daily Report Standards

The GC's daily report is the foundational evidence document for any delay claim or defense. The PM should establish daily report requirements during preconstruction and monitor compliance throughout construction.

### Required Content (Every Day, Including Non-Work Days)

| Field | What to Record | Why It Matters |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| **Date** | Calendar date | Establishes the timeline |
| **Weather** | Morning and afternoon conditions: temp, precipitation (type and duration), wind. Record even on clear days. | Weather delay claims require comparison against actual conditions. No record of good weather = no baseline for bad weather. |
| **Manpower** | Each trade on site with headcount by trade (not total — per trade). Include GC's own forces. | Proves who was working and at what capacity. Essential for evaluating whether the GC was adequately staffing the project. |
| **Work performed** | Specific description of what work was done, in which areas, by which trades. Not "continued framing" — "Framed interior walls in Rooms 104-108 per sheet A-301." | Establishes actual progress against the schedule. Specific descriptions tie work to drawings and schedule activities. |
| **Work NOT performed** | What was scheduled but didn't happen, and why. "Plumbing rough-in scheduled for today; sub did not show. Called at 8am; no response." | Critical for delay documentation. If the GC's schedule says plumbing was supposed to start Monday and the sub didn't show until Thursday, the daily report is the evidence. |
| **Deliveries** | Material deliveries received, with quantity and condition. Note any damaged or missing items. | Supply chain delay evidence; damage claims |
| **Inspections** | Which inspections were called, who inspected, result (pass/fail/partial), any corrections required | Inspection-related delays are common and well-documented when daily reports capture them |
| **Visitors** | Owner, architect, consultants, AHJ officials on site with arrival/departure times | Establishes who was present and when — relevant for CA visit disputes and inspection coordination |
| **Issues / incidents** | Safety incidents, quality deficiencies, coordination conflicts, design errors discovered, unforeseen conditions | The event log. If it's not in the daily report, it didn't happen (in the eyes of a claim). |
| **Photos** | Minimum: one overview photo per active work area. Document any issues, conditions, or completed work before it's covered. | Visual evidence is difficult to dispute. A photo with a date stamp is powerful corroboration. |

### PM's Daily Report Review

The PM doesn't write the daily report (the GC's superintendent does), but the PM should:

- **Review daily reports weekly** at minimum, same-day during critical periods
- **Flag missing or inadequate reports** immediately — don't wait until there's a dispute
- **Supplement with PM field reports** for significant events, inspections, or meetings observed firsthand
- **Require photo documentation** for any condition that could become a dispute (unforeseen conditions, damaged work, weather impacts)

### Common Daily Report Failures

| Failure | Risk | PM Response |
|---------|------|-------------|
| Generic descriptions ("continued work") | No evidentiary value; can't tie to schedule activities | Require specificity per the reporting standard above |
| Missing days (no report filed) | Creates a gap in the record that opposing parties will exploit | Require daily submission; flag any missed reports at OAC |
| No weather data on clear days | Can't establish the baseline for weather delay analysis | Require weather every day, including "clear and 72°F — no impact" |
| No manpower counts by trade | Can't prove or disprove adequate staffing | Require per-trade headcounts, not just total bodies on site |
| No record of non-events | If the report doesn't say "plumber didn't show up," it looks like the plumber was there | Require recording of scheduled-but-not-performed work with explanations |
| Reports filed days later | Loses contemporaneous status; reduced evidentiary weight | Require same-day or next-morning submission |

## Time Extension Requests

### GC-Initiated Time Extension Process

When the GC believes the project is entitled to additional time, the standard process is:

```
Step 1: GC identifies a delay event
    ↓
Step 2: GC provides written notice within the contractual notice period
        (typically 7-14 days from when the delay was identified or
         reasonably should have been identified)
    ↓
Step 3: GC submits formal time extension request with:
        - Description of the delay event
        - Classification (excusable/compensable per contract)
        - Time Impact Analysis (TIA) showing the effect on the critical path
        - Supporting documentation (daily reports, RFI logs, delivery records, etc.)
        - Number of days requested
    ↓
Step 4: PM reviews the request:
        - Is the notice timely?
        - Is the delay event valid and supported by evidence?
        - Is the TIA methodology sound?
        - Does the critical path impact match the claimed days?
        - Are there concurrent delays that offset the claim?
    ↓
Step 5: PM recommends approval, partial approval, or denial to the owner
    ↓
Step 6: Owner decides; formal change order executed (if approved)
    ↓
Step 7: Contract completion date adjusted; LD calculation updated
```

### PM Review Checklist for Time Extension Requests

| Review Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|------------|--------------|----------|
| **Timely notice** | Was the notice filed within the contractual window? | Late notice may waive the right to a time extension (depends on contract and jurisdiction) |
| **Excusability** | Is this actually an excusable delay per the contract? | Non-excusable delays (GC's fault) don't get time extensions |
| **Critical path impact** | Does the delay event actually affect the critical path, or does it have float? | Many GC claims inflate impact by using non-critical activities. Require TIA on the approved schedule. |
| **Concurrency** | Was the GC also causing delays at the same time? | Concurrent delays may offset the claim — neither party gets time in many jurisdictions |
| **Mitigation** | Did the GC take reasonable steps to mitigate the delay? | Failure to mitigate can reduce or eliminate a time extension |
| **Duration** | Are the claimed days reasonable? | Compare against actual impact, not theoretical worst-case |
| **Causation** | Is there a clear causal link between the event and the delay? | "The RFI took 3 weeks" doesn't mean the project was delayed 3 weeks if the work could continue in other areas |

### Common Time Extension Scenarios

| Scenario | Typical Outcome | PM Strategy |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Weather days exceeding historical norm** | Excusable, non-compensable. Time extension for days above norm. | Compare actual vs. baseline weather allowance established at preconstruction. Approve only the excess. |
| **Owner-directed design change** | Excusable and compensable. Time + cost. | Process as a change order with both cost and time impact. Require TIA. |
| **Permit delay (AHJ backlog)** | Usually excusable, non-compensable (depends on contract). | Document the delay cause (AHJ, not GC). Approve if beyond reasonable control. |
| **Unforeseen site conditions** | Excusable, typically compensable. | Verify conditions were truly unforeseeable (not identifiable from geotech or Phase I ESA). |
| **Material supply chain delay** | Depends on contract. Often excusable if industry-wide; non-excusable if GC ordered late. | Check when the GC placed the order vs. when they should have. Late ordering = non-excusable. |
| **Sub no-show / sub default** | Non-excusable (GC's responsibility to manage subs). | Deny time extension. GC must recover at own cost. See [[Subcontractor Default & Replacement]]. |
| **RFI response delay (architect)** | Excusable and compensable (if architect is owner's agent). | Track RFI response times rigorously. Compare actual vs. contractual response deadlines. |

## Owner-Caused Delay Documentation

When the owner (or owner's agents — architect, PM, consultants) causes a delay, the GC may be entitled to both time and money. The PM must document owner-caused delays with the same rigor as GC-caused delays, because this documentation protects the owner by establishing an accurate record and prevents inflated claims later.

### Common Owner-Caused Delays

| Owner Action | How It Causes Delay | Documentation Needed |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Late design decisions | GC can't proceed without direction | Decision request date, decision received date, days gap |
| Design changes during construction | Rework, re-procurement, re-inspection | ASI date, scope change description, schedule impact |
| Late owner-furnished items (FF&E, equipment, technology) | GC can't install what hasn't arrived | Contractual delivery date vs. actual delivery date |
| Slow RFI responses | Work in affected area stops | RFI submittal date, response date, days open vs. contractual target |
| Slow submittal reviews | Materials can't be ordered without approved submittals | Submittal date, return date, days vs. contractual target |
| Scope additions without time extension | More work in the same timeframe | Change order log showing added scope without corresponding time |
| Restricted site access | Can't work in areas the owner controls | Dates access was requested vs. dates access was granted |
| Third-party interference (other owner contractors) | Coordination conflicts with owner's separate contractors | Daily report entries documenting interference events |

### The PM's Dual Obligation

The PM has a dual documentation obligation:

1. **Protect the owner from inflated GC claims** by maintaining an independent record of what actually happened and when
2. **Acknowledge legitimate owner-caused delays** by documenting them honestly — denying valid delays damages credibility and strengthens the GC's position in any dispute

The worst position for a PM is to deny a legitimate delay, get overruled in arbitration, and then owe the GC time + money + legal costs + interest. Honest documentation serves the owner better than defensive documentation.

## Claim Structure and Presentation

When a delay results in a formal claim (from either party), the claim document should follow a standard structure. Atlas should advise on both preparing and evaluating claims.

### Standard Delay Claim Components

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Executive Summary** | One-page overview: what happened, who is responsible, what is claimed (time and/or money) |
| **Contract Basis** | Specific contract provisions that entitle the claimant to relief (time extension clause, differing site conditions, change order provisions) |
| **Factual Narrative** | Chronological account of events, supported by contemporaneous documentation references |
| **Delay Analysis** | Schedule analysis showing the impact on the critical path (TIA, as-planned vs. as-built, or windows analysis per [[Schedule Recovery & Acceleration]]) |
| **Causation** | Clear link between the delay event, the responsible party, and the schedule/cost impact |
| **Damages Calculation** | Itemized cost impact: extended general conditions, idle labor, material escalation, lost productivity, acceleration costs |
| **Supporting Documentation** | Appendices: daily reports, meeting minutes, RFI log, schedule updates, correspondence, photos, weather data |

### Evaluating a GC's Claim (PM Checklist)

| Question | If Yes | If No |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Is the claim timely per the contract? | Proceed with review | May be waived — consult contract and legal |
| Does the contract provide for this type of relief? | Evaluate on merits | Deny on contractual basis |
| Is the factual narrative supported by contemporaneous records? | Strong claim | Weak claim — request better documentation |
| Does the schedule analysis use an accepted methodology? | Evaluate the analysis | Reject and require proper analysis |
| Is the critical path impact real (not float consumption)? | Valid schedule impact | Impact may be overstated |
| Are damages reasonable and documented? | Negotiate on amounts | Challenge with independent estimate |
| Were concurrent delays present? | May offset or reduce the claim | Claim stands on its own |
| Did the GC mitigate? | Supports reasonableness | May reduce damages |

## Concurrent Delay

Concurrent delay — when both the owner and the GC are independently causing delays at the same time — is the most contested area of construction delay claims.

### General Principles

| Jurisdiction / Approach | Treatment of Concurrent Delay |
|------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Traditional (majority)** | Neither party recovers time or money. Each bears its own costs. |
| **Apportionment** | Allocate delay proportionally based on each party's contribution. Used in some jurisdictions and increasingly favored. |
| **Dominant cause** | The party that caused the "dominant" delay is responsible for the full impact. Rarely used; difficult to apply. |

### PM Strategy for Concurrent Delay

- **Document each delay independently.** Even if two delays overlap in time, track them as separate events with separate causes, separate evidence, and separate schedule impacts.
- **Require the GC to address concurrency in any TIA.** If the GC's time extension request doesn't acknowledge concurrent GC-caused delays, the analysis is incomplete.
- **Maintain an independent schedule analysis capability.** The PM should be able to evaluate whether the GC's claimed delay and an owner-caused delay are truly concurrent (overlapping on the critical path) or merely contemporaneous (happening at the same time but on different paths).

## Dispute Resolution Ladder

Most construction contracts provide a tiered dispute resolution process. The PM should know the tiers and manage issues at the lowest possible level.

### Typical Resolution Tiers

```
Tier 1: Field Resolution (PM + GC Project Manager)
    ↓ (if unresolved within 7-14 days)
Tier 2: Executive Negotiation (Owner principal + GC principal)
    ↓ (if unresolved within 14-30 days)
Tier 3: Mediation (neutral third party facilitates settlement)
    ↓ (if unresolved)
Tier 4: Arbitration or Litigation (binding decision by arbitrator or court)
```

### PM's Role at Each Tier

| Tier | PM's Job |
|------|----------|
| **Field resolution** | Negotiate directly. Most delays should be resolved here. Use documentation to support your position. Be fair — an unreasonable PM position at Tier 1 weakens the owner's credibility at Tier 3-4. |
| **Executive negotiation** | Prepare the briefing package for the owner's principal. Include the factual record, schedule analysis, contract provisions, and a recommended settlement range. |
| **Mediation** | Prepare the mediation statement. Organize all documentation chronologically. Identify areas of agreement to narrow the dispute. Have a settlement authority number from the owner before mediation begins. |
| **Arbitration / Litigation** | Support the owner's legal counsel. Prepare the schedule analysis, organize evidence, and be available as a fact witness. The PM is not an advocate — the PM is a professional witness presenting the documented record. |

## Record Retention

### What to Keep and How Long

| Document | Retention Period | Storage Format |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Daily reports | Duration of project + statute of limitations (varies by state; typically 4-10 years) | Digital (PDF or native format) |
| Meeting minutes (OAC, coordination) | Same as above | Digital |
| Schedule updates (all versions) | Same as above — **keep every version, not just the latest** | Native format (.xer, .mpp) + PDF |
| RFI log with all responses | Same as above | Digital |
| Submittal log with all reviews | Same as above | Digital |
| Change order documentation (PCO through executed CO) | Same as above | Digital |
| Correspondence (email, letters) | Same as above | Digital with metadata preserved |
| Photos (all, with date/time/location metadata) | Same as above | Original format, organized by date |
| Pay applications with all backup | Same as above | Digital |
| Contracts and amendments | Duration of project + statute of limitations + warranty period | Digital + hard copy |

### Tennessee-Specific Notes

- Tennessee statute of limitations for breach of written contract: **6 years** (T.C.A. § 28-3-109)
- Tennessee statute of repose for construction defects: **4 years** from substantial completion (T.C.A. § 28-3-202) — but can extend to **10 years** in certain circumstances
- **PM recommendation:** Retain all project records for a minimum of 10 years after substantial completion

## Practical Templates

### Delay Event Log

Maintain as a running log throughout construction. Every delay event gets an entry, regardless of magnitude.

```
| Event # | Date Identified | Description | Cause | Responsible Party | CP Impact? | Days Impact | Status | Resolution |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|------------------|-----------|-------------|--------|------------|
| D-001 | YYYY-MM-DD | [Specific description] | [Weather / Owner / GC / Sub / AHJ / Design] | [Party name] | Y/N | X days | Open / Resolved | [How resolved] |
```

### Time Extension Tracker

```
| TE # | Date Requested | Event Reference | Days Requested | PM Recommendation | Owner Decision | Days Approved | New Completion Date | CO # |
|------|---------------|----------------|---------------|-------------------|---------------|--------------|--------------------|----|
| TE-001 | YYYY-MM-DD | D-001 | X days | Approve / Partial / Deny | Approved | X days | YYYY-MM-DD | CO-XXX |
```

### Weather Day Tracker

```
| Date | AM Conditions | PM Conditions | Precip (in.) | Temp Range | Work Impact | Weather Day? | Notes |
|------|--------------|---------------|-------------|-----------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| YYYY-MM-DD | Clear, 65°F | T-storms 2-5pm | 0.8" | 62-78°F | Concrete pour postponed; interior work continued | Yes (partial) | 3 hrs lost on exterior |
```
