# Value Engineering Framework

> Cross-cutting reference for [[Construction PM Knowledge Base]]
> Related: [[03 - Design — SD, DD, CD]], [[05 - Procurement & Bidding]], [[07 - Construction Execution]], [[Financial Management & Billing]], [[Contract Types & Structures]]
> Pricing reference: [[TPL-Merchandising-and-VE]] (Tally KB — VE Swap Library with per-division unit costs)

---

## Purpose

Value engineering is the disciplined process of finding equivalent function at lower cost — not scope reduction, not quality cutting, not corner-cutting. This note gives Atlas the PM's strategic framework for when to initiate VE, how to structure the exercise, how to present options to clients without undermining design intent, and how to manage VE decisions through the project lifecycle. For specific VE swap pricing by CSI division, Atlas should reference the Tally KB's VE Swap Library in [[TPL-Merchandising-and-VE]].

---

## The VE Principle

**VE ≠ Scope Reduction.**

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Value Engineering** | Same function, lower cost. The building performs identically for the occupant. | TPO roof instead of built-up roof — same waterproofing performance, lower install cost |
| **Scope Reduction** | Removed function. The building does less. | Eliminating a restroom from the floor plan to save money |
| **Quality Reduction** | Inferior performance. The building does the same thing, but worse. | Using 60-mil TPO instead of 80-mil to save $1.50/SF — shorter lifespan, more risk |
| **Deferred Scope** | Postponed function. The building will eventually do it, but not at opening. | Deferring landscaping to post-occupancy — still in the budget, just later |

The PM's job is to keep VE discussions in the first category and call out when they drift into the other three. Clients and GCs will sometimes conflate these — the PM is the gatekeeper.

## When to Initiate VE

### The VE Timing Curve

| Phase | VE Potential | Redesign Cost | PM Recommendation |
|-------|-------------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Pre-design / Programming** | Highest | None | Best time for fundamental VE (building size, systems selection, structural approach) |
| **Schematic Design** | Very High | Low | Excellent — major systems decisions are still flexible |
| **Design Development** | High | Low-Medium | The sweet spot for most VE exercises. Design is defined enough to price, flexible enough to change. |
| **50% CDs** | Moderate | Medium-High | Some VE still possible but requires redesign. Cost of change is increasing. |
| **100% CDs / Permit Set** | Low | High | Limited to material substitutions and minor scope adjustments. Major VE requires permit revision. |
| **During Construction** | Very Low | Very High | Change orders. Cost of VE change often exceeds the savings. Only worth pursuing for large savings or budget emergencies. |

**PM Rule:** If the project budget is tight, initiate VE at DD — not after bids come in over budget. Reactive VE (after a budget bust) is more expensive, more stressful, and less effective than proactive VE during design.

### Triggers for VE Exercise

| Trigger | VE Approach |
|---------|-------------|
| **Estimate exceeds budget at any design milestone** | Formal VE exercise immediately — before advancing to next design phase |
| **Bids come in over GMP or budget** | Urgent VE + scope review. Must resolve before contract execution. |
| **Client requests cost reduction** | Proactive VE presentation with options and trade-offs |
| **Program standardization opportunity** | VE across the prototype — savings multiply across all sites |
| **Market conditions change** (material escalation, labor shortage) | Re-evaluate VE options that were previously rejected when costs were lower |
| **Design exceeds TIA allowance** | VE to bring hard costs within TIA cap |

## Structuring a VE Exercise

### Step 1: Establish the Target

Before brainstorming VE ideas, define the savings target and constraints:

- **Budget gap:** How much needs to be saved? ($X or X%)
- **Hard constraints:** What can't change? (Brand standards, code requirements, lease requirements, owner non-negotiables)
- **Soft constraints:** What the client prefers not to change but will consider if the savings are significant
- **Timeline:** When does the VE decision need to be made? (Tied to design schedule, procurement deadlines, or permit submission)

### Step 2: Generate Options

VE ideas should come from multiple sources — the PM shouldn't generate them alone:

| Source | What They Contribute |
|--------|---------------------|
| **GC / CMAR** | Means and methods alternatives; material substitutions; constructability improvements |
| **Architect** | Design simplification; material alternatives that maintain design intent; specification flexibility |
| **Engineers (MEP, structural)** | System alternatives (HVAC types, structural systems, electrical configurations); code optimization |
| **Estimator (Tally)** | Cost impact of each option; benchmark comparison against similar projects. See [[TPL-Merchandising-and-VE]] for division-by-division swap library. |
| **PM** | Schedule impact; TIA implications; client precedent from similar projects; rollout program considerations |

### Step 3: Evaluate and Present

Every VE option should be presented with the same information:

```
VE Item: [Description]
Division: [CSI division]
Gross Savings: [$X]
Redesign Cost: [$X] (if any)
Net Savings: [$X]
Schedule Impact: [+/- days or none]
Quality / Performance Impact: [None / Minor aesthetic / Reduced lifespan / etc.]
Code Compliance: [Compliant / Requires verification / Non-compliant]
Client Brand Impact: [None / Requires approval / Not permitted by brand standards]
Decision Deadline: [Date — tied to procurement or design schedule]
PM Recommendation: [Recommend / Acceptable / Caution / Do Not Recommend]
```

### Step 4: Client Decision Meeting

Present VE options as a prioritized menu, not a take-it-or-leave-it list:

**Tier 1: Recommend (no impact to quality or function)**
These are genuine VE items — same performance, lower cost. The PM recommends all of these.

**Tier 2: Acceptable (minor trade-offs)**
These have some aesthetic, lifespan, or convenience impact but the trade-off is reasonable for the savings.

**Tier 3: Available but Cautionary (significant trade-offs)**
These save money but compromise something meaningful. The PM presents them with clear warnings.

**Never present Tier 3 items without Tier 1 and 2.** If the PM jumps straight to "here's what we can cut," the client perceives panic, not strategy.

### Step 5: Execute and Track

Once VE decisions are made:

- Architect issues revised drawings or specifications (formal ASI or drawing revision)
- Estimator updates the budget to reflect confirmed savings
- PM tracks VE items on the VE Log (see below) through design completion, permitting, and construction
- If a VE item is reversed later (client changes their mind), it becomes a change order — treat it as one

## VE by Project Phase — What's on the Table

### Pre-Design / Programming

| Opportunity | Potential Savings | Complexity |
|------------|------------------|-----------|
| Reduce building footprint (program efficiency) | 5-15% of total | Low — but requires client buy-in on program |
| Single-story vs. multi-story (where site allows) | 10-20% of structural + vertical transport | Medium — site and zoning dependent |
| Prototype optimization (rollout programs) | Compounds across all sites | High up front; high long-term value |
| Delivery method selection (DB vs. CMAR vs. DBB) | 3-8% schedule-related savings | Low — decision framework in [[Project Delivery Methods]] |

### Schematic Design

| Opportunity | Potential Savings | Complexity |
|------------|------------------|-----------|
| Structural system selection (steel vs. wood vs. CMU) | 5-15% of structural cost | Medium — engineer evaluation required |
| HVAC system type (packaged vs. split vs. VRF) | $2-10/SF | Medium — affects ceiling space, electrical, controls |
| Exterior cladding material selection | $5-20/SF of wall area | Low-Medium — aesthetic impact |
| Roof system selection | $2-5/SF | Low |
| Simplify building geometry (fewer jogs, corners, transitions) | 3-10% of envelope + structural | Medium — architect involvement critical |

### Design Development (The Sweet Spot)

This is where the majority of VE exercises occur. The design is defined enough to price accurately, and changes don't require fundamental redesign. For specific per-division swap alternatives and pricing, see [[TPL-Merchandising-and-VE]].

**Highest-yield VE categories at DD:**

| Category | Typical Savings Range | Frequency |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Finishes (flooring, paint, ceiling, millwork) | $3-15/SF | Very common — almost every project |
| HVAC simplification | $2-8/SF | Common — especially on smaller projects |
| Electrical (fixture selection, controls) | $2-8/SF | Common |
| Millwork / casework | $50-300/LF | Common on restaurant and retail |
| Structural simplification | $3-10/SF (where applicable) | Less common — usually locked by DD |
| Exterior cladding downgrade | $5-15/SF of wall | Moderate — aesthetic sensitivity |

### Construction Documents (Limited)

By CDs, VE is limited to material substitutions that don't require redesign:

- Equivalent-performance material swaps (spec-compliant alternates)
- Fixture and equipment substitutions (same performance, lower-cost manufacturer)
- Simplification of details that don't affect performance (connection types, trim profiles)

**PM Warning:** Any VE change after the permit set requires a permit revision. In some jurisdictions, this means re-review fees and additional weeks of review time. The cost of the permit revision may negate the VE savings.

### During Construction (Emergency Only)

VE during construction is a change order, with all the associated costs:

- GC re-pricing
- Potential restocking charges on already-ordered materials
- Redesign fees
- Schedule disruption
- Potential permit revision

**Only pursue construction-phase VE when:**
- The savings are substantial (>$25K on a typical retail/restaurant project)
- The budget is in genuine distress
- The item hasn't been installed or ordered yet
- The permit impact is manageable

## VE for Rollout Programs

VE on a rollout program is exponentially more valuable because savings multiply across every site. A $5,000 VE item across a 30-store rollout is $150,000 in program savings.

### Program VE Strategy

| Approach | When | PM Action |
|----------|------|-----------|
| **Prototype VE** | At program start or prototype refresh | Run a comprehensive VE exercise on the prototype before it's deployed. Every dollar saved flows to every future site. |
| **Procurement consolidation** | Ongoing | Negotiate program-level pricing for high-volume items (light fixtures, HVAC units, flooring, millwork). Volume discounts of 5-15% are achievable. |
| **Specification standardization** | At program start | Reduce the number of approved materials/fixtures to what performs well. Fewer SKUs = better pricing, simpler procurement, fewer RFIs. |
| **Lessons learned VE** | After first 3-5 sites | Review completed sites for recurring cost overruns or unnecessary complexity. Turn findings into permanent prototype changes. |
| **Regional VE** | Per market | Some VE items are regional: insulation R-values vary by climate zone; structural requirements vary by seismic zone; labor rates affect the cost-benefit of prefab vs. field-built. |

### Program VE Governance

On rollout programs, VE decisions should be managed at the program level, not per-site:

- **Program-level VE:** Changes to the prototype that apply to all future sites. Requires client brand/design approval.
- **Site-level VE:** One-off changes for a specific site due to local conditions. Does not change the prototype.
- **PM Rule:** Never let a site-level VE drift into a permanent prototype change without going through program governance. One PM's "great idea" on Site A becomes a nightmare if it conflicts with brand standards or creates inconsistency across the portfolio.

## VE Log

Maintain a running VE log from the first budget conversation through construction close. This log is a living document and should be reviewed at every OAC meeting when budget is a topic.

```
| VE # | Division | Description | Gross Savings | Redesign Cost | Net Savings | Impact | Decision Deadline | Status | Decision |
|------|----------|-------------|--------------|--------------|-------------|--------|------------------|--------|----------|
| VE-001 | 09 | LVT → VCT in BOH areas | $8,500 | $0 | $8,500 | Aesthetic — BOH only | 2026-04-15 | Approved | Client accepted |
| VE-002 | 06 | Simplify millwork profiles | $12,000 | $1,500 | $10,500 | Minor aesthetic | 2026-04-15 | Approved | Client accepted |
| VE-003 | 23 | Eliminate BAS; use standalone stats | $15,000 | $0 | $15,000 | No remote monitoring | 2026-04-15 | Rejected | Client wants BAS |
| VE-004 | 07 | 60-mil TPO vs. 80-mil | $6,000 | $0 | $6,000 | Reduced roof life | 2026-04-15 | Rejected | PM did not recommend |
```

**Log Rules:**
- Every VE item gets logged, even if rejected — the record shows due diligence
- Include the decision and who made it
- Decision deadline is mandatory — tied to procurement or design schedule
- Net savings accounts for any redesign, re-permitting, or coordination cost
- Reference [[TPL-Merchandising-and-VE]] for specific unit cost benchmarks by division

## Presenting VE to Clients

### The Right Framing

**Good:** "We've identified $85,000 in potential savings across three categories. Here's a menu with our recommendations, ranked by impact to the project."

**Bad:** "We're over budget. Here's a list of things we can cut."

The difference is strategic vs. reactive. Even when VE is triggered by a budget problem, the PM should present it as a structured exercise with clear options and professional recommendations — not a panicked list of cuts.

### Client Psychology

- **Clients don't want to feel like they're getting less.** Frame VE as "same building, smarter choices." Show them what stays the same, not just what changes.
- **Give them control.** A tiered menu (Tier 1/2/3) lets the client choose their comfort level. They feel empowered, not cornered.
- **Quantify trade-offs.** "This saves $12K and reduces the millwork lifespan from 15 years to 12 years" is a decision. "This saves $12K" is incomplete.
- **Know their non-negotiables before the meeting.** If the client is a restaurant franchisee with brand standards, don't present a VE item that violates those standards. It wastes time and undermines credibility.
- **Don't oversell savings.** Present realistic numbers. If the VE savings require perfect execution to achieve, say so. Overpromising on VE and under-delivering is worse than a conservative estimate.

## VE and TIA Budgets

For tenant improvement projects funded by a TIA (see [[Tenant Improvement Allowances]]):

- VE may be necessary to keep hard costs within the TIA cap
- Landlord-required scope (base building upgrades, ADA compliance) typically cannot be VE'd — it's non-negotiable
- Tenant-elective scope (finishes above standard, upgraded fixtures) is the primary VE target
- If VE brings costs below the TIA, confirm whether the unused allowance is recoverable or forfeited (per lease terms)
- Document all VE decisions clearly — TIA disputes often hinge on what was included in the original scope vs. what was value-engineered out
