# Estimating & Takeoffs

> **Purpose:** The numbers side of building — how to quantify materials, estimate labor, calculate costs, and evaluate bids. Estimating is the bridge between a plan on paper and a budget in reality. For a GC, accurate estimating is the difference between profit and loss. For a PM evaluating a GC's bid, understanding estimating mechanics helps identify padded bids, missing scope, and unrealistic pricing. For a personal builder, it means knowing what a project will actually cost before committing.

---

## The Estimating Process

### Step 1: Takeoff

A takeoff is the physical measurement of every material needed for the project, derived from the plans. It's tedious, methodical work — and it's where accuracy starts or dies.

**Systematic approach:**
1. Work through the plans one system at a time (foundation, framing, roofing, siding, etc.)
2. Measure from the plans (or the actual site for renovation work)
3. Convert measurements to material quantities (linear feet of lumber, sheets of plywood, cubic yards of concrete, squares of roofing)
4. Add waste factors (materials damaged, mis-cut, or lost — typically 5-15% depending on the material)
5. Organize by material type and vendor for pricing

### Step 2: Pricing

Apply unit costs to the takeoff quantities:
- **Material costs** — Price from suppliers. Get actual quotes for large quantities; use price books or recent purchases for estimates.
- **Labor costs** — Estimated hours × labor rate (including burden — taxes, insurance, benefits). Labor is the hardest to estimate accurately.
- **Equipment costs** — Rental rates for anything not owned (crane, excavator, scaffolding, compressor)
- **Subcontractor costs** — For subbed trades, get bids based on the project plans. Compare at least 3 bids per trade.

### Step 3: Overhead and Profit

After direct costs (materials + labor + equipment + subs), add:
- **General conditions** — Site supervision, temporary utilities, dumpsters, portable toilets, permits, insurance, temporary fencing. Typically 8-15% of direct costs.
- **Overhead** — Office rent, vehicles, office staff, accounting, marketing, insurance beyond project-specific. Varies widely by company size.
- **Profit** — The GC's return. Typically 8-15% for residential, sometimes higher for complex or high-risk work.

## Common Takeoff Formulas

### Concrete

**Volume = Length × Width × Thickness, converted to cubic yards.**

1 cubic yard = 27 cubic feet.

| Application | Typical Dimensions | Formula |
|------------|-------------------|---------|
| Slab | Length × Width × Thickness | (L' × W' × T") ÷ 12 ÷ 27 = CY |
| Footing | Length × Width × Depth | Same formula |
| Wall | Perimeter × Height × Thickness | P' × H' × T' ÷ 27 = CY |

**Example:** 1,500 sq ft slab, 4" thick:
1,500 × (4÷12) ÷ 27 = 18.5 CY. Order 20 CY (always round up — you can't order a partial yard from a ready-mix truck, and running short mid-pour is catastrophic).

**Waste factor for concrete:** 5-10% depending on forming accuracy and site conditions.

### Framing Lumber

**Studs per wall:** (Wall length in feet ÷ stud spacing in feet) + 1. Then add for corners, intersections, trimmers, king studs, and cripples.

Quick rule of thumb: For 16" OC framing, figure 1 stud per linear foot of wall. This accounts for the base count plus extras for openings, corners, and intersections.

**Example:** 200 LF of exterior walls at 16" OC:
- Base count: 200 ÷ 1.33 + 1 ≈ 151 studs
- Rule of thumb: ~200 studs (accounts for extras)
- Order: 200 studs + 10% waste = 220 studs

**Plates:** 3 plates per wall (1 bottom, 2 top) × wall length. Remember plates come in standard lengths (8', 10', 12', 14', 16', 20'). Plan cuts to minimize waste.

**Headers:** Count every opening. Size per span tables. Typically doubled 2x material with 1/2" plywood spacer, or LVL.

### Sheathing (OSB/Plywood)

**Sheets = Area ÷ 32 sq ft per sheet** (for 4' × 8' panels).

Wall sheathing: Perimeter × wall height ÷ 32. Subtract for large openings (windows, doors), but don't subtract for small openings (the waste covers cut pieces around the openings).

Roof sheathing: Roof area (not footprint — account for slope). Roof area = footprint area × slope factor.

| Roof Pitch | Slope Factor |
|-----------|-------------|
| 4/12 | 1.054 |
| 5/12 | 1.083 |
| 6/12 | 1.118 |
| 7/12 | 1.158 |
| 8/12 | 1.202 |
| 9/12 | 1.250 |
| 10/12 | 1.302 |
| 12/12 | 1.414 |

**Example:** 1,500 sq ft footprint, 6/12 pitch:
1,500 × 1.118 = 1,677 sq ft roof area ÷ 32 = 52.4 sheets. Order 58 (10% waste on roof due to hips, valleys, and cuts).

### Roofing

Roofing is measured in **squares** (1 square = 100 sq ft).

Roof area ÷ 100 = number of squares. Shingles are sold in bundles — typically 3 bundles per square for standard 3-tab or architectural shingles.

**Example:** 1,677 sq ft roof area = 16.8 squares. Order 17 squares (51 bundles) + 1 extra square for starters, ridge cap, and waste = 54 bundles.

Don't forget: underlayment, drip edge, ice/water shield, ridge vent, flashing, and roofing nails are separate line items.

### Drywall

**Sheets = Wall and ceiling area ÷ 32 sq ft per sheet** (standard 4' × 8' sheets).

Quick residential rule of thumb: Finished square footage × 3.5 = approximate sq ft of drywall needed (covers walls and ceilings for typical 8' ceiling height rooms). Then ÷ 32 for number of sheets.

**Example:** 1,500 sq ft home: 1,500 × 3.5 = 5,250 sq ft ÷ 32 = 164 sheets. Add 10% waste = 180 sheets.

Don't forget tape, joint compound (approximately 1 box of compound per 100 sq ft), corner bead, and screws (about 1 lb per 100 sq ft).

### Insulation

**Batts:** Area to insulate ÷ coverage per package (varies by width and R-value — check the bag).

**Blown:** Area × desired depth × density = bags needed. Manufacturer provides coverage tables per bag at rated R-values.

**Spray foam:** Measured in board feet (1 board foot = 1 sq ft × 1" thick). Cost is typically quoted per board foot. For 3.5" of closed-cell in a 2x4 wall: area × 3.5 = board feet.

### Paint

**Coverage:** Standard interior latex covers 350-400 sq ft per gallon (one coat). Figure two coats on walls (and sometimes three for deep colors or color changes).

**Example:** 5,250 sq ft of wall and ceiling area × 2 coats ÷ 375 sq ft/gallon = 28 gallons. Plus primer: 5,250 ÷ 375 = 14 gallons. Plus trim paint: varies by LF of trim.

## Labor Estimating

Labor is the most variable and hardest-to-predict cost. General productivity rates for residential (experienced crew, moderate complexity):

| Activity | Unit | Productivity Range | Notes |
|----------|------|-------------------|-------|
| Framing (wood, residential) | SF of floor area | 3-6 SF per labor hour | Varies hugely by complexity |
| Roofing (asphalt shingles) | Square | 1-2 hours per square | Simple roof; complex roofs 3-4 hours |
| Drywall hanging | Sheet | 15-25 minutes per sheet | 8' ceilings, walls; add time for ceilings |
| Drywall taping/finishing | SF | 80-120 SF per hour | Level 4 finish |
| Interior painting | SF | 150-250 SF per hour | Walls, rolling; cut-in is slower |
| Trim carpentry (baseboard) | LF | 10-20 LF per hour | Paint-grade; stain-grade takes longer |
| Trim carpentry (crown) | LF | 5-12 LF per hour | Depends on complexity and ceiling height |
| Cabinet installation | Cabinet | 1-2 hours per cabinet | Uppers faster than bases; complexity varies |
| Tile (floor, standard) | SF | 30-60 SF per day | Large format faster; mosaic much slower |
| Hardwood flooring | SF | 60-100 SF per day | Nail-down; floating is faster |

**Labor burden:** The actual cost of labor is higher than the hourly wage. Add:
- FICA (employer share): 7.65%
- Workers' compensation insurance: 5-30% depending on trade (carpentry is typically 12-20%)
- General liability insurance: 2-5%
- Unemployment taxes: varies by state
- Benefits (if any): health insurance, PTO, retirement

**Rule of thumb:** Multiply the wage by 1.3-1.5 to get the fully burdened labor cost. A carpenter earning $25/hr costs the company $32-37/hr when burden is included.

## Markup vs. Margin — Know the Difference

These are not the same thing and confusing them is a common and costly mistake.

**Markup** = (Selling Price - Cost) ÷ Cost × 100

**Margin** = (Selling Price - Cost) ÷ Selling Price × 100

| Markup | Equivalent Margin | Selling Price (on $100 cost) |
|--------|------------------|------------------------------|
| 10% | 9.1% | $110 |
| 15% | 13.0% | $115 |
| 20% | 16.7% | $120 |
| 25% | 20.0% | $125 |
| 33% | 25.0% | $133 |
| 50% | 33.3% | $150 |

**Why it matters:** If your target is 20% profit margin but you apply a 20% markup, you're actually earning 16.7% margin. Over a $300,000 project, that's a $10,000 difference. Always clarify whether you're discussing markup or margin.

## Evaluating Bids (PM Perspective)

When reviewing a GC's bid or a sub's proposal, look for:

**Completeness:**
- Does the bid cover the full scope? Compare the bid items against the plans item by item.
- Are allowances clear? An "allowance" is a placeholder amount — the actual cost may be higher or lower. Common for fixtures, appliances, and finish materials that the owner hasn't selected yet.
- Are exclusions listed? What's NOT in the bid? Compare exclusions across bidders — one may exclude something another includes.

**Reasonableness:**
- Unit costs within market range? If one bidder's framing cost is 30% below the others, either they made an error, they plan to cut corners, or they're buying the job (and will make it up on change orders).
- Labor productivity assumptions reasonable? Unusually low labor hours means either a very efficient crew or an underestimate that will become a change order.
- Materials properly specified? Cheaper materials (builder-grade fixtures, lower insulation values, thinner concrete) reduce the bid but may not match the design intent.

**Risk allocation:**
- Who carries the risk of price escalation on materials?
- What's the contingency (if any)?
- What's the change order markup?
- Is there a retainage clause?

## Quick Estimating for Personal Projects

For personal home improvement projects, a simplified approach:

1. **List every material** you'll need — be specific (product, size, quantity)
2. **Price at your local supplier** — Home Depot/Lowe's for general, specialty lumber yard for quality/hardwood/trim
3. **Add 15% waste/miscellany** — fasteners, adhesive, sandpaper, blades, miscellaneous supplies always add up
4. **Double your time estimate** — however long you think it will take, double it. This is the single most accurate rule of thumb in DIY construction.
5. **Add tool cost** if you need to buy or rent tools for this specific project
