# Investment Strategy & Portfolio Theory

> Cross-cutting reference for [[Personal Finance Knowledge Base]]
> **Priority:** 🟡 Near-term — begin with employer match immediately; expand as debt is reduced and emergency fund is built.

---

## Core Investing Principles

Before any discussion of specific investments, these foundational truths should guide every decision:

### 1. Time in the Market Beats Timing the Market
The S&P 500 has returned roughly 10% annually (before inflation) over the long term, despite wars, recessions, pandemics, and crashes. Missing the 10 best days in a 20-year period can cut returns in half. The cost of sitting on the sidelines waiting for the "right time" almost always exceeds the cost of buying at a "bad time."

### 2. Compound Interest Is the Only Free Lunch
$10,000 invested at 8% annual return:
- After 10 years: ~$21,600
- After 20 years: ~$46,600
- After 30 years: ~$100,600

The money earned in years 20-30 ($54,000) is more than the total accumulated in the first 20 years. Starting early is the single most powerful advantage.

### 3. Costs Matter Enormously
A 1% difference in annual fees on a $100,000 portfolio over 30 years costs roughly $100,000+ in lost growth. This is why low-cost index funds are the default recommendation for nearly everyone.

### 4. Diversification Reduces Risk Without Eliminating Return
Owning many different assets — across sectors, geographies, and asset classes — smooths out the ride without significantly reducing long-term returns. The goal is to never have all your money dependent on one company, one sector, or one country.

### 5. Risk and Return Are Linked
Higher potential returns require accepting higher volatility. There is no high-return, low-risk investment. Anyone selling one is lying or confused.

## Asset Classes Explained

### Stocks (Equities)
**What they are:** Ownership shares in companies.
**Expected long-term return:** 7-10% annually (nominal).
**Risk:** High short-term volatility. Individual stocks can go to zero. Broad index funds have always recovered over long periods (historically).
**Role in portfolio:** Primary growth engine for long-term wealth building.

**Sub-categories:**
- **US Large-Cap:** S&P 500, Fortune 500 companies. The core of most portfolios.
- **US Mid/Small-Cap:** Smaller companies. Higher growth potential, higher volatility.
- **International Developed:** European, Japanese, Australian markets. Diversification beyond US.
- **Emerging Markets:** China, India, Brazil, etc. Higher growth potential, higher political/economic risk.

### Bonds (Fixed Income)
**What they are:** Loans to governments or corporations that pay regular interest.
**Expected long-term return:** 3-5% annually.
**Risk:** Low to moderate. Bond prices fall when interest rates rise. Government bonds (especially US Treasuries) are considered the safest assets.
**Role in portfolio:** Stability, income, and diversification against stock downturns.

**Sub-categories:**
- **US Treasury Bonds:** Backed by the US government. Safest category.
- **Corporate Bonds:** Higher yield than Treasuries, higher default risk.
- **Municipal Bonds:** Tax-exempt interest. Beneficial for high-tax-bracket investors.
- **I Bonds:** Inflation-protected savings bonds. Great for short-term inflation hedging.

### Real Estate
**What it is:** Property ownership or real estate investment trusts (REITs).
**Expected long-term return:** 5-8% annually (varies widely by market and type).
**Risk:** Illiquid (hard to sell quickly), market-dependent, management-intensive (for direct ownership).
**Role in portfolio:** Income generation, inflation hedge, diversification.
**For most beginners:** REIT index funds provide real estate exposure without being a landlord.

### Cash & Cash Equivalents
**What it is:** HYSA, money market funds, T-Bills, CDs.
**Expected return:** Tracks short-term interest rates (currently 4-5%, historically 1-3%).
**Risk:** Minimal principal risk. Main risk is inflation erosion.
**Role in portfolio:** Emergency fund, short-term goals, dry powder for opportunities.

### Alternative Assets
**What they include:** Commodities (gold, oil), cryptocurrency, private equity, collectibles.
**Expected return:** Highly variable and unpredictable.
**Risk:** High. Often illiquid. Many are speculative.
**Role in portfolio:** Small allocation (0-10%) for those who understand the risks. Not recommended as core holdings for someone in the accumulation phase.

## Portfolio Construction

### The Three-Fund Portfolio (The Gold Standard for Simplicity)
This is the most widely recommended portfolio for the majority of investors. Three funds cover the entire global market:

| Fund | Allocation | Example (Vanguard) | Expense Ratio |
|------|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| US Total Stock Market | 60% | VTSAX / VTI | 0.03% |
| International Total Stock Market | 30% | VTIAX / VXUS | 0.07% |
| US Total Bond Market | 10% | VBTLX / BND | 0.03% |

**Why it works:** Broad diversification, extremely low costs, captures global market returns, requires minimal maintenance.

### Asset Allocation by Age (Rule of Thumb)
The traditional guideline: **Stocks % = 110 minus your age.** So at age 30, roughly 80% stocks and 20% bonds. At 50, roughly 60/40.

This is a starting point, not a law. Factors that shift allocation:
- **Higher risk tolerance or longer timeline** → more stocks
- **Stable income (government job, tenure)** → can afford more stock risk
- **Variable income or approaching a major purchase** → more bonds/cash
- **Existing pension or Social Security coverage** → can afford more stock risk

### For Current Stage (Learning + Accumulation)
**Recommended starting allocation: 90/10 (stocks/bonds) or even 100% stocks** if retirement is 30+ years away and you can stomach volatility. Time heals all stock market wounds — but only if you don't sell during downturns.

## Account Types and Tax Treatment

Understanding WHERE to hold investments is as important as WHAT to invest in.

### Tax-Advantaged Accounts (Use First)

| Account | Tax Treatment | 2025-2026 Limits | Best For |
|---------|--------------|------------------|----------|
| **401(k) / 403(b)** | Traditional: tax-deferred. Roth: tax-free growth. | $23,500/year (2025) | Employer match + tax reduction |
| **Roth IRA** | Post-tax contributions, tax-free growth and withdrawals | $7,000/year (2025) | Long-term growth, flexibility |
| **Traditional IRA** | Tax-deductible contributions (income limits apply), tax-deferred growth | $7,000/year (2025) | Tax deduction if eligible |
| **HSA** | Pre-tax in, tax-free growth, tax-free out (for medical) | $4,300 individual / $8,550 family (2025) | Triple tax advantage — stealth retirement account |

### Tax-Location Strategy
Different investments generate different types of taxable events. Placing them in the right accounts matters:

- **Tax-advantaged accounts (401k, IRA, HSA):** Hold bonds, REITs, and actively managed funds here (these generate ordinary income that would be taxed at higher rates)
- **Roth accounts:** Hold highest-growth assets (US stocks, small-cap) — you want the biggest gains to be tax-free
- **Taxable brokerage:** Hold broad market index funds (tax-efficient due to low turnover), international funds (for foreign tax credit), and municipal bonds

### Taxable Brokerage Account
After all tax-advantaged space is used, invest in a regular brokerage account. Key considerations:
- Long-term capital gains (held >1 year) are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income
- Short-term gains (held <1 year) are taxed as ordinary income
- Tax-loss harvesting can offset gains (see [[Tax Planning & Optimization]])
- Favor tax-efficient index funds (low turnover = fewer taxable events)

## Investment Selection

### Index Funds vs. Actively Managed Funds
Over 15-year periods, roughly 90% of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index after fees. The evidence is overwhelming and consistent:

**Index funds win** because:
- Lower fees (0.03-0.10% vs. 0.50-1.50%+)
- Lower turnover (fewer taxable events)
- No manager risk (the fund tracks the index, period)
- Broad diversification built in

**When active management might make sense:**
- Tax-loss harvesting strategies in a taxable account
- Specific sector expertise you can verify
- Municipal bond funds where active selection adds value
- Never pay more than 0.50% ER for an actively managed fund

### ETFs vs. Mutual Funds
Both can be index funds. The choice between them is mostly administrative:

| Feature | ETF | Mutual Fund |
|---------|-----|-------------|
| Trading | Real-time during market hours | End-of-day NAV |
| Minimum Investment | Price of one share | Often $1,000-$3,000 (some have no minimum) |
| Tax Efficiency | Slightly more tax-efficient | Comparable for most index funds |
| Auto-Investing | Harder to automate exact dollar amounts | Easy to auto-invest exact dollar amounts |
| Fractional Shares | Available at most brokers now | Inherently supports partial shares |

**For automated investing in a 401k or IRA:** Mutual funds are easier (set dollar amounts).
**For a taxable brokerage:** ETFs are slightly more tax-efficient.

### Recommended Fund Providers
- **Vanguard:** Pioneer of index investing. Owned by fund shareholders. Lowest-cost leader.
- **Fidelity:** Zero-fee index funds (FZROX, FZILX). Excellent platform.
- **Schwab:** Competitive index funds. Great customer service.

All three are excellent. Pick whichever your 401k or brokerage is with. Don't overthink this.

## Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Invest a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals regardless of market price. This is the default strategy for anyone with a paycheck:

- 401k contributions every paycheck = DCA
- Monthly Roth IRA contribution = DCA
- Weekly transfer to brokerage = DCA

DCA removes the emotional decision of "is now a good time to invest?" The answer is always "if you have money to invest and a long time horizon, yes."

**Lump sum vs. DCA:** If you receive a windfall (bonus, inheritance, tax refund), mathematically, investing it all immediately outperforms DCA about 2/3 of the time. But if the emotional risk of investing a lump sum right before a downturn would cause panic selling, DCA the windfall over 3-6 months.

## Rebalancing

Over time, your portfolio drifts from your target allocation as different assets grow at different rates. Rebalancing means selling what's grown and buying what's lagged to return to your target.

**Frequency:** Annually or when allocation drifts more than 5% from target.
**Method:** Use new contributions to rebalance first (buy the underweight asset class) before selling.
**In tax-advantaged accounts:** Rebalance freely — no tax consequences.
**In taxable accounts:** Be mindful of capital gains when selling. Use new contributions instead.

## Common Investing Mistakes

- **Waiting to start until you "know enough."** Start with a target-date fund. Learn as you go.
- **Checking your portfolio daily.** More frequent checking correlates with worse performance due to panic selling.
- **Chasing past performance.** Last year's hot fund is often this year's laggard. Mean reversion is real.
- **Trying to time the market.** Professionals can't do this consistently. You can't either.
- **Panic selling during downturns.** Market drops are normal. A 10%+ drop happens roughly once a year. A 20%+ drop happens every 3-5 years. A 30%+ drop happens once a decade. If you sell, you lock in losses and miss the recovery.
- **Over-concentrating in employer stock.** Your income already depends on your employer. Don't let your savings depend on them too.
- **Neglecting tax-advantaged space.** Every dollar in a taxable account that could have been in a Roth IRA or 401k is leaving money on the table.

## The Target-Date Fund Option

If all of this feels overwhelming, the single best move is: **put everything into a target-date index fund** matching your expected retirement year (e.g., Vanguard Target Retirement 2060).

A target-date fund is a professionally managed mix of stocks and bonds that automatically becomes more conservative as you approach retirement. It handles asset allocation, diversification, and rebalancing for you. The expense ratio is slightly higher than individual index funds (0.10-0.15%) but the convenience is worth it for someone just starting.

You can always get more sophisticated later. Don't let perfect be the enemy of invested.
