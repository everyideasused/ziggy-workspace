# 💊 Vegan Micronutrient Mastery

> **Purpose:** Comprehensive reference on the critical micronutrients that require active management on a vegan diet. Covers deficiency risk, food sources, bioavailability factors, supplementation thresholds, and practical strategies for each nutrient.

---

## The Critical 7 — Vegan Nutrition Priority Stack

| Nutrient | Risk Level | Consequence of Deficiency | Primary Strategy |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| B12 | 🔴 Cannot get from plants | Irreversible nerve damage, anemia | Supplement always |
| Vitamin D3 | 🔴 Minimal food sources | Bone loss, immune dysfunction, mood | Supplement + sun |
| EPA/DHA (Omega-3) | 🔴 High conversion inefficiency | Brain fog, inflammation, CVD risk | Algae oil supplement |
| Iron | 🟡 Lower bioavailability form | Fatigue, anemia, poor performance | Food + absorption strategy |
| Zinc | 🟡 Phytate binding reduces uptake | Immune suppression, poor recovery | Food + soaking strategy |
| Iodine | 🟡 No land plants are reliable | Thyroid dysfunction, metabolism | Seaweed or iodized salt |
| Calcium | 🟡 No dairy | Bone density loss, muscle cramping | Food strategy + D3 synergy |

## 1. Vitamin B12 — Non-Negotiable

### Why Vegans Are at Universal Risk

B12 is produced exclusively by bacteria. Animal products contain it because animals consume bacteria or are given B12 injections. **No whole plant food reliably provides adequate B12.** Fermented foods, spirulina, and algae are NOT reliable sources — research consistently shows inadequate or inactive analogues.

### Deficiency Consequences
- **Early:** Fatigue, weakness, numbness/tingling in hands and feet
- **Late (irreversible):** Peripheral neuropathy, spinal cord degeneration, megaloblastic anemia, cognitive decline
- **Subclinical:** Elevated homocysteine → cardiovascular and neurological risk even before clinical symptoms

### Sources

| Source | B12 Content | Reliability |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Cyanocobalamin supplement | Dose-controlled | ✅ Best option |
| Methylcobalamin supplement | Dose-controlled | ✅ Excellent (active form) |
| Nutritional yeast (B12 fortified) | ~2.4mcg/2 tbsp | ✅ Good supplement to diet |
| Fortified plant milk | ~1.2mcg/cup | ✅ Good supplement to diet |
| Fortified breakfast cereals | Variable | ✅ Check label |
| Spirulina / algae | Pseudovitamin B12 | ❌ Inactive analogues — don't rely on |
| Fermented foods (tempeh, miso) | Trace | ❌ Unreliable |

### Supplementation Protocol
- **Daily:** 250–500mcg cyanocobalamin OR 1,000mcg methylcobalamin
- **Weekly (alternative):** 2,500mcg cyanocobalamin 2x/week
- **Test:** Serum B12 and MMA (methylmalonic acid) — MMA is more sensitive. Test annually.
- **Optimal range:** B12 >400 pg/mL, MMA <270 nmol/L

## 2. Vitamin D3 — Sun Vitamin

### The Vegan D Problem

D2 (ergocalciferol) is plant-derived but significantly less potent than D3 (cholecalciferol). Lichen-derived D3 supplements are vegan and preferred. Food sources are inadequate for most people.

### Deficiency Risk Factors
- Living above 35°N or 35°S latitude (Nashville = 36°N — borderline)
- Winter months (October–March in Nashville)
- Dark skin pigmentation (requires longer sun exposure)
- Spending most time indoors
- Sunscreen use blocking UVB synthesis

### Deficiency Consequences
- Bone loss (osteoporosis, fractures)
- Immune system dysregulation
- Muscle weakness and fatigue
- Mood disorders (depression, seasonal affective disorder)
- Impaired calcium absorption

### Sources and Supplementation

| Source | D3 Content | Notes |
|--------|------------|-------|
| Sun (UVB on skin) | Highly variable | 15–30 min midday, no sunscreen, full arm/leg exposure |
| Lichen-derived D3 (supplement) | 1,000–4,000 IU | **Best vegan option** |
| Fortified plant milk | 100–150 IU/cup | Supplementary only |
| Fortified OJ | ~137 IU/cup | Supplementary only |
| UV-exposed mushrooms | 400+ IU/100g | Gill-side up, 30 min midday sun |

### Protocol
- **Supplement:** 1,000–2,000 IU D3 (lichen-derived) daily in summer; 2,000–4,000 IU in winter
- **Test:** 25-hydroxyvitamin D — annually
- **Optimal range:** 40–60 ng/mL (100–150 nmol/L)
- **Critical:** Always take D3 with a fat-containing meal (fat-soluble vitamin)
- **Co-factor:** Vitamin K2 (MK-7) 100–200mcg daily helps direct calcium to bones (not arteries)

## 3. Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)

### The Conversion Problem

ALA (alpha-linolenic acid) is abundant in flax, chia, hemp, and walnuts. However:
- ALA → EPA conversion: 5–10% in healthy individuals
- ALA → DHA conversion: 2–5%
- Conversion is further impaired by high omega-6 intake (common in Western diets)
- Competition from LA (linoleic acid) for the same enzyme (delta-6 desaturase)

**Result:** Plant ALA sources cannot reliably provide adequate EPA and DHA.

### Functions of EPA and DHA

| Fatty Acid | Primary Functions |
|-----------|------------------|
| EPA | Anti-inflammatory signaling, cardiovascular protection, mood regulation, joint health |
| DHA | Brain structure (60% of brain fat is DHA), eye retina, cell membrane fluidity, cognition |

### Sources

| Source | EPA | DHA | Notes |
|--------|-----|-----|-------|
| Algae oil (supplement) | 100–300mg | 200–400mg | **Only reliable vegan source** |
| Walnuts | Trace ALA precursor only | Trace | Cannot substitute |
| Flaxseed | ALA only | None | Cannot substitute |
| Chia seeds | ALA only | None | Cannot substitute |
| Hemp seeds | ALA only + balanced ratio | None | Best plant omega source |

### Protocol
- **Supplement:** Algae-based EPA/DHA — 250–500mg DHA + EPA daily
- **Food support:** Include flax, chia, hemp, walnuts to optimize ALA → EPA conversion substrate
- **Avoid high omega-6 oils** — corn, soybean, sunflower reduce conversion efficiency
- **Test:** Omega-3 index (EPA+DHA as % of total red blood cell fatty acids) — target >8%

## 4. Iron — Bioavailability Challenge

### Non-Heme vs. Heme Iron

| Type | Found In | Bioavailability | Notes |
|------|----------|----------------|-------|
| Heme | Meat, fish | 15–35% | Regulated absorption |
| Non-heme | Plants, supplements | 2–20% | Highly variable |

Vegan iron absorption is lower but can be optimized. The body also **upregulates absorption** when stores are low, providing some self-regulation.

### Inhibitors of Iron Absorption

| Inhibitor | Found In | Strategy |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Phytates | Grains, legumes, seeds | Soak, sprout, or ferment |
| Polyphenols | Coffee, tea, red wine | Avoid 30 min before/after iron-rich meals |
| Calcium | Fortified foods, supplements | Separate by 2+ hours |
| Oxalates | Spinach, rhubarb, beet greens | Blanch or cook to reduce |

### Enhancers of Iron Absorption

| Enhancer | Mechanism | Practical Application |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| Vitamin C | Reduces Fe³⁺ to Fe²⁺ (more absorbable) | Squeeze lemon on lentil soup, eat bell pepper with iron foods |
| Alliums (garlic, onion) | Increases absorption | Cook iron foods with garlic/onion |
| Fermentation | Degrades phytates | Tempeh, sourdough, fermented veggies |
| Sprouting | Reduces phytate content | Sprouted lentils, sprouted grain bread |
| Cooking in cast iron | Leaches iron into food | Significant effect with acidic foods |

### Best Vegan Iron Sources

| Source | Iron per 100g (cooked) | Notes |
|--------|------------------------|-------|
| Pumpkin seeds | 8.8mg | Raw; snack or add to meals |
| Lentils | 3.3mg | Cook with vitamin C source |
| Tofu | 3.4mg | Varies by brand |
| Tempeh | 2.7mg | Fermented = better absorption |
| Chickpeas | 2.9mg | Soak and cook from dried |
| Soybeans/edamame | 3.5mg | Excellent source |
| Spinach | 2.7mg | High oxalates — cook to reduce |
| Quinoa | 1.5mg | Good with varied diet |
| Hemp seeds | 2.4mg | Raw — add to smoothies |
| Blackstrap molasses | 3.6mg/tbsp | Dense; add to oatmeal |

### Protocol
- **Food first:** Hit 14–18mg/day from diverse plant sources (men: 8mg RDA, but absorption loss means aim higher)
- **Vitamin C pairing:** Every iron-rich meal should include a vitamin C source
- **Test:** Serum ferritin, serum iron, TIBC, hemoglobin — annually. Ferritin <20 ng/mL = low.
- **Supplement only if deficient** — excess iron is pro-oxidative (unlike most other nutrients, more is not better)

## 5. Zinc — The Immune + Recovery Mineral

### Bioavailability Issues

Phytic acid (phytate) in grains, legumes, and seeds chelates zinc, reducing absorption to 15–26% from plant sources (vs. 35–40% from meat).

**Strategies to improve zinc bioavailability:**
1. **Soak legumes** overnight — degrades phytate by 20–50%
2. **Sprout grains and legumes** — reduces phytate 30–60%
3. **Ferment** — sourdough and tempeh have far better zinc bioavailability
4. **Use leavened bread** over flatbread — yeast fermentation degrades phytate
5. **Pair with sulfur-containing foods** — garlic, onion, alliums enhance absorption

### Best Vegan Zinc Sources

| Source | Zinc per 100g | Absorption Strategy |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| Pumpkin seeds | 7.8mg | Excellent — eat raw or lightly toasted |
| Hemp seeds | 9.9mg | Excellent — sprinkle on meals |
| Cashews | 5.6mg | Soak overnight |
| Sesame seeds/tahini | 4.6mg | Ferment or sprout sesame |
| Chickpeas | 1.5mg | Soak + cook from dried |
| Lentils | 1.3mg | Soak + cook |
| Tofu | 0.8mg | — |
| Oats | 4.0mg (raw) | Soak overnight |
| Quinoa | 1.1mg (cooked) | Good overall nutrient profile |

### Protocol
- **Target:** 11–15mg/day (RDA 11mg for men; vegans should target 15–20mg due to reduced bioavailability)
- **Test:** Serum zinc (fasted) — target 70–120 mcg/dL
- **Supplement:** 15–25mg zinc picolinate (most bioavailable form) if dietary intake is low
- **Caution:** >40mg/day supplemental zinc competes with copper — always pair zinc supplement with 1–2mg copper

## 6. Iodine — The Forgotten Mineral

### Why Vegan Diets Often Lack Iodine

- No seafood (primary conventional source)
- No dairy (iodine comes from iodine used in dairy sanitation — not a natural food source)
- Land plants have no reliable iodine content
- Only reliable vegan sources: **seaweed** and **iodized salt**

### Iodine Functions
- Thyroid hormone synthesis (T3, T4)
- Metabolic rate regulation
- Brain development
- Immune function

### Sources

| Source | Iodine Content | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| Nori (sushi seaweed) | 16–43mcg/sheet | Mild — good regular option |
| Dulse | ~1,500mcg/5g | Very high — limit to small amounts |
| Wakame | ~800mcg/5g | High — use sparingly |
| Kelp / kombu | 1,000–5,000+mcg/g | **Highly variable — supplementation risk** |
| Iodized salt | ~150–190mcg/tsp | Reliable, controllable |
| Fortified plant milk | ~30mcg/cup | Check label |

### Protocol
- **Strategy 1:** ¼ tsp iodized salt daily + varied diet
- **Strategy 2:** Nori 2–3 sheets/day (mild, controlled dose)
- **Supplement:** 150mcg potassium iodide if neither option is consistent
- **Avoid:** High-dose kelp supplements (extremely variable, risk of excess)
- **Target:** 150–300mcg/day (RDA 150mcg; UL 1,100mcg)
- **Test:** Urine iodine concentration — goal 100–200 mcg/L

## 7. Calcium — Beyond Dairy

### Calcium Without Dairy is Absolutely Achievable

The dairy industry's calcium narrative is misleading. Bioavailability varies enormously:

| Source | Calcium | Absorption % | Absorbed Calcium |
|--------|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Cow's milk | 300mg/cup | 32% | ~96mg |
| Fortified soy milk | 300mg/cup | 36% | ~108mg |
| Kale | 100mg/cup | 40% | ~40mg |
| Bok choy | 74mg/cup | 54% | ~40mg |
| Tofu (calcium-set) | 250mg/half block | 32% | ~80mg |
| Almonds | 75mg/oz | 21% | ~16mg |
| Broccoli | 43mg/cup | 53% | ~23mg |
| Fortified OJ | 350mg/cup | 36% | ~126mg |
| Spinach | 245mg/cup | **5%** | ~12mg (oxalate blocks) |

### Best Absorbed Vegan Calcium Sources

**Highest bioavailability (prioritize):**
- Low-oxalate greens: kale, bok choy, broccoli, collard greens, cabbage, watercress
- Fortified plant milks and OJ (check for calcium carbonate fortification)
- Calcium-set tofu (listed as "calcium sulfate" on ingredient label)
- Almonds, sesame seeds/tahini, chia seeds

**High calcium but low bioavailability (supplement, don't rely on):**
- Spinach, beet greens, Swiss chard (high oxalate)

### Bone Health Beyond Calcium

Calcium alone doesn't build bones. The full matrix:
- **Vitamin D3** — essential for calcium absorption in the gut
- **Vitamin K2 (MK-7)** — directs calcium to bones, not arteries
- **Magnesium** — regulates calcium transport; 1:1 to 2:1 calcium:magnesium ratio
- **Protein** — bone matrix is protein; adequate protein supports bone density
- **Exercise** — weight-bearing activity is the strongest bone density stimulus

### Protocol
- **Target:** 1,000mg/day from food
- **Food first:** 2–3 servings of fortified plant milk + calcium-set tofu + leafy greens
- **Supplement if needed:** Calcium citrate (better absorbed than carbonate, especially without food) — no more than 500mg at a time
- **Test:** DEXA scan every 2–3 years for athletes; serum calcium is not sensitive to deficiency

## Bonus: Often-Overlooked Micronutrients

### Vitamin K2 (MK-7)
- Found in: Natto (fermented soybeans — extremely high), some fermented foods
- Function: Activates osteocalcin and MGP — routes calcium to bones, prevents arterial calcification
- Vegan sources are limited outside natto; **supplement 100–200mcg MK-7 if D3 supplementing**

### Choline
- Found in: Tofu, soy milk, quinoa, broccoli, Brussels sprouts, kidney beans
- Function: Cell membrane integrity, liver function, neurotransmitter synthesis (acetylcholine)
- Target: 550mg/day; vegans often fall short
- Consider: Soy lecithin as a supplement or concentrated soy food intake

### Selenium
- Found in: Brazil nuts (1–2 nuts = RDA), sunflower seeds, mushrooms
- Function: Thyroid hormone metabolism, antioxidant defense (glutathione peroxidase)
- Easiest fix: **2 Brazil nuts daily = 200mcg = more than adequate**
- Do not supplement high-dose — toxic at >400mcg/day

### Magnesium
- Found in: Dark leafy greens, pumpkin seeds, dark chocolate, black beans, almonds, quinoa
- Function: 300+ enzymatic reactions, muscle function, sleep quality, insulin sensitivity
- Most vegans get adequate from diet; supplement magnesium glycinate 200–400mg at night if sleep is poor

## Annual Lab Panel for Vegans

| Lab Test | Target | Deficiency Flag |
|----------|--------|-----------------|
| B12 (serum) | >400 pg/mL | <200 = deficient |
| MMA (methylmalonic acid) | <270 nmol/L | Elevated = functional B12 deficiency |
| 25-OH Vitamin D | 40–60 ng/mL | <20 = deficient |
| Serum ferritin | 30–100 ng/mL | <20 = low |
| Serum zinc (fasted) | 70–120 mcg/dL | <70 = insufficient |
| Urine iodine | 100–200 mcg/L | <100 = insufficient |
| Omega-3 index | >8% | <4% = high risk |
| Homocysteine | <7 µmol/L | >10 = B12/folate concern |
| Complete blood count | Normal ranges | — |
