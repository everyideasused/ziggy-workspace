## Critical Facts & Governing Rules

### What Is ASE Master Automobile Technician?

ASE (Automotive Service Excellence) is the nationally recognized nonprofit certification body for the automotive service industry, active since 1972. Master Automobile Technician status is earned by passing all eight A-series certification exams: A1 through A8. Over 240,000 professionals hold ASE certification; roughly 58,000 hold Master Technician status.

**Critical clarification:** A9 (Light Vehicle Diesel Engines) is **NOT** required for Master Technician status. Passing A1–A8 earns the credential. A9 is a valuable supplemental certification, and this plan includes it, but the Master designation is awarded upon completion of A1–A8.

### Eligibility Requirements

ASE requires one of the following before you can sit for exams:

- **Path A:** 2 years of relevant hands-on work experience in automotive repair, OR
- **Path B:** 1 year of relevant hands-on experience + a 2-year degree or equivalent formal training program

**Your Path:** EET degree coursework combined with documented garage hours will satisfy Path B.

**Action:** Start logging every repair from April 2026 forward. By the time you sit for your first exam in Fall 2027, you will have 18 months of documented hands-on work, which — combined with your active EET enrollment — positions you well.

**Important:** ASE's experience verification is self-certified during registration. You attest that you meet the requirement. However, ASE reserves the right to audit. Keep your experience log meticulous and honest.

### Exam Format

All A-series exams are computer-based, multiple-choice, and administered at Prometric test centers. Each exam includes 10 additional unscored "research" questions mixed in with scored questions — you will not know which ones they are, so answer every question. Results are reported as pass/fail on a scaled scoring system, and you receive instant results at the test center.

### Certification Validity & Renewal

Each ASE certification is valid for 5 years. You must recertify before expiration. Recertification exams are shorter (approximately half the questions). ASE also offers a Renewal App ($52/year) that delivers one question per certification area per month — if you answer consistently, your certifications stay active without returning to a test center. Plan to batch your initial exams strategically so renewals align.

### Retake Policy

If you fail an exam, you must wait 30 full calendar days before purchasing and scheduling a retake. You pay the full exam fee again. This makes first-attempt passes critical from both a time and cost perspective.

## Garage Safety Protocols

You are working alone, without an instructor, on vehicles that weigh 4,000+ pounds and contain explosive pyrotechnic devices (airbags), pressurized fluids, and high-amperage electrical systems. Safety is not a suggestion — it's the prerequisite that makes everything else in this plan possible.

### General Garage Safety

**Never work under a vehicle supported only by a jack.** Floor jacks are lifting devices, not support devices. They can fail, leak, or tip. Always place rated jack stands under the frame or designated lift points before putting any part of your body under the vehicle. Use a minimum of 3-ton rated stands for your Jeeps.

**Wear safety glasses for every task.** Battery acid, brake fluid, coolant, rust particles, and spring-loaded clips can all reach your eyes. Make this automatic — glasses go on when you walk into the garage.

**Wear nitrile or mechanic's gloves.** Brake dust contains metals and formerly contained asbestos. Used motor oil is a known carcinogen with prolonged skin contact. Coolant (ethylene glycol) is toxic. Gloves protect you and keep your grip clean.

**Keep a fire extinguisher rated for Class B (flammable liquids) and Class C (electrical) within arm's reach.** A dry chemical ABC extinguisher ($25–40 at any hardware store) covers all garage scenarios. Mount it on the wall near your primary work area, not buried behind tools.

**Work in a ventilated space.** Running engines produce carbon monoxide — colorless, odorless, and lethal in an enclosed garage. Open the garage door fully or use an exhaust extraction hose whenever the engine is running. If you feel lightheaded, shut the engine off and get fresh air immediately.

**Tell someone what you're doing.** Working alone means nobody knows if something goes wrong. Text someone before a long wrench session. Keep your phone accessible (not under the vehicle with you).

### Electrical System Safety

**Disconnect the negative battery terminal before any electrical work.** This eliminates the risk of accidental short circuits, sparks near the battery (which can produce explosive hydrogen gas), and unintended activation of electrical components.

**SRS (Airbag) Safety — This Can Kill You.** The Supplemental Restraint System is the single most dangerous system you will encounter in DIY automotive work. Airbag inflators contain pyrotechnic charges that deploy with enough force to cause severe injury or death.

**Rules for working near SRS components:**

- **Disconnect the negative battery terminal and wait a minimum of 15 minutes** before working near any airbag component. The SRS system has a backup capacitor that retains enough energy to deploy an airbag even with the battery disconnected. The 15-minute wait allows this capacitor to fully discharge.
- **Never probe SRS wiring with a multimeter or test light.** SRS circuits operate on very low current — external test equipment can trigger deployment. SRS wiring is identified by yellow or yellow-striped outer sleeves. If you see yellow wiring, stop and research before touching it.
- **Never carry an airbag module with the deployment side facing toward your body.** If it accidentally deploys, the force should be directed away from you. Store modules with the deployment side (trim cover) facing up.
- **Areas where SRS components live:** Steering wheel (driver airbag and clockspring), dashboard (passenger airbag), A-pillars and roofline (curtain airbags), front seats (side airbags and seat belt pretensioners), center console (knee airbags on some vehicles). Be aware of these zones when removing interior trim, seats, or steering components.
- **When in doubt, don't touch it.** SRS repair and replacement is one area where taking the vehicle to a professional is the smart call, not the lazy one.

**Battery safety.** Car batteries produce hydrogen gas during charging, which is explosive. Never smoke, create sparks, or use open flames near a battery. When disconnecting, remove the negative terminal first to prevent accidental grounding. When reconnecting, attach positive first, then negative.

**High-amperage awareness.** A car battery can deliver hundreds of amps — enough to weld a wrench to a terminal, melt wiring, and start a fire. Remove rings, watches, and metal bracelets before working around batteries and the starter circuit. Never lay tools across battery terminals.

### Chemical Handling

**Brake fluid** destroys paint on contact and irritates skin. Wipe spills immediately with a clean rag. Wash skin thoroughly after contact.

**Coolant (ethylene glycol)** is sweet-tasting and lethal to animals and children. Clean up all spills. Dispose of used coolant properly — most auto parts stores accept it for free recycling.

**Used motor oil and transmission fluid** are hazardous waste. Never pour them down a drain or into the ground. Collect in a sealed container and bring to AutoZone, O'Reilly, or your county hazardous waste facility for free disposal.

**Refrigerant (AC systems)** is regulated by the EPA under Section 608 of the Clean Air Act. You are legally required to hold EPA 608 certification before handling refrigerant. Intentional venting of refrigerant into the atmosphere is a federal offense with fines up to $44,539 per day per violation. Do not attempt AC system work until you hold your 608 certification (scheduled for Spring 2027 in this plan).

### Lifting and Support Points

Consult your Haynes manual for the designated jack and jack stand placement points on each of your vehicles. Using the wrong lift point can crush undercarriage components, bend frame rails, or cause the vehicle to slide off the jack. The Wrangler and Grand Cherokee have different lift point locations — learn both.

## Your Vehicle Fleet — The Practice Lab

### 2018 Jeep Wrangler (JK or JL)

This is your primary hands-on vehicle. The Wrangler's 4WD system (transfer case, front/rear differentials, driveshafts) makes it the perfect study platform for A3 (Manual Drivetrain & Axles). Its relatively simple electrical architecture is approachable for A6 work. Solid axle front suspension gives excellent access to ball joints, tie rods, and steering components for A4 study. If equipped with a manual transmission, it becomes even more valuable for A3 clutch and transmission work.

**Applicable ASE areas:** A1, A3, A4, A5, A6, A8 (primary), A7 (supplemental)

### 2020 Jeep Grand Cherokee

The Grand Cherokee adds complexity with its independent front suspension, automatic transmission, and more advanced body electrical systems (power seats, advanced HVAC controls, larger BCM integration). This is your primary vehicle for A2 (Automatic Transmission) study and your secondary platform for A4, A5, and A6. If this is the diesel (3.0L EcoDiesel), it becomes your A9 study platform as well.

**Applicable ASE areas:** A1, A2, A4, A5, A6, A7, A8 (primary), A9 (if diesel)

### Honda Grom

The Grom is a quick-turn practice vehicle — oil changes take 15 minutes, valve adjustments teach engine internals fundamentals, and chain maintenance builds mechanical feel. While ASE certification is for automobiles, the Grom provides low-stakes repetitions that build confidence and hand skill.

**Applicable practice areas:** Oil changes (A1), valve clearance (A1), electrical diagnostics (A6), brake pad replacement (A5), spark plug service (A8)
