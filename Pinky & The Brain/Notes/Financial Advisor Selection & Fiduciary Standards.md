# Financial Advisor Selection & Fiduciary Standards

> Cross-cutting reference for [[Personal Finance Knowledge Base]]
> **When to use:** If/when you decide to work with a human financial advisor.

---

## The Fiduciary Standard vs. Suitability Standard

This is the most important distinction in the financial advisory industry:

**Fiduciary standard:** The advisor is legally obligated to act in your best interest. They must disclose all conflicts of interest and cannot recommend products that benefit them more than you.

**Suitability standard:** The advisor only needs to recommend products that are "suitable" for your situation — even if a cheaper, better option exists that pays them less commission. This is the standard many broker-dealers and insurance agents operate under.

**Always work with a fiduciary.** Ask directly: "Are you a fiduciary at all times, with all recommendations?" If they hesitate or qualify the answer, move on.

## Credential Hierarchy

Not all financial professionals are equal. Here's what the major credentials actually mean:

### Top Tier (Comprehensive Planning)

| Credential | Full Name | What It Means | Fiduciary? |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|------------|
| **CFP** | Certified Financial Planner | Gold standard for comprehensive financial planning. Requires education, 6,000 hours of experience, rigorous exam, and ongoing ethics requirements. | Yes (when providing financial planning) |
| **CFA** | Chartered Financial Analyst | Deep investment analysis expertise. Three grueling exams over 2-4 years. Primarily investment-focused. | Ethical duty (not always legal fiduciary) |
| **CPA/PFS** | CPA + Personal Financial Specialist | CPA with financial planning overlay. Strong on tax + planning integration. | Depends on context |

### Specialized Credentials

| Credential | Focus Area |
|-----------|-----------|
| **ChFC** | Advanced financial planning (similar scope to CFP, different pathway) |
| **CLU** | Life insurance and estate planning |
| **RICP** | Retirement income planning |
| **EA** | Enrolled Agent — IRS-authorized tax specialist |
| **CDFA** | Certified Divorce Financial Analyst |

### Red Flag Titles (Not Necessarily Bad, But Verify)
- "Financial Advisor" — this title is unregulated. Anyone can use it.
- "Wealth Manager" — same. No specific credential required.
- "Financial Consultant" — same.
- Insurance agent with a "Financial Planner" business card — may be selling products, not planning.

**Bottom line:** Look for CFP, CFA, or CPA/PFS as a baseline. The credential alone doesn't guarantee quality, but it guarantees a minimum standard of education, ethics, and accountability.

## Fee Structures

How an advisor gets paid determines their incentives. This is not cynicism — it's economics.

### Fee-Only (Recommended)
The advisor is paid directly by you. No commissions from product sales.

| Fee Model | Typical Range | Best For |
|-----------|--------------|----------|
| Hourly | $150-400/hour | One-time questions, specific planning needs |
| Flat fee (per plan) | $1,000-5,000/year | Comprehensive planning, annual review |
| AUM (% of assets under management) | 0.5-1.0% of managed assets | Ongoing management + planning |
| Retainer | $100-300/month | Ongoing access, regular planning |

**AUM caution:** 1% doesn't sound like much, but on a $500,000 portfolio, that's $5,000/year — every year — regardless of performance. Over 30 years, this compounds to significant drag. If using AUM, ensure the advisor provides comprehensive planning (tax, estate, insurance, retirement), not just investment management. If they're only managing investments, a robo-advisor at 0.25% does the same thing.

### Fee-Based (Proceed with Caution)
The advisor charges fees AND earns commissions on some products. This creates conflicts of interest. Not necessarily bad, but requires more scrutiny. Ask for full disclosure of all compensation.

### Commission-Only (Avoid for Planning)
The advisor is paid by the companies whose products they sell. Their incentive is to sell you products — annuities, whole life insurance, loaded mutual funds — not to give you the best advice.

## How to Find a Good Advisor

### Directories
- **NAPFA (National Association of Personal Financial Advisors):** napfa.org — fee-only fiduciary advisors only
- **Garrett Planning Network:** garrettplanningnetwork.com — hourly, as-needed fee-only advisors
- **XY Planning Network:** xyplanningnetwork.com — fee-only advisors specializing in Gen X/Y clients
- **CFP Board:** letsmakeaplan.org — searchable directory of all CFP professionals
- **FINRA BrokerCheck:** brokercheck.finra.org — check any advisor's disciplinary history

### Questions to Ask Before Hiring

1. Are you a fiduciary at all times?
2. How are you compensated? (Get this in writing)
3. What credentials do you hold?
4. What is your investment philosophy?
5. How often will we meet?
6. What's your typical client profile? (You want someone who works with people in your situation)
7. Can I see a sample financial plan?
8. Who has access to my accounts? What are the custody arrangements?
9. Have you ever had disciplinary actions or client complaints? (Verify on BrokerCheck)
10. What happens if you leave the firm or retire?

## When to Hire an Advisor vs. DIY

### DIY Is Probably Fine If:
- You're in the accumulation phase with straightforward finances
- You're willing to spend 2-5 hours/month learning and managing
- Your tax situation is simple (W-2, standard deduction)
- You can follow a simple investment plan without emotional interference
- This vault + knowledge base provides the framework you need

### Consider an Advisor If:
- Your financial situation becomes complex (multiple income sources, stock options, business ownership)
- You experience a major life event (inheritance, divorce, death of spouse, disability)
- You need specialized tax planning (Roth conversions, estate planning, business entity structure)
- You can't or don't want to manage it yourself and would otherwise do nothing
- You need accountability and behavioral coaching during volatile markets
- Your net worth crosses $500k+ and the stakes of mistakes increase

### The Hybrid Approach
Use an hourly or flat-fee advisor for periodic check-ups and specific questions, but manage your own investments in low-cost index funds. This gives you professional oversight without paying ongoing AUM fees.
