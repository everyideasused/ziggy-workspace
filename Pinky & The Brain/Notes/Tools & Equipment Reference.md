# Tools & Equipment Reference

> **Purpose:** What tools do what, when to use them, and whether to buy or rent. A carpenter's tools are the interface between knowledge and execution — you can understand framing perfectly but without the right saw, level, and fastener system, theory stays on paper. This note covers the essential tool categories for residential construction and home improvement, organized by function.

---

## The Buy vs. Rent Decision

**Buy when:**
- You'll use it on multiple projects (or regularly for maintenance)
- The tool is specific to your trade or a recurring task
- Rental cost over 3-4 uses exceeds purchase price
- Quality matters for the work you do (a good miter saw pays for itself in clean cuts)

**Rent when:**
- One-time or rare use (concrete saw, skid steer, scaffolding, trencher)
- The tool is large, expensive, and requires maintenance you don't want to handle
- You need a specialty tool for one phase of one project
- You want to test a tool type before investing (rent a track saw before buying one)

## Measuring & Layout Tools

| Tool | What It Does | When You Need It |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Tape measure (25' or 35')** | Measures everything. The most-used tool on any job. | Every task. Buy a quality one — the blade should be stiff for 7'+ standout. |
| **Framing square (steel, 24" × 16")** | Lays out rafter cuts, stair stringers, checks square. The face has rafter tables printed on it. | Framing: rafter layout, stringer layout, checking corners. |
| **Speed square (7" or 12")** | Quick angle marking, 90° and 45° cuts, rafter angle guide, saw guide for crosscuts. | Every day on a framing job. Indispensable. |
| **Combination square** | Marks 90° and 45°, checks depth, scribes reveals for trim. | Finish carpentry: marking reveal lines, checking depth of dados. |
| **Chalk line** | Snaps straight lines on long surfaces (floor layout, sheathing lines, siding courses). | Framing layout, sheathing, siding, tile layout. |
| **4' level** | Checks plumb and level on walls, posts, door jambs. | Framing: wall plumb. Finish: door/window installation, cabinet leveling. |
| **Laser level (self-leveling cross-line)** | Projects level and plumb lines across a room. Replaces a 4' level for many tasks and works at any distance. | Cabinet installation, tile layout, framing layout, suspended ceilings. Extremely useful — borderline essential for finish work. |
| **Plumb bob** | Establishes a true vertical line. Old-school but perfectly accurate. | Foundation-to-framing alignment, layout transfer between floors. |
| **Stud finder** | Locates framing behind drywall. | Trim installation, hanging heavy items, cabinet installation. Electronic models work for most situations. |
| **Moisture meter** | Measures moisture content of wood and building materials. | Checking lumber MC before installation, diagnosing moisture problems, verifying drying after leaks. |

## Cutting Tools

### Saws

| Saw | What It Does | Best For |
|-----|-------------|----------|
| **Circular saw (7-1/4" sidewinder)** | General-purpose crosscuts and rip cuts. The workhorse. | Framing, sheathing, rough carpentry. Every builder needs one. |
| **Miter saw (10" or 12" compound sliding)** | Precise crosscuts and miter cuts. The king of trim work. | Trim carpentry, baseboard, crown, casing, framing cut lists. A 12" sliding compound miter handles everything up to 2x12 crosscuts and wide crown. |
| **Table saw** | Rip cuts (cutting along the length of boards). Precise, repeatable, fast. | Ripping plywood, making consistent narrow strips, dados with a dado blade, angled rips. |
| **Track saw (plunge-cut circular saw with guide rail)** | Straight, precise cuts in sheet goods without a table saw. Replaces a table saw for many operations. | Breaking down plywood and sheet goods on site. Cleaner cuts than a circular saw with a guide. |
| **Reciprocating saw (Sawzall)** | Demolition, rough cuts in place, cutting through nails and mixed materials. | Demo work, cutting pipe/conduit in walls, pruning (with the right blade), rough openings in existing walls. |
| **Jigsaw** | Curved cuts, cutouts, intricate shapes. | Countertop cutouts (sink), curved trim, electrical box cutouts in paneling, template cutting. |
| **Oscillating multi-tool** | Detail cuts in tight spaces, flush cuts, scraping, sanding small areas. | Undercutting door casing for flooring, removing grout, flush-cutting nails, trimming shims. Surprisingly versatile. |
| **Coping saw** | Back-cuts the profile of moldings for coped inside corners. | Trim carpentry: every inside corner of baseboard, crown, and chair rail. |

### Which Saws to Buy First

For a builder starting a tool collection, priority order:

1. **Circular saw** — does 80% of rough cutting
2. **Miter saw** (12" sliding compound) — transforms trim accuracy and speed
3. **Reciprocating saw** — essential for demo and remodeling
4. **Oscillating multi-tool** — fills gaps no other tool handles
5. **Jigsaw** — curved cuts and cutouts
6. **Table saw or track saw** — for when you're ripping regularly

## Fastening Tools

| Tool | What It Does | Best For |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Framing nailer (21° or 30° pneumatic or cordless)** | Drives 16d and 10d nails into framing. | Framing: plates, studs, sheathing, subfloor. Massive time savings vs. hand-nailing. |
| **Finish nailer (15-gauge or 16-gauge)** | Drives finish nails for trim. 15-gauge for structural trim connections; 16-gauge for general trim. | Baseboard, casing, crown, stair parts, cabinet trim. |
| **Brad nailer (18-gauge)** | Drives thin brads for lightweight trim and holding pieces while glue dries. | Small moldings, shoe molding, holding mitered joints, craft/furniture work. |
| **Pin nailer (23-gauge)** | Nearly invisible headless pins. Holds miter joints without filling. | Miter joints in trim (pin + glue), delicate moldings, pre-finished materials. |
| **Stapler (narrow crown)** | Fastens housewrap, vapor barrier, insulation facing, carpet pad. | Underlayment, building paper, insulation. |
| **Impact driver** | Drives screws with high torque. Replaced the drill for most screw-driving tasks. | Every screw on the job: deck screws, structural screws, cabinet screws, drywall screws. Faster and more controlled than a drill. |
| **Drill/driver** | Drills holes and drives screws (lower torque than impact). | Drilling holes for bolts, piloting screw holes, driving screws in delicate materials where impact would overdrive. |
| **Hammer drill** | Drills into concrete and masonry using percussion action. | Tapcon screws, concrete anchors, masonry drilling. A standard drill won't cut it — masonry requires hammer action. |

### Cordless vs. Pneumatic

**Cordless (battery)** is dominant for most tools now. Modern brushless 18V/20V platforms match pneumatic performance for nailers, drills, and impacts. Advantages: no compressor, no hose, more portable. Disadvantage: battery cost, battery life on large projects.

**Pneumatic** still wins for high-volume framing (all-day nailing on a framing crew), roofing, and when you need the lightest possible tool weight. A compressor and hose setup powers multiple nailers simultaneously.

**Recommendation for a personal builder:** Invest in one battery platform (Milwaukee, DeWalt, or Makita are the top three — pick one and stay with it for battery compatibility). Start with impact driver + drill/driver combo kit. Add nailers as projects require them.

## Hand Tools — The Essentials

No matter how many power tools you own, these hand tools are irreplaceable:

| Tool | Purpose | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| **Framing hammer (20-22 oz)** | Driving nails by hand, adjusting framing members, general persuasion | Smooth face for finish, milled face for framing (grips nail head) |
| **Finish hammer (14-16 oz)** | Finish nailing, setting nails below surface with nail set | Lighter, won't dent trim |
| **Nail set** | Drives finish nail heads below the surface for filling | Set of 3 (1/32", 2/32", 3/32") covers all finish nail sizes |
| **Utility knife** | Cuts everything: insulation, sheathing tape, caulk tubes, shims, packaging | Retractable with spare blades. Most-used tool on the belt after the tape measure. |
| **Flat bar / pry bar** | Prying, pulling nails, minor demo, adjusting framed walls | Keep both a flat bar (thin, for trim removal without damage) and a larger pry bar (for framing adjustment) |
| **Cat's paw** | Extracting nails driven flush or below surface | Digs under the nail head. Essential for corrections and demo. |
| **Chisels (set of 4: 1/4", 1/2", 3/4", 1")** | Mortising for hinges and strike plates, cleaning up joints, trimming wood | Keep them sharp — a dull chisel is dangerous (requires more force, more likely to slip) |
| **Block plane** | Shaves thin amounts from edges — trimming doors, easing edges, fitting joints | The most satisfying hand tool. Adjustable blade depth. Essential for hanging doors. |
| **Handsaw (Japanese pull saw)** | Cuts in tight spaces where no power saw fits | Flush-cutting, trimming shims, detail cuts. Pull saws cut on the pull stroke — thinner kerf, more control. |
| **Clamps (bar clamps, spring clamps, pipe clamps)** | Holding pieces in position during assembly, glue-ups, alignment | You can never have enough clamps. Start with 4 bar clamps (24" and 36") and a handful of spring clamps. |

## Safety Equipment

Non-negotiable for every task:

| Equipment | When | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| **Safety glasses / goggles** | Every cutting, drilling, nailing, demolition, and overhead task | Impact-rated (ANSI Z87.1). Prescription safety glasses exist — use them. |
| **Hearing protection** | Any power tool use, especially circular saws, routers, miter saws, impact drivers | Foam plugs (NRR 32) for sustained noise. Electronic muffs for intermittent (hear conversations between cuts). |
| **Dust mask / respirator** | Cutting MDF, sanding, spray foam, insulation, concrete cutting, painting | N95 minimum for dust. Half-face respirator with appropriate cartridges for chemical exposure (paint, adhesive, spray foam). |
| **Work gloves** | Material handling, rough framing, concrete, demolition | NOT while operating rotating tools (saws, drills) — gloves can catch and pull your hand into the blade. |
| **Hard hat** | Any overhead hazard, multi-story construction, when working below others | Required on commercial sites. Smart on residential when framing upper floors or working in excavations. |
| **Steel/composite-toe boots** | Any construction site | Minimum 6" height for ankle support. Electrical hazard (EH) rated. |
| **Fall protection** | Working at heights 6' or more above lower level (OSHA residential standard) | See [[Construction Safety & OSHA Residential]] for full requirements |

## Tool Maintenance Basics

**Saw blades:** Replace carbide-tipped blades when cuts start burning the wood or requiring excessive force. Clean pitch buildup with blade cleaner (Simple Green works). A sharp blade is safer — it requires less force and produces less kickback risk.

**Batteries:** Store lithium-ion batteries at room temperature, partially charged (40-60%) for long-term storage. Don't leave them on the charger indefinitely. Cold weather reduces capacity temporarily but doesn't damage the cells.

**Levels:** Check accuracy periodically. Place the level on a flat surface, note the bubble position, then flip the level end-for-end. The bubble should read the same. If not, the level is out of calibration.

**Air tools:** Oil the fittings per manufacturer instructions. Drain the compressor tank after each use to prevent internal corrosion. A water separator on the air line protects nailers from moisture.
