## SECTION A — KETTLEBELL EXERCISES

### A1. KB Swing (Two-Hand)
**Primary**: Glute max, hamstrings  
**Secondary**: Erector spinae, lats, core, forearms  
**Equipment**: Kettlebell (start moderate; 35–44lb range)

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Hinge, don't squat" — the movement is a hip hinge, not a knee-dominant squat
- "Hike the bell back like a football snap"
- "Drive hips forward explosively — like slamming a door with your hips"
- "Squeeze glutes hard at the top — stand tall, don't lean back"
- "Let the bell float to chest height from hip power alone — arms are just a connection"
- "Brace core before each rep"

**Common Errors**:
- Squatting down instead of hinging back (quads dominate instead of glutes)
- Hyperextending the lower back at top (lean back instead of standing tall with squeezed glutes)
- Rounding the upper back on the backswing (maintain thoracic extension)
- Arms bending and pulling the bell (power comes from hips only)

**Rep Ranges**: 10–20 reps for conditioning; 5–10 heavy for strength/power  
**Nathan Note**: The KB swing is the #1 glute builder in Nathan's arsenal. It trains the glute max through explosive hip extension — the exact movement pattern that builds size and power. Prioritize this movement in every lower-body session.

---

### A2. KB Swing (Single-Arm / One-Hand)
**Primary**: Glute max, hamstrings, anti-rotation core  
**Secondary**: Rear delt, lats, obliques  

**Additional Cues vs. Two-Hand**:
- "Resist rotation — the opposite shoulder wants to twist back; fight it"
- "Think about screwing your foot into the ground for hip stability"

**Nathan Note**: One-arm swings add a rotational demand that activates the obliques and rear delt as stabilizers. Programs glute med as a hip stabilizer. Excellent progression from two-hand swings.

---

### A3. KB Romanian Deadlift (RDL)
**Primary**: Hamstrings, glute max  
**Secondary**: Erector spinae, upper back (isometric), core  
**Equipment**: Kettlebell(s) — can hold one or two

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Soft bend in the knees — this is not a squat"
- "Push your hips back to the wall behind you"
- "Maintain a proud chest, spine neutral — imagine a broomstick along your back"
- "Feel a deep stretch in the hamstrings at the bottom"
- "Drive through the heels on the way up"
- "Squeeze glutes at the top — do not hyperextend"

**Common Errors**:
- Rounding the lower back (reduces hamstring load, risks injury)
- Squatting down instead of hinging
- Not achieving full hip extension at the top

**Progressions**: Single-leg RDL → B-stance RDL → Single-leg with KB  
**Regressions**: Bodyweight hip hinge drill with wall contact

**Rep Range**: 8–15 reps  
**Nathan Note**: The RDL trains the hamstrings through a long muscle length — the most effective position for hypertrophy. Pair with KB swings for complete posterior chain development.

---

### A4. Single-Leg KB Deadlift
**Primary**: Hamstrings, glute max, glute med (standing leg)  
**Secondary**: Core (anti-rotation), erector spinae, ankle stabilizers  

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Hinge at the hip of the standing leg; trail leg extends behind like a counterbalance"
- "Think of your body as a see-saw — hips are the fulcrum"
- "Keep hips square — don't let the trail hip open up"
- "Soft gaze 3–4 feet in front for balance"

**Common Errors**:
- Rotating the hip of the working leg outward
- Not hinging deeply enough (turning it into a squat)
- Losing balance — slow the tempo, reduce load

**Rep Range**: 6–10 reps per side  
**Nathan Note**: Outstanding glute med activator and the best single-leg movement for Nathan given KB-only equipment. Builds functional hip stability essential for running and overall lower body performance.

---

### A5. KB Goblet Squat
**Primary**: Quads, glute max  
**Secondary**: Core, upper back (isometric), adductors  
**Equipment**: Single KB (held vertically at chest)

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Elbows between knees at the bottom — use them to push knees out"
- "Chest stays tall throughout — don't round forward"
- "Heels stay on the floor — if they rise, work on ankle mobility"
- "Squat deep — below parallel if possible"
- "Drive through the whole foot on the way up"

**Common Errors**:
- Heels rising (ankle mobility limitation)
- Forward lean (thoracic mobility limitation)
- Knees caving inward (glute med weakness — cue to push knees out)

**Progressions**: KB front rack squat → Pause goblet squat → KB pistol squat progression  
**Regressions**: Box squat (to a controlled depth)

**Rep Range**: 10–20 reps  
**Nathan Note**: The goblet squat is a corrective tool as much as a strength exercise. The counterbalance allows deeper, more upright squatting than most bodyweight squats. Excellent for quad and glute development.

---

### A6. KB Single-Arm Row
**Primary**: Lats, rhomboids, mid-trap  
**Secondary**: Rear delt, biceps, erector spinae (ipsilateral)  

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Drive the elbow up and back — not just 'pull up'"
- "Think about putting your elbow in your back pocket"
- "At the top, hold 1 second — feel the lat squeeze"
- "Keep the non-working shoulder from dipping"
- "Brace the core — don't let the hips rotate to help the lift"

**Common Errors**:
- Shrugging the shoulder at the top (upper trap dominates)
- Rotating the torso excessively to complete the row (turns into a partial deadlift)
- Rowing to the chest instead of the hip (bicep dominates instead of lat)

**Progressions**: Chest-supported row → Pendlay row → Renegade row  
**Regressions**: Incline dumbbell row (can use KB with both hands)

**Rep Range**: 8–15 reps per side  
**Nathan Note**: The single-arm KB row is the primary lat builder in Nathan's toolkit. Row to the hip to maximize lat involvement. This is a V-shape movement and should appear in every upper-body session.

---

### A7. KB Clean
**Primary**: Glutes, hamstrings, lats, traps  
**Secondary**: Core, forearms, shoulder (catch position)  

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Start like a swing — explosive hip drive is the motor"
- "Pull the elbow up high and tight to the body"
- "Guide the bell into the rack position — don't let it flip over your hand"
- "Rack position: bell rests on forearm, elbow at ribcage, shoulder packed"
- "Soft landing in the rack — absorb, don't crash"

**Common Errors**:
- Banging the forearm on every catch (elbow not pulling up)
- Using the arm to muscle the bell up (hip power is insufficient)
- Rack position with the bell away from the body (creates shoulder stress)

**Rep Range**: 5–8 per side (heavier loads); 10–15 per side (moderate loads in circuits)  
**Nathan Note**: The clean is a power development tool and a prerequisite for the KB press. It also serves as a conditioning movement in complexes.

---

### A8. KB Press (Single-Arm Overhead)
**Primary**: Anterior and lateral deltoid, triceps  
**Secondary**: Upper trap, core (anti-lateral flexion), serratus  

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Tight rack position first — don't press from a flared-out position"
- "Press up and slightly back — the arm finishes overhead in line with the ear, not in front"
- "Pack the shoulder — actively reach upward at the top, don't just stop"
- "Brace the core hard — opposite glute squeezed to prevent lumbar hyperextension"
- "Slow and controlled on the way down"

**Common Errors**:
- Leaning sideways (QL weakness or load too heavy)
- Flaring elbow out excessively on the way up
- Not achieving full lockout overhead
- Shrugging the shoulder at the top

**Progressions**: Half-kneeling press → Bottoms-up press → Z-press (seated on floor, legs straight)  
**Regressions**: Push press (use leg drive to assist)

**Rep Range**: 5–10 reps per side  
**Nathan Note**: The overhead press is critical for lateral deltoid and shoulder development (V-shape). Single-arm version recruits more core than bilateral pressing — appropriate for Nathan's equipment setup.

---

### A9. KB Turkish Get-Up (TGU)
**Primary**: Shoulder complex (full), core (all planes), hip flexors, glutes  
**Secondary**: Triceps, lats, serratus, upper back  

**Phases**: Lie → Roll to elbow → Press to hand → Hip bridge → Half-kneel → Stand (reverse to return)

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Eyes on the bell at all times"
- "Punch the bell up — create a vertical arm with the shoulder packed"
- "Move slow and deliberate — this is a thinking movement"
- "Find a stable position at each step before moving to the next"

**Common Errors**: Rushing through phases, losing shoulder pack, looking away from the bell

**Rep Range**: 1–3 reps per side (this is a practice movement, not a high-rep exercise)  
**Nathan Note**: The TGU is the most comprehensive single exercise in Nathan's arsenal. It builds shoulder integrity, glute strength, hip mobility, and total-body coordination simultaneously. Include 1–2 TGUs per upper-body session as a warm-up or movement practice.

---

### A10. KB Farmer's Carry
**Primary**: Forearms, traps, core (anti-lateral flexion)  
**Secondary**: Glute med (single-arm), erectors, quads, calves  

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Stand tall — don't let the weight pull you into a lateral lean"
- "Shoulder packed down — resist the shrug"
- "Strong heel-to-toe stride"

**Variations**:
- **Two-hand**: General strength and conditioning
- **Single-arm (suitcase carry)**: Anti-lateral flexion core, glute med activation
- **Overhead carry**: Shoulder stability and overhead positioning

**Distance/Duration**: 20–40 meters per set, or 30–60 seconds  
**Nathan Note**: One of the most underrated movements. Excellent for grip, posture, trap development, and core strength. Use as a finisher or between sets of upper body work.

---

### A11. KB Hip Thrust (Floor-Based)
**Primary**: Glute max (highest activation of any exercise)  
**Secondary**: Hamstrings, adductors  

**Setup**: Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat. Place KB on hips for resistance.

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Drive through the heels — not the toes"
- "Squeeze glutes explosively at the top — hold for 1–2 seconds"
- "Full hip extension at top — hips parallel to the floor"
- "Tuck chin — avoid neck strain"
- "Press feet into the floor; don't let knees cave"

**Progressions**: Single-leg hip thrust → Elevated hip thrust (shoulders on bench if available) → Band-resisted hip thrust  
**Regressions**: Glute bridge (floor only, no elevation)

**Rep Range**: 15–30 reps (bodyweight); 10–20 reps (loaded)  
**Nathan Note**: The hip thrust produces the highest glute max EMG activation of any exercise — higher than squats, deadlifts, or lunges. This should be a staple in every lower-body session. KB placed on hip crease for resistance.

## SECTION B — CALISTHENICS EXERCISES

### B1. Pull-Up
**Primary**: Lats, teres major  
**Secondary**: Biceps, rear delt, rhomboids, mid-trap, core  

**Grip Variations**:
- **Pronated (overhand)**: Greater lat engagement; wider grip increases lat stretch
- **Supinated (chin-up)**: Greater bicep involvement; slightly easier for most people
- **Neutral (hammer)**: Often easiest; balanced lat and bicep activation
- **Wide grip**: More lat emphasis, less range of motion
- **Narrow grip**: More bicep and teres major emphasis

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Start from a dead hang — full shoulder elevation, then depress shoulders to initiate"
- "Lead with the chest — pull the chest to the bar, not the chin over the bar"
- "Elbows drive down to your back pockets"
- "Hold 1 second at top — full lat contraction"
- "Slow on the way down — 3-count eccentric minimum"

**Common Errors**:
- Not starting from a dead hang (removing lat stretch)
- Kipping or using momentum
- Chin leading instead of chest (reduces lat involvement)
- Shrugging into the neck instead of depressing shoulder blades

**Progressions**: Weighted pull-ups → Archer pull-ups → L-sit pull-ups → Typewriter pull-ups  
**Regressions**: Jumping pull-ups → Negatives (eccentric only) → Band-assisted → Ring rows

**Rep Range**: 5–12 for strength/hypertrophy; weighted for progressive overload  
**Nathan Note**: Pull-ups are the single most important exercise for V-shape development. Nathan should build toward weighted pull-ups as the primary overload progression. Every back session should include pull-ups or chin-ups.

---

### B2. Push-Up
**Primary**: Pectoralis major, anterior deltoid, triceps  
**Secondary**: Serratus anterior, core  

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Body is a rigid plank — brace core, squeeze glutes"
- "Elbows at 45 degrees from the torso — not flared 90 degrees"
- "Lower chest to floor — full range of motion"
- "At the top, push the floor away and reach forward (protract scapulae)"
- "Inhale on the way down, exhale on the push"

**Variations**:
- **Wide push-up**: Greater pec emphasis
- **Diamond/close push-up**: Tricep emphasis
- **Incline push-up (feet elevated)**: Upper chest and anterior delt
- **Decline push-up (hands elevated)**: Lower chest
- **Pike push-up**: Anterior and lateral deltoid (shoulder press pattern)
- **Pseudo planche push-up**: Advanced; shifts load to shoulders and serratus

**Progressions**: Archer push-up → One-arm push-up progression → Ring push-up  
**Regressions**: Incline push-up (hands elevated) → Knee push-up

**Rep Range**: 10–30 depending on variation  
**Nathan Note**: Use push-up variations to train the shoulder press pattern (pike push-up, handstand push-up progressions) for lateral delt development to complement KB pressing.

---

### B3. Dip
**Primary**: Lower chest (costal pec), triceps, anterior delt  
**Secondary**: Rhomboids, serratus  
**Equipment**: Parallel bars, rings, or two sturdy chairs/tables

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Slight forward lean for chest emphasis; upright for tricep emphasis"
- "Lower until upper arm is at least parallel to the floor"
- "Elbows at 45 degrees — not flared wide"
- "Control the descent — 2–3 second eccentric"

**Progressions**: Ring dips → Weighted dips  
**Regressions**: Bench dips → Box dips → Assisted dips (one foot for support)

**Rep Range**: 8–20 reps  
**Nathan Note**: Dips are Nathan's primary chest and tricep exercise. Ring dips add a stabilization demand. Excellent for building the lower chest fullness that completes the upper body aesthetic.

---

### B4. Inverted Row
**Primary**: Lats, rhomboids, rear delt, mid-trap  
**Secondary**: Biceps, core  
**Equipment**: KB at floor level under a stable table or bar

**Setup**: Lie under a bar/table, hands gripping overhand, body in a straight line from heels to head, pulling chest up to bar.

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Body stays completely rigid — like a reverse plank"
- "Pull chest to bar — lead with chest, not chin"
- "Elbows drive back"
- "Hold 1 second at the top"

**Progressions**: Feet elevated → Weighted vest → Rings → Feet-elevated with slow eccentric  
**Regressions**: Higher bar angle (easier) → Bent-knee variation

**Rep Range**: 10–20 reps  
**Nathan Note**: An excellent horizontal row alternative when pull-up volume is fatiguing the biceps. Great antagonist pair with push-ups.

---

### B5. Glute Bridge (Bodyweight)
**Primary**: Glute max, hamstrings  
**Secondary**: Core, adductors  

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Drive through heels — toes can even be slightly raised"
- "Squeeze glutes as hard as possible at the top"
- "Full hip extension — no half reps"
- "Hold 2–3 seconds at top for maximum glute activation"

**Progressions**: Single-leg glute bridge → Elevated hip thrust → Loaded hip thrust  
**Nathan Note**: The regression of the hip thrust. Can be done as a warm-up to activate glutes before heavy lower body work.

---

### B6. Bodyweight Squat
**Primary**: Quads, glute max  
**Secondary**: Hamstrings, calves, core  

**Progressions**: Narrow stance → Wide stance → Pause squat → Jump squat → Pistol squat progression (box pistol → assisted pistol → full pistol)

**Nathan Note**: The pistol squat (single-leg squat) is the gold standard calisthenics leg exercise. Nathan should progress toward it as an ongoing long-term goal. It requires significant quad strength, hip mobility, and ankle flexibility.

---

### B7. Lunge Variations
**Primary**: Quads, glute max, glute med  
**Secondary**: Hamstrings, core, adductors  

**Variations**:
- **Forward lunge**: More quad dominant
- **Reverse lunge**: More glute dominant (recommended for Nathan)
- **Lateral lunge**: Adductor and glute med emphasis
- **Bulgarian split squat**: Greatest glute and quad overload of any lunge; rear foot elevated on chair/step
- **Walking lunge**: Conditioning and athletic carry-over

**Bulgarian Split Squat Cues**:
- "Front foot far enough forward that the knee stays behind the toes at the bottom"
- "Drive through the heel of the front foot"
- "Torso upright — resist the urge to lean forward"
- "Drop the back knee toward the floor — full range of motion"

**Rep Range**: 8–15 per leg  
**Nathan Note**: The Bulgarian split squat with a KB (goblet hold or suitcase hold) is arguably the best lower body exercise in Nathan's equipment set. It rivals squatting for quad and glute development and applies significant unilateral load.

---

### B8. Plank Variations
**Primary**: Transverse abdominis, rectus abdominis, obliques  
**Secondary**: Glutes, shoulders, serratus  

**Variations**:
- **Standard plank**: General core stability
- **Side plank**: Obliques, glute med
- **Plank with shoulder tap**: Anti-rotation (obliques, TVA)
- **Hollow body hold**: The calisthenics gold standard for anterior core
- **RKC plank** (maximum tension version): Squeeze everything — fists, arms, glutes, legs — intensifies the standard plank dramatically

**Hollow Body Hold Cues**:
- "Lower back pressed flat to the floor — no air gap"
- "Ribs compressed down — no rib flare"
- "Arms overhead, legs low — find the lowest position where you can maintain the lower back contact"
- "This should burn quickly"

**Duration**: 20–60 seconds for planks; 10–30 seconds for hollow body (harder)  
**Nathan Note**: The hollow body hold is the foundational core exercise of gymnastics and calisthenics. It builds the anterior core strength that protects the spine in all heavy lifts.

---

### B9. Dead Bug
**Primary**: Transverse abdominis, rectus abdominis  
**Secondary**: Hip flexors (controlled), shoulder flexors  

**Coaching Cues**:
- "Lower back pressed to the floor throughout — this is the single most important cue"
- "Exhale fully as you extend opposite arm and leg"
- "Move SLOWLY — this is a control exercise, not a conditioning one"
- "Never let the lower back arch off the floor"

**Rep Range**: 5–10 reps per side (slow and controlled)  
**Nathan Note**: The dead bug is the antidote to anterior pelvic tilt. It teaches the TVA to stabilize the spine against limb movement — the exact demand of running, swings, and heavy lifts.

## SECTION C — RUNNING AND CARDIO

### C1. Steady-State Running (LISS — Low Intensity Steady State)
**Intensity**: 60–70% max heart rate (conversational pace)  
**Duration**: 20–60 minutes  
**Max HR Estimate for Nathan (44)**: 220 – 44 = 176 bpm; LISS zone = 105–123 bpm  

**Benefits**:
- Enhances mitochondrial density (aerobic capacity)
- Promotes fat oxidation (especially fasted)
- Active recovery — increases blood flow to muscles without additional damage
- Cardiovascular health

**Nathan Note**: 2–3 LISS runs per week at 20–40 minutes is ideal during recomp. Keep intensity low enough to preserve muscle (high-intensity cardio can compete with muscle-building signals if overdone).

---

### C2. HIIT — High Intensity Interval Training
**Format**: Alternating work and rest intervals  
**Common Protocols**:
- **Tabata**: 20s max effort / 10s rest × 8 rounds (4 minutes total)
- **30/30**: 30s hard / 30s easy × 8–10 rounds
- **Sprint intervals**: 10–15s all-out sprint / 60–90s walk × 6–10 rounds

**Benefits**:
- Significantly elevates EPOC (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption — "afterburn")
- Improves VO2 max more efficiently than LISS
- Preserves muscle mass better than long-duration cardio
- Time efficient

**Agent Programming Rule**: Maximum 2 HIIT sessions per week. HIIT creates significant muscle damage and nervous system fatigue — program at least 48 hours before heavy lifting sessions.

---

### C3. KB Cardio Conditioning (Running Path Alternative)
When the running path isn't available, KB complexes provide metabolic conditioning:

**KB Complex Example (Anti-Glycolytic Circuit)**:
- 5 KB Swings → 5 KB Cleans (per side) → 5 KB Presses (per side)
- Rest 60–90 seconds
- Repeat 4–6 rounds

**Benefits**: Cardiovascular conditioning + resistance training stimulus simultaneously

## SECTION D — TARGETED GLUTE ACTIVATION EXERCISES

These are not main lifts — they are activation and isolation movements to directly target the glutes.

### D1. Clamshell
**Target**: Glute medius  
**Setup**: Lie on side, hips stacked, knees bent at 45 degrees  
**Cue**: "Open the knee like a clamshell — don't let the hip roll back"  
**Rep Range**: 20–30 reps per side (high rep; glute med is Type I dominant)  
**Nathan Note**: Use as a warm-up before every lower body session.

### D2. Hip Abduction (Standing or Side-lying)
**Target**: Glute medius, glute minimus  
**Progressions**: Side-lying → Standing → With resistance band

### D3. Cable/Band Kickback (KB Variation)
**Target**: Glute max at end-range extension  
**Setup**: On all fours; drive heel toward ceiling, squeezing glute hard at top  
**Rep Range**: 15–25 per side  
**Nathan Note**: The donkey kick finishes the glute max at the shortened position — complementary to swings and hip thrusts that stress the stretched position.

### D4. Frog Pump
**Target**: Glute max with external rotation  
**Setup**: On back, soles of feet together, knees out; pump hips up squeezing glutes  
**Rep Range**: 20–40 reps  
**Nathan Note**: The external rotation position maximally recruits glute max fibers that are missed in standard hip thrusts.

## SECTION E — MOBILITY AND STRETCHING EXERCISES

### E1. Hip Flexor Stretch (Kneeling)
**Target**: Iliopsoas, rectus femoris  
**Hold**: 60–90 seconds per side  
**Cue**: "Squeeze the rear glute and tuck the pelvis under slightly — you should feel the stretch deepen"

### E2. Pigeon Pose
**Target**: Piriformis, glute med, hip external rotators  
**Hold**: 90 seconds per side  
**Nathan Note**: Essential for glute recovery and preventing piriformis syndrome, especially after heavy swing and running sessions.

### E3. Doorframe Lat Stretch
**Target**: Latissimus dorsi  
**Setup**: Grab a doorframe overhead; let your bodyweight pull you into the stretch  
**Hold**: 30–60 seconds

### E4. Thoracic Extension (Over Foam Roller / Edge of Bed)
**Target**: Thoracic spine mobility  
**Why**: Improves overhead pressing, reduces shoulder pain, corrects rounded shoulders  
**Hold**: 10–15 repetitions of gentle extension

### E5. World's Greatest Stretch
**Movement sequence**: Forward lunge → reach inside arm to floor → rotate top arm to ceiling → rock back to hamstring stretch  
**Targets**: Hip flexors, thoracic rotation, hamstrings, groin  
**Reps**: 5 per side as part of warm-up

### E6. Couch Stretch
**Target**: Hip flexors, quads  
**Setup**: Rear foot against the wall, front leg in lunge position  
**Hold**: 90 seconds per side  
**Nathan Note**: One of the most effective hip flexor stretches available. Critical for eliminating anterior pelvic tilt and unlocking glute activation.

**Upper Pull**:
- Pull-Up + KB Single-Arm Row + Inverted Row + Face Pull + Bicep curl

**Conditioning**:
