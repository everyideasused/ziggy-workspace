# Residential Construction Sequencing

> **Purpose:** The builder's critical path — the order in which a house or building gets built, from dirt to done. Understanding sequencing is essential for both managing subcontractors as a GC and planning personal projects. Every delay, defect, and cost overrun traces back to something being done out of sequence or a predecessor being incomplete.

---

## The Cardinal Rule of Sequencing

**Work flows from the outside in, from the bottom up, and from rough to finish.**

- Outside in: site work → foundation → framing → envelope → MEP rough → insulation → drywall → finish
- Bottom up: footings → foundation walls → floor → walls → roof
- Rough to finish: framing → rough mechanicals → insulation → drywall → trim → paint → fixtures

Breaking this sequence creates rework, damage to completed work, and inspection failures.

---

## Phase Sequence — Residential New Construction

### Phase 1: Site Preparation

**Activities:**
- Clearing and grubbing (remove trees, brush, topsoil in building footprint)
- Rough grading (establish building pad elevation, drainage slopes)
- Erosion control (silt fence, construction entrance, required before any disturbance)
- Utility location and marking (call 811 — every time, no exceptions)
- Temporary utilities (power pole, water meter, portable toilet)

**Critical predecessor:** Permits must be in hand. Erosion control must be installed before any earth disturbance.

**Common sequencing mistake:** Starting grading before the survey is staked. The building location on the lot is a legal requirement — setbacks, easements, and lot lines must be marked.

**Inspection trigger:** Erosion control in place (some jurisdictions require pre-construction inspection).

---

### Phase 2: Foundation

**Activities:**
- Excavation (foundation trenches, basement dig, pier holes)
- Footing forms and rebar placement
- Footing pour and cure
- Foundation wall forming (or block laying)
- Waterproofing / damp-proofing exterior of foundation walls
- Drain tile installation (perimeter drainage)
- Under-slab plumbing rough (if slab-on-grade or basement slab)
- Under-slab vapor barrier and insulation
- Backfill (only after foundation is braced or floor structure is in place)

**Critical predecessor:** Soil bearing verification. If soil conditions don't match the geotechnical report (or presumptive code values), stop and get engineering input before pouring concrete on questionable soil.

**Common sequencing mistakes:**
- Backfilling before the floor structure braces the foundation walls (creates lateral pressure that can bow or crack unbraced walls)
- Pouring the slab before under-slab plumbing is inspected
- Forgetting the vapor barrier under slab (moisture problems for the life of the building)

**Inspection triggers:** Footing (before pour), foundation wall (before backfill), under-slab plumbing, under-slab vapor barrier.

---

### Phase 3: Floor Structure

**Activities:**
- Sill plate installation (pressure-treated, anchor bolts, sill seal gasket)
- Rim joist / band joist
- Floor joist installation (dimensional lumber, engineered I-joists, or floor trusses)
- Bridging / blocking at bearing points and mid-span
- Subfloor sheathing (glued and screwed for squeak-free performance)
- Beam and post installation (steel beams, LVL beams, adjustable columns)

**Critical predecessor:** Foundation must be cured, inspected, and level. Anchor bolts must be in place and properly located.

**Common sequencing mistake:** Installing subfloor before the framing crew has laid out wall plate locations. Some builders prefer to deck the floor first (provides a work platform), others frame walls on the slab/deck. Either approach works but must be planned.

**Inspection trigger:** Floor framing (often combined with wall framing inspection).

---

### Phase 4: Wall Framing

**Activities:**
- Layout (snap lines for plate locations, mark door/window openings)
- Plate cutting and assembly (bottom plate, top plate, double top plate)
- Stud layout at 16" or 24" OC
- Header installation at all openings
- Wall raising and bracing
- Plumbing and squaring
- Sheathing (structural panels — OSB or plywood, or ZIP system)
- Hold-down and strap installation per engineering

**Critical predecessor:** Floor must be level, square, and structurally complete. Engineered connections (hold-downs, straps) must be specified before framing starts — not added after.

**Common sequencing mistakes:**
- Framing walls before reviewing the window/door schedule (openings don't match units ordered)
- Installing sheathing before verifying wall plumb and square (sheathing locks in errors)
- Forgetting to install backing for wall-mounted fixtures (cabinets, grab bars, TV mounts, towel bars)

**Inspection trigger:** Wall framing, shear wall nailing, hold-down installation.

---

### Phase 5: Roof Structure

**Activities:**
- Ceiling joist or truss installation
- Ridge board/beam installation (for stick-framed roofs)
- Rafter cutting and installation (or truss setting by crane)
- Collar ties, rafter ties, and hurricane clips
- Roof sheathing
- Fascia and subfascia
- Roof underlayment (felt or synthetic)
- Drip edge and ice/water shield at eaves and valleys
- Roofing material installation (shingles, metal, tile)

**Critical predecessor:** Walls must be plumb, braced, and sheathed (or at least the sheathing corners completed for racking resistance). Trusses require crane access — site must accommodate the crane reach.

**Common sequencing mistakes:**
- Shingling before the roof sheathing is inspected
- Forgetting ice/water shield at valleys and eaves (code requirement in many zones)
- Not installing fascia before gutters (fascia must be in place first, but gutter contractor may arrive before fascia is complete)

**Inspection trigger:** Roof framing (before sheathing, or with sheathing if jurisdiction allows).

**Milestone: Dry-in / Under Roof.** Once the roof is on and the walls are sheathed with WRB, the building is "dried in." This is a major milestone — interior work can begin without weather damage risk. Many builders push to dry-in as fast as possible.

---

### Phase 6: Windows, Doors & Exterior Envelope

**Activities:**
- Window and door installation with proper flashing sequence (sill flashing → unit → side flashing → head flashing — lapped shingle-style, bottom to top)
- Housewrap / WRB installation and integration with window/door flashing
- Exterior trim
- Siding or cladding installation
- Exterior painting or staining (often done before interior to protect from weather)
- Gutter installation
- Deck framing and ledger board connection (with proper ledger flashing)

**Critical predecessor:** Roof must be complete. WRB must be properly lapped and integrated before siding goes on. Windows/doors must be installed before siding (the siding terminates against them).

**Common sequencing mistakes:**
- Installing siding before windows are in (leaves no way to properly flash the window-to-wall transition)
- Reverse-lapping WRB seams (upper course must lap over lower course — water sheds down, not up)
- Nailing through the WRB drainage plane without sealing penetrations
- Attaching a deck ledger without proper flashing (the number one cause of catastrophic deck failures)

---

### Phase 7: Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing Rough-In (MEP Rough)

**Activities:**
- HVAC ductwork and equipment rough (supply, return, refrigerant lines)
- Electrical rough (panel, circuits, boxes, low-voltage wiring, smoke/CO detector locations)
- Plumbing rough (supply lines, drain/waste/vent — DWV, water heater location, gas piping)
- Fire sprinkler rough (if required)
- Low-voltage rough (data, security, speaker wire, smart home)

**Critical predecessor:** Framing must be complete and inspected. All three trades need access to the same wall and floor cavities, so coordination is essential. Typical sequence: plumbing first (largest pipes, least flexible routing), then HVAC (ductwork needs space), then electrical (most flexible routing).

**Common sequencing mistakes:**
- Plumber and electrician drilling through the same structural members without coordination (over-notching/boring weakens framing)
- Running ducts in exterior walls (loses insulation space, creates condensation risk)
- Not pulling low-voltage wiring during rough-in (nearly impossible to add after drywall)
- Electrician placing boxes without confirming cabinet layout (outlets end up behind upper cabinets)

**Inspection triggers:** Plumbing rough, HVAC rough, electrical rough — each trade inspected separately in most jurisdictions. All three must pass before insulation.

---

### Phase 8: Insulation & Air Sealing

**Activities:**
- Air sealing all penetrations (caulk, spray foam, gaskets at all wire/pipe penetrations through plates and sheathing)
- Attic bypasses sealed (plumbing stacks, duct chases, chimney gaps, recessed light housings)
- Rim joist insulation and air sealing
- Wall cavity insulation (batts, blown-in, or spray foam)
- Ceiling/attic insulation
- Floor insulation (if over crawlspace or unconditioned space)
- Vapor retarder installation (if required — climate-zone dependent)

**Critical predecessor:** ALL mechanical rough-ins must be complete and inspected. All penetrations must be made before air sealing — cutting holes after sealing defeats the purpose.

**Common sequencing mistakes:**
- Insulating before all rough inspections pass (may need to remove insulation for corrections)
- Insulating before air sealing (insulation is NOT an air barrier — batts especially allow air to pass right through)
- Compressing batts into cavities (reduces R-value proportionally)
- Missing the rim joist (often the largest thermal weak point in the building)

**Inspection trigger:** Insulation inspection (before drywall). Many jurisdictions now require blower door testing for air leakage.

---

### Phase 9: Drywall

**Activities:**
- Hanging drywall (ceiling first, then walls — ceiling edges are covered by wall sheets)
- Taping and mudding (three coats minimum for Level 4 finish, sanded between coats)
- Texture application (if specified — skip-trowel, orange peel, knockdown, smooth)
- Priming (PVA primer on new drywall before any finish painting)

**Critical predecessor:** Insulation must be inspected and approved. All backing/blocking must be in place. Any in-wall work that requires access (tub/shower units, medicine cabinets, fireplaces) must be set before drywall encloses the cavity.

**Common sequencing mistakes:**
- Hanging drywall before the tub/shower unit is in place (one-piece tubs won't fit through a standard door — they must be set during framing or before drywall closes the bathroom walls)
- Not verifying backing for wall-mounted items (towel bars, grab bars, upper cabinets) — last chance before it's hidden forever
- Rushing tape/mud coats (insufficient drying between coats causes bubbling and cracking)

**Inspection trigger:** Drywall nailing inspection (some jurisdictions).

---

### Phase 10: Interior Finish & Trim

**Activities:**
- Interior painting (prime → ceilings → walls → trim — always in this order)
- Cabinet installation (kitchen, bath, laundry)
- Countertop templating and installation
- Interior door hanging and hardware
- Baseboard, casing, crown molding, and window trim
- Closet shelving and organizers
- Stair railings, balusters, and handrails
- Tile installation (floors, backsplash, shower)
- Hardwood or LVP flooring (usually after cabinets, before base trim)
- Finish plumbing (fixtures, faucets, toilets — "trim out")
- Finish electrical (devices, covers, fixtures, switch plates — "trim out")
- Finish HVAC (registers, grilles, thermostat)
- Appliance installation

**Critical predecessor:** Drywall must be complete, primed, and painted (at least first coat on walls). Cabinets before countertops. Flooring before base trim. Painting before finish electrical trim (painters damage cover plates and fixtures).

**Common sequencing mistakes:**
- Installing hardwood floors before painting (paint drips on hardwood are a nightmare)
- Installing base trim before flooring (trim should sit on top of finished floor)
- Finishing plumbing before countertops are in (faucet holes must align with sink cutout)
- Installing light fixtures before final paint coat (painters must mask or remove them)

---

### Phase 11: Final Punch & Closeout

**Activities:**
- Punch list walk (identify all incomplete, defective, or damaged items)
- Punch list corrections by each trade
- Final cleaning (construction clean, then detail clean)
- Final grading and landscaping
- Driveway and walkway paving
- Final mechanical test and balance (HVAC)
- Final inspection by building department
- Certificate of Occupancy issued
- Warranty documentation to owner
- Key handover

**Inspection trigger:** Final building inspection (all trades, all systems).

---

## The Critical Path — What Actually Drives the Schedule

In residential construction, the critical path typically runs through:

1. **Foundation** — weather-dependent, curing time is fixed
2. **Framing** — largest single phase, most labor-intensive
3. **Dry-in** — unlocks all interior work (weather no longer a constraint)
4. **MEP rough + inspection** — three trades competing for the same cavities, plus inspection wait times
5. **Drywall** — long lead time (hang, tape, three coats, sand, prime) and blocks all finish work
6. **Cabinets + countertops** — long lead items (4-8 weeks) that gate kitchen/bath completion

If any of these fall behind, the entire schedule shifts.
