# Retirement Planning & Optimization

> Cross-cutting reference for [[Personal Finance Knowledge Base]]
> **Priority:** 🟢 Ongoing — start early (even while paying off debt), optimize over time.

---

## The Retirement Equation

Retirement planning answers one question: **Will you have enough money to sustain your lifestyle when you stop earning income?**

```
Money Needed = Annual Living Expenses × Years in Retirement
Money Available = Savings + Investment Growth + Social Security + Any Pensions
Gap = Money Needed - Money Available
```

If there's a gap, you either save more, invest more aggressively, work longer, or spend less in retirement. Ideally, you figure this out decades in advance when time is still on your side.

---

## The Key Numbers

### How Much Do You Need? (The 25x Rule)
The most commonly cited guideline: save **25 times your expected annual expenses in retirement.**

This is derived from the "4% rule" — research showing that withdrawing 4% of your portfolio annually, adjusted for inflation, has historically sustained a portfolio for 30+ years.

**Example:** If you expect to spend $50,000/year in retirement, you need approximately $1,250,000 in invested assets.

**Caveats:**
- The 4% rule assumes a 30-year retirement and a 50/50 to 75/25 stock/bond portfolio
- Earlier retirement (40s-50s) may require a more conservative 3-3.5% withdrawal rate
- Social Security and any pensions reduce the amount your portfolio needs to cover
- Healthcare costs are the biggest wildcard before Medicare eligibility (age 65)

### Savings Milestones by Age (Rough Benchmarks)
These assume saving 15% of gross income starting in your mid-20s:

| Age | Target (× Salary) |
|-----|-------------------|
| 30 | 1× annual salary |
| 35 | 2× annual salary |
| 40 | 3× annual salary |
| 45 | 4× annual salary |
| 50 | 6× annual salary |
| 55 | 7× annual salary |
| 60 | 8× annual salary |
| 67 | 10× annual salary |

If you're behind, don't panic — increase your savings rate and let compounding catch up. Starting late is still better than never starting.

## Retirement Account Deep Dive

### 401(k) / 403(b)

**What it is:** Employer-sponsored retirement account. Contributions come from your paycheck.

**Traditional 401(k):** Contributions reduce taxable income now. You pay taxes when you withdraw in retirement.
**Roth 401(k):** Contributions are after-tax. Growth and withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.

**2025 Contribution Limits:**
- Under 50: $23,500/year
- Age 50+: $31,000/year (includes catch-up)
- Age 60-63: $34,750/year (enhanced catch-up — new provision)

**Employer Match:** This is free money. If your employer matches 50% of contributions up to 6% of salary, contributing at least 6% gets you the full match. Not capturing the match is leaving guaranteed 50-100% returns on the table.

**Traditional vs. Roth 401(k) Decision:**
- **Choose Roth** if you expect your tax rate to be higher in retirement (early career, expect income growth, tax rates may rise)
- **Choose Traditional** if you expect your tax rate to be lower in retirement (peak earning years, high current tax bracket)
- **When uncertain:** Split between both for tax diversification
- For someone in the accumulation phase with decades ahead, Roth is generally favored — tax-free growth for 30+ years is extremely powerful

### Roth IRA

**What it is:** Individual retirement account with after-tax contributions and tax-free growth/withdrawals.

**2025 Limits:** $7,000/year ($8,000 if 50+)
**Income Limits (2025):** Phase-out begins at $150,000 MAGI (single) / $236,000 (married filing jointly). Above these limits, direct Roth IRA contributions aren't allowed — but the "Backdoor Roth" strategy may be available.

**Why it's powerful:**
- Tax-free growth forever
- No RMDs (Required Minimum Distributions) during your lifetime
- Contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn anytime, tax-free and penalty-free — this provides a safety valve
- Excellent estate planning vehicle (heirs inherit tax-free, though they must distribute within 10 years under SECURE Act rules)

**Backdoor Roth IRA:** For high-income earners above the Roth IRA income limits — contribute to a Traditional IRA (non-deductible), then convert to Roth. Legal and widely used, but consult a tax professional if you have existing Traditional IRA balances (pro-rata rule applies).

### Traditional IRA

**What it is:** Individual retirement account with potentially tax-deductible contributions and tax-deferred growth.

**Deductibility:** If you (or your spouse) are covered by an employer retirement plan, the deduction phases out at certain income levels. If neither is covered, it's fully deductible regardless of income.

**When it makes sense:** Primarily as a vehicle for the Backdoor Roth strategy, or if you're not covered by an employer plan and want a tax deduction.

### HSA (Health Savings Account)

**What it is:** A savings account for medical expenses, available if you have a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP).

**2025 Limits:** $4,300 (individual) / $8,550 (family)

**The triple tax advantage:**
1. Contributions are pre-tax (or tax-deductible)
2. Growth is tax-free
3. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free

**The stealth retirement account strategy:** Pay medical expenses out of pocket now, save receipts, let the HSA grow tax-free. After age 65, withdraw for any purpose (medical withdrawals remain tax-free; non-medical withdrawals are taxed like a Traditional IRA but with no penalty). Effectively becomes the most tax-advantaged account in existence.

### SEP IRA / Solo 401(k)

**For self-employed or side income:** If you have any 1099 income, you may be eligible for these accounts with much higher contribution limits ($69,000+ for Solo 401k in 2025). Consult a tax professional — the math can be very favorable.

## The Contribution Priority Order

For someone in the accumulation phase, the recommended order is:

```
1. 401(k) up to employer match ← Free money first
2. HSA (if eligible) ← Triple tax advantage
3. Roth IRA (max out) ← Tax-free growth
4. 401(k) to max ← Tax-deferred growth
5. Taxable brokerage ← After all tax-advantaged space is used
```

**At minimum:** Contribute enough to capture the full employer match. This is the single highest-return, lowest-risk financial move available.

## Social Security

### How Benefits Are Calculated
Benefits are based on your highest 35 years of earnings. The SSA applies a formula to calculate your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA), which is what you'd receive at Full Retirement Age (FRA).

### Claiming Strategies

| Claiming Age | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| 62 (earliest) | ~30% permanent reduction from FRA benefit |
| 67 (FRA for most) | Full benefit amount |
| 70 (maximum) | ~24% permanent increase from FRA benefit |

**General guidance:**
- Waiting to claim increases your benefit by roughly 8% per year between FRA and 70
- If you're healthy and can afford to wait, delaying to 70 is usually mathematically optimal
- The break-even point is typically around age 80 — if you live past 80, delaying wins
- Spousal benefits, survivor benefits, and divorce considerations add complexity — consult SSA.gov or a financial planner for your specific situation

### Don't Count on It as Your Only Source
Social Security was designed to replace roughly 40% of pre-retirement income for average earners. It was never meant to be a complete retirement plan. Think of it as a baseline, not the whole picture.

## Retirement Income Planning

### The Bucket Strategy
A common framework for generating retirement income:

**Bucket 1 — Cash (1-2 years of expenses):** HYSA, money market. Covers near-term spending regardless of market conditions.
**Bucket 2 — Conservative (3-5 years of expenses):** Bonds, CDs, short-term bond funds. Refills Bucket 1.
**Bucket 3 — Growth (everything else):** Stocks, index funds. Long-term growth to refill Bucket 2 and fight inflation.

This structure prevents forced selling of stocks during a downturn — you live off Buckets 1-2 while Bucket 3 recovers.

### Tax-Efficient Withdrawal Order
In retirement, which account you withdraw from matters for taxes:

**General order:**
1. Taxable accounts first (use up basis, take advantage of lower capital gains rates)
2. Tax-deferred accounts (401k, Traditional IRA) — taxed as ordinary income
3. Roth accounts last — tax-free, let them grow as long as possible

**Reality:** Most retirees benefit from a blended approach, pulling from different accounts to manage their tax bracket year by year. Roth conversions in low-income years can be powerful.

## FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early)

For reference — not prescriptive, but the concepts are useful even if early retirement isn't the goal:

**The core idea:** Save aggressively (50-70% of income), invest in low-cost index funds, and reach a portfolio of 25-33× annual expenses. At that point, investment returns replace employment income.

**Useful even if you work until 65:**
- The savings rate math applies to everyone
- The concept of "enough" is psychologically liberating
- Building financial independence gives you career flexibility even if you keep working
