# 🫚 Anti-Inflammatory Vegan Nutrition

> **Purpose:** Evidence-based guide to using whole plant foods to minimize systemic inflammation, accelerate recovery, and build long-term resilience. Covers phytonutrients, oxidative stress, and the dietary strategies that support performance and longevity.

---

## Inflammation: Friend and Foe

### Acute vs. Chronic Inflammation

| Type | Trigger | Duration | Outcome |
|------|---------|---------|---------|
| Acute (adaptive) | Training, injury, infection | Hours to days | Healing, adaptation, growth |
| Chronic (pathological) | Poor diet, stress, seed oils, processed food, sleep deprivation | Weeks to years | Disease, impaired recovery, poor performance |

**Training requires acute inflammation** — this is the signal for muscle adaptation. The goal is NOT to suppress all inflammation (anti-inflammatory drug use around training impairs adaptation). The goal is to:
1. Minimize baseline chronic inflammation
2. Allow acute training inflammation to run its course
3. Provide nutrients that support the resolution phase (active process)

### Vegan Diet and Inflammation

Research consistently shows vegans have:
- Lower C-reactive protein (CRP) — the primary inflammation marker
- Lower interleukin-6 (IL-6)
- Lower TNF-alpha
- Higher antioxidant capacity (ORAC)
- Lower oxidative stress markers

The mechanism: high fiber → healthy microbiome → short-chain fatty acids → systemic anti-inflammatory effect + high phytonutrient intake → polyphenols directly inhibit NF-κB (master inflammation switch).

## Phytonutrients — The Plant Defense System

Plants make thousands of compounds (phytonutrients) to defend against pathogens, UV radiation, and oxidative stress. When we eat these plants, we benefit from the same protective chemistry.

### The Color Code — Eat the Rainbow

**Each color = different phytonutrient class:**

| Color | Phytonutrient Class | Key Compounds | Best Sources |
|-------|-------------------|--------------|-------------|
| 🔴 Red | Lycopene, anthocyanins | Lycopene (anticancer, cardiovascular) | Tomatoes (cooked = more lycopene), watermelon, red pepper |
| 🟠 Orange | Carotenoids | Beta-carotene (vitamin A precursor), alpha-carotene | Sweet potato, carrot, butternut squash, orange |
| 🟡 Yellow | Carotenoids, flavonoids | Lutein/zeaxanthin (eye health), quercetin | Corn, yellow pepper, lemon, pineapple |
| 🟢 Green | Chlorophyll, glucosinolates, folate | Sulforaphane (cancer prevention), folate, iron | Broccoli, kale, spinach, Brussels sprouts, bok choy |
| 🔵🟣 Blue/Purple | Anthocyanins | Anti-inflammatory, cognitive support, blood pressure | Blueberries, blackberries, purple cabbage, eggplant |
| ⚪ White/Tan | Allicin, quercetin, beta-glucan | Allicin (immune, cardiovascular), quercetin | Garlic, onion, mushrooms, oats, cauliflower |

**Goal: 5–9 servings of varied-color produce daily**

| Food | Key Compounds | Anti-Inflammatory Mechanism |

## The Pro-Inflammatory Foods to Minimize

Even on a vegan diet, some foods drive inflammation:

| Food | Mechanism | Practical Limit |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| Refined seed oils (corn, soybean, sunflower) | High omega-6 → arachidonic acid cascade | Use EVOO, avocado oil instead |
| White sugar (excess) | Advanced glycation end products (AGEs), insulin spikes | Use fruit, dates, maple syrup instead |
| Refined flour / ultra-processed carbs | Rapid glucose spikes → oxidative stress | Whole grains > refined |
| Trans fats | Directly pro-inflammatory; still found in some vegan processed foods | Read labels — avoid partially hydrogenated oils |
| Excessive alcohol | Gut permeability ("leaky gut"), liver stress | Minimize |
| Heavily charred/grilled food | Acrylamide, HCAs, PAHs | Use lower temp cooking when possible |
| Ultra-processed vegan foods | Emulsifiers disrupt gut microbiome, refined ingredients drive inflammation | Minimize processed vegan meats, snacks |

## Curcumin — The Most Studied Anti-Inflammatory

### Why Turmeric Is Special

Curcumin (the active compound in turmeric) is one of the most studied natural anti-inflammatory compounds. In trials:
- Comparable to 500mg ibuprofen for pain relief in some conditions
- Reduces DOMS when taken around exercise
- Inhibits NF-κB (master inflammation switch)
- Upregulates Nrf2 (master antioxidant response)
- Some evidence for muscle recovery acceleration

### The Bioavailability Problem

Curcumin is poorly absorbed from turmeric on its own (only 1% of curcumin is bioavailable). Three solutions:

1. **Black pepper (piperine):** Increases bioavailability 2,000%. Always pair turmeric with black pepper.
2. **Fat:** Curcumin is fat-soluble. Consume with a fat source (EVOO, coconut oil, nuts).
3. **Heat:** Cooking turmeric in fat increases bioavailability further.

**Golden milk formula (anti-inflammatory recovery drink):**
```
2 cups plant milk
1 tsp turmeric
¼ tsp black pepper (critical)
½ tsp cinnamon
1 tsp coconut oil or ghee (or any fat)
1 tsp maple syrup
½ inch fresh ginger (optional)
Simmer 5 minutes → strain → drink
```

**For serious anti-inflammatory effect:** 1,000–1,500mg curcumin with piperine daily (supplement form) — food levels are too low for therapeutic effect.

## Omega-6:Omega-3 Ratio — The Inflammation Balance

### Why the Ratio Matters

Omega-6 and omega-3 fatty acids compete for the same enzymes (delta-6 desaturase and COX/LOX enzymes).

- **Omega-6 → arachidonic acid → pro-inflammatory eicosanoids (PGE2, LTB4)**
- **Omega-3 EPA → anti-inflammatory eicosanoids (PGE3, LTB5)**

Evolutionarily, humans ate at a ~4:1 omega-6:omega-3 ratio. Modern Western diets: 15:1 to 20:1 — massively pro-inflammatory.

**Vegan optimal target:** ≤4:1 omega-6:omega-3

### Achieving an Optimal Ratio

**Reduce omega-6:**
- Use EVOO and avocado oil instead of soybean, corn, sunflower oil
- Minimize ultra-processed foods (all use seed oils)

**Increase omega-3:**
- Algae DHA/EPA supplement — 250–500mg daily (most impactful)
- Flaxseed, chia, hemp seeds, walnuts daily (ALA precursor)
- Walnut oil on salads (raw — highest ALA in oils)

## Exercise Recovery Nutrition

### The Inflammation Timeline After Training

| Timeframe | What's Happening | Nutrition Priority |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 0–2 hrs | Acute inflammatory cascade begins; MPS initiated | Protein + carbs (replenish, rebuild) |
| 2–24 hrs | Peak inflammatory cytokines; tissue repair | Adequate protein, anti-inflammatory foods |
| 24–72 hrs | Resolution phase; antioxidants support | Tart cherry, berries, curcumin, omega-3 |
| 72+ hrs | Adaptation and growth | Continued protein; micronutrient support |

### Anti-Inflammatory Recovery Stack

**Immediately post-workout:**
- 35–45g vegan protein (tempeh, pea protein shake)
- 60–100g carbohydrates (replenish glycogen)
- Hydration + electrolytes

**Within 24 hours:**
- Tart cherry juice (12 oz) — shown to reduce DOMS 20–30% in RCTs
- Omega-3 supplement (if not already taken that day)
- Berries (blueberries, blackberries) — 1 cup

**Ongoing support:**
- Turmeric (with black pepper and fat) daily
- Green tea 2–3 cups
- 28g walnuts
- Consistent EVOO use

### When NOT to Suppress Inflammation

Avoid mega-dose antioxidants (vitamin C >1g, vitamin E >400 IU) immediately post-workout — these can blunt the adaptation signal. Let acute training inflammation do its job. Focus anti-inflammatory nutrition in the 24–72 hour window, not immediately after training.

## Polyphenols — The Longevity Compounds

### Why Polyphenol Diversity Matters

Each class of polyphenol feeds different gut bacteria and activates different gene expression pathways. More diversity = more benefit. Aim for 1,000+ mg polyphenols daily (most research suggests 500–1,000mg/day shows benefit).

**Highest polyphenol foods (per 100g):**

| Food | Polyphenols (mg/100g) | Key Compound |
|------|----------------------|-------------|
| Cloves | 15,000+ | Eugenol |
| Peppermint | 11,960 | Rosmarinic acid |
| Dark chocolate (85%) | 1,664 | Epicatechin, catechins |
| Blackberries | 260 | Anthocyanins |
| Blueberries | 560 | Anthocyanins, pterostilbene |
| Flaxseed | 1,528 | Lignans |
| Chestnuts | 1,215 | — |
| Black olives | 569 | Oleuropein |
| Coffee (as beverage) | ~200–400/cup | Chlorogenic acids |
| Green tea | ~100–200/cup | EGCG, catechins |
| Red cabbage | 322 | Anthocyanins |
| Pomegranate | 492 | Punicalagins |
| Plums | 377 | Neochlorogenic acid |
| Artichoke | 260 | Cynarin, chlorogenic acid |

**Practical daily polyphenol targets:**
- 1 cup blueberries or mixed berries
- 1–2 cups green or black tea
- 1 oz dark chocolate (85%+)
- EVOO daily (polyphenol content varies significantly — choose high-quality)
- Herbs and spices with every meal (cloves, oregano, thyme, rosemary, turmeric, ginger)
