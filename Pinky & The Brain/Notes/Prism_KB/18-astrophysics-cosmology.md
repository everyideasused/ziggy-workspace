# Astrophysics & Cosmology

## The Big Picture

Astrophysics applies all of physics — mechanics, thermodynamics, nuclear physics, relativity, quantum mechanics — to the largest objects and scales in the universe. Cosmology asks the biggest questions: How did the universe begin? What is it made of? How will it end?

> **ELI5:** Look up at the stars. Each one is a giant ball of gas so hot that it fuses atoms together, turning hydrogen into helium and releasing enough energy to shine for billions of years. When big stars die, they explode and scatter the heavy elements that make up planets and people. The entire universe started as an unimaginably hot, dense point 13.8 billion years ago and has been expanding and cooling ever since.

## Stellar Physics

### How Stars Work

A star is a balance between two forces:
- **Gravity** pulling inward (wants to collapse the star)
- **Radiation pressure** from nuclear fusion pushing outward (wants to blow it apart)

This balance (**hydrostatic equilibrium**) can last billions of years.

### Stellar Evolution

**Main sequence (90% of a star's life):** Fusing hydrogen to helium in the core. The Sun has been doing this for 4.6 billion years and will continue for ~5 billion more.

**Red giant phase:** Core hydrogen exhausted. Core contracts and heats. Outer layers expand and cool (red). Helium fusion begins in the core.

**For low-mass stars (< 8 M_Sun):** Shed outer layers as a planetary nebula, leaving a **white dwarf** — a carbon/oxygen core supported by electron degeneracy pressure. Size of Earth, mass of Sun.

**For massive stars (> 8 M_Sun):** Fusion continues up the periodic table: He → C → O → Ne → Si → Fe. Iron is the endpoint — fusing iron consumes energy instead of releasing it. The core collapses in seconds:
- If remnant < ~3 M_Sun → **neutron star** (city-sized, held up by neutron degeneracy pressure, spins hundreds of times per second)
- If remnant > ~3 M_Sun → **black hole** (nothing stops the collapse)

The collapse triggers a **supernova** — an explosion so bright it can outshine an entire galaxy. Supernovae scatter heavy elements (carbon, oxygen, iron, gold) into space, seeding future stars and planets.

> **ELI5:** You are literally made of star stuff. The carbon in your body was forged in a star that exploded before our Sun formed. The iron in your blood came from a supernova.

### The Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram

Plot luminosity vs. temperature for stars. Most fall on the **main sequence** — a diagonal band from hot/bright (upper left) to cool/dim (lower right). Position on the main sequence is determined almost entirely by mass: more massive = hotter, brighter, and shorter-lived.

## The Big Bang

> **ELI5:** The universe started 13.8 billion years ago as something incredibly hot and dense — not an explosion IN space, but an explosion OF space itself. Space has been expanding and cooling ever since. The evidence is overwhelming.

### Evidence

**Hubble's Law (1929):** Distant galaxies are moving away from us. The farther away, the faster they recede. Space itself is expanding.
```
v = H₀ × d

H₀ ≈ 70 km/s/Mpc (Hubble constant)
```

**Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB):** The afterglow of the Big Bang. When the universe was ~380,000 years old, it cooled enough for atoms to form, and light could travel freely. That light has been redshifted by the expansion to microwave wavelengths (2.725 K). Discovered 1965 by Penzias and Wilson. The CMB is remarkably uniform but has tiny fluctuations (~1 part in 100,000) that seeded galaxies.

**Big Bang Nucleosynthesis:** The first few minutes produced hydrogen (~75%), helium (~25%), and traces of lithium and deuterium. These predicted abundances match observations precisely.

**Light element abundances, large-scale structure, and the age of the oldest stars** all independently confirm the Big Bang model.

## Dark Matter

> **ELI5:** Galaxies spin so fast that they should fly apart — there isn't enough visible matter to hold them together gravitationally. Something invisible is providing extra gravity. We call it dark matter. It doesn't glow, reflect, or absorb light. We know it's there because we can see its gravitational effects.

### Evidence

- **Galaxy rotation curves:** Stars at the edges of galaxies orbit as fast as inner stars. Without dark matter, outer stars should orbit slower (Keplerian decline). Flat rotation curves imply a massive invisible halo.
- **Gravitational lensing:** Light from distant objects bends around galaxy clusters more than visible mass can explain.
- **CMB fluctuations:** The pattern of hot and cold spots in the CMB requires dark matter to reproduce.
- **Large-scale structure:** Computer simulations of galaxy formation only match observations when dark matter is included.

### What We Know

- Makes up ~27% of the universe's mass-energy
- Interacts gravitationally
- Does NOT interact electromagnetically (invisible)
- Not any known particle in the Standard Model
- Candidates: WIMPs (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles), axions, sterile neutrinos — none detected yet

## Dark Energy

> **ELI5:** In 1998, astronomers discovered that the universe's expansion is accelerating. Something is pushing space apart faster and faster. We call it dark energy, and we have almost no idea what it is. It makes up ~68% of the universe.

### Evidence

**Type Ia supernovae** — Standard candles (known brightness). Distant supernovae are dimmer than expected for a decelerating universe → the expansion is accelerating.

### The Cosmological Constant

The simplest model: dark energy is a constant energy density of empty space (vacuum energy).

```
Λ = cosmological constant (Einstein's "biggest blunder" — turns out he was accidentally right)
```

**The cosmological constant problem:** Quantum field theory predicts vacuum energy ~10¹²⁰ times larger than observed. This is arguably the worst prediction in all of physics.

## Cosmic Inflation

> **ELI5:** In the first tiny fraction of a second after the Big Bang, the universe expanded by a factor of at least 10²⁶ — like a marble expanding to larger than the observable universe in 10⁻³² seconds. This explains why the universe looks so uniform in every direction.

### Problems Inflation Solves

**Horizon problem:** Opposite sides of the CMB are at the same temperature, but they're too far apart to have ever been in causal contact (without inflation).

**Flatness problem:** The universe's geometry is flat (parallel lines stay parallel) to extraordinary precision. Without inflation, this requires absurdly fine-tuned initial conditions.

**Monopole problem:** Grand Unified Theories predict magnetic monopoles. Inflation dilutes them to undetectable densities.

### Quantum Seeds

During inflation, quantum fluctuations in the inflaton field were stretched to cosmic scales. These became the density variations visible in the CMB and eventually grew into galaxies and galaxy clusters. Quantum mechanics seeded the large-scale structure of the universe.
