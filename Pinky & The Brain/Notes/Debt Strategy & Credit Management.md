# Debt Strategy & Credit Management

> Cross-cutting reference for [[Personal Finance Knowledge Base]]
> **Priority:** 🔴 Immediate — high-interest debt is a guaranteed negative return on your money.

---

## The Debt Framework

Not all debt is equal. The key distinction is whether the debt is **working for you** (building an asset or enabling income) or **working against you** (depreciating purchases, consumption spending, high interest).

### Debt Classification

| Category | Interest Range | Examples | Strategy |
|----------|---------------|----------|----------|
| **Toxic** | 15-30%+ | Credit cards, payday loans, store cards | Eliminate immediately — financial emergency |
| **Bad** | 7-15% | Personal loans, high-rate auto loans, private student loans | Aggressive payoff — these drain wealth |
| **Neutral** | 4-7% | Federal student loans, moderate auto loans | Math vs. motivation — payoff or invest depends on context |
| **Tolerable** | 2-4% | Low-rate mortgage, 0% promo financing | Minimum payments okay — invest the difference if disciplined |

**Rule of thumb:** If the interest rate exceeds what you'd reasonably earn investing (~7-10% historical stock market average), prioritize paying it off. If it's below that, the math favors investing — but the psychology of debt-free living has real value too.

## Payoff Strategies

### The Avalanche Method (Mathematically Optimal)
Pay minimums on all debts. Throw every extra dollar at the **highest interest rate** first.

**Pros:** Minimizes total interest paid. Saves the most money over time.
**Cons:** If the highest-rate debt has a large balance, progress feels slow. Motivation can stall.
**Best for:** People who are math-driven and don't need frequent "wins" to stay motivated.

### The Snowball Method (Psychologically Optimal)
Pay minimums on all debts. Throw every extra dollar at the **smallest balance** first.

**Pros:** Quick wins build momentum. Reduces the number of open debts faster. Proven to increase completion rates.
**Cons:** May pay more total interest if the smallest balance isn't the highest rate.
**Best for:** People who need motivation and tangible progress. Those who've tried and failed with the avalanche.

### The Hybrid Approach
Start with snowball to build momentum (eliminate 1-2 small debts quickly), then switch to avalanche for the remaining larger balances.

**Best for:** Most people, honestly. Get the motivational wins, then optimize.

### The Debt Consolidation Option
Roll multiple high-interest debts into a single lower-interest loan or balance transfer card.

**When it makes sense:** If you can get a significantly lower rate, you have the discipline not to re-rack the paid-off cards, and the consolidation fees don't eat the savings.
**When it doesn't:** If it's just shuffling debt around without addressing spending, if the new rate isn't meaningfully better, or if the lower payment tempts you to slow down payoff.

## The Debt Payoff Execution Plan

### Step 1: Full Debt Inventory
List every debt with these fields:

| Debt | Creditor | Balance | APR | Minimum Payment | Payoff Target |
|------|----------|---------|-----|-----------------|---------------|
| | | $ | % | $ | |

### Step 2: Calculate Your Debt Payoff Budget
```
Monthly Take-Home - Essential Expenses - Minimum Debt Payments = Available for Extra Payments
```

### Step 3: Pick Your Strategy
Choose avalanche, snowball, or hybrid based on your personality and debt profile. There's no wrong answer — the best strategy is the one you'll actually follow.

### Step 4: Automate and Protect
- Set up auto-pay for all minimums (never miss a payment)
- Schedule extra payments on a specific date each month
- Freeze or cut up credit cards if spending discipline is an issue (but don't close accounts — that hurts credit score)

### Step 5: Track and Celebrate
- Update debt balances monthly during [[Monthly Finance Review]]
- Create a [[Goal]] note for each major debt payoff milestone
- When a debt is eliminated, redirect that entire payment to the next target (this is the "snowball" effect regardless of which method you use)

## Credit Score Fundamentals

### What Makes Up Your Score (FICO Model)

| Factor | Weight | What It Means |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| Payment History | 35% | On-time payments. One missed payment can tank your score. Auto-pay everything. |
| Credit Utilization | 30% | How much of your available credit you're using. Below 30% is okay, below 10% is excellent. |
| Length of Credit History | 15% | Average age of your accounts. Don't close old cards — they help. |
| Credit Mix | 10% | Having different types (revolving credit, installment loans) helps slightly. Don't take on debt just for this. |
| New Credit Inquiries | 10% | Hard pulls from applications. Multiple inquiries in a short window for the same loan type are typically grouped. |

### Score Ranges

| Range | Rating | Impact |
|-------|--------|--------|
| 800-850 | Exceptional | Best rates on everything. |
| 740-799 | Very Good | Qualifies for most premium products. |
| 670-739 | Good | Decent rates. Room for improvement. |
| 580-669 | Fair | Higher rates. Limited options. |
| 300-579 | Poor | Difficulty qualifying. Very high rates. |

### Quick Credit Score Wins
- **Never miss a payment.** Set up auto-pay for at least minimums on every account.
- **Lower utilization.** Pay down credit card balances. If possible, pay mid-cycle (before the statement closes) to lower reported utilization.
- **Don't close old accounts.** Keep them open and occasionally use them for a small recurring charge.
- **Limit hard inquiries.** Only apply for credit when you need it. Rate-shopping for a mortgage or auto loan within a 14-45 day window counts as a single inquiry.
- **Check for errors.** Pull free reports from annualcreditreport.com annually. Dispute any inaccuracies.

## Credit Cards as Tools, Not Traps

### The Rules for Using Credit Cards Wisely
1. **Pay the full statement balance every month.** No exceptions. If you can't do this consistently, switch to debit.
2. **Use for purchases you'd make anyway.** Credit cards are a payment method, not a spending increase.
3. **Collect rewards on spending you'd do regardless.** Cash back, travel points — these are a bonus, not a reason to spend.
4. **Keep utilization low.** Especially in the months before applying for a mortgage or major loan.

### When to Avoid Credit Cards
- If you carry balances month to month
- If having available credit leads to overspending
- During active debt payoff if discipline is an issue

## Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)

Lenders use DTI to assess your ability to take on new debt. It matters for mortgages, auto loans, and personal loans.

```
Front-End DTI = Housing Costs ÷ Gross Monthly Income (target: ≤28%)
Back-End DTI = All Monthly Debt Payments ÷ Gross Monthly Income (target: ≤36%)
```

**Conventional mortgage qualification** typically requires back-end DTI under 43%, though 36% or below is preferred. FHA allows up to 50% in some cases.

## When to Seek Professional Help

- **Debt exceeds annual income** (excluding mortgage): Consider a fee-only financial advisor or nonprofit credit counseling (NFCC members)
- **Considering bankruptcy:** Consult a bankruptcy attorney — the decision is complex and state-specific
- **IRS debt:** Do not ignore. Set up a payment plan or consult a tax professional
- **Student loan complexity:** Federal repayment plans, PSLF, and forgiveness programs are byzantine. A student loan-specific advisor may be worth it
