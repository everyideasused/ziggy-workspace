# KNOWLEDGE BASE MODULE 06: HOME SERVICES & CONTRACTORS

## THE CONTRACTOR GAME
Hiring contractors is one of the highest-variance spending categories. The difference between a good hire and a bad one can be $5,000-50,000+ on a single project. This module is about finding quality work at fair prices.

---

## THE UNIVERSAL 3-BID RULE

### Never Hire Without 3 Bids
For any job over $500, get a minimum of 3 bids from different contractors. This is non-negotiable.

**Why 3 bids:**
- Establishes market price range for your specific job
- Reveals outliers (both too cheap and too expensive)
- Creates competition among contractors
- Gives you negotiating leverage

**How to compare bids:**
- Request itemized bids (not lump sum) so you can see material vs. labor breakdown
- Ensure all bids cover the same scope of work (apples to apples)
- Cheapest bid is NOT always best — extremely low bids often mean: cutting corners, uninsured, or bait-and-switch with change orders later
- The middle bid is often the sweet spot

---

## FINDING QUALITY CONTRACTORS

### Sources (Best to Worst)
1. **Personal referrals**: Friends, family, neighbors who had similar work done recently. Ask to see the completed work.
2. **Nextdoor app**: Neighborhood-specific recommendations with context from real neighbors.
3. **Local building supply stores**: Staff at lumber yards, plumbing supply houses, electrical supply houses know who does quality work locally.
4. **State licensing boards**: Verify license and check complaint history before hiring.
5. **Google Reviews / Yelp**: Useful with volume (50+ reviews). Read the negative reviews carefully — how did the contractor respond?
6. **HomeAdvisor / Angi**: Lead generation service. Contractors pay for leads. Quality varies. Can be useful for generating initial list.
7. **Thumbtack**: Similar to HomeAdvisor. Instant quotes from interested contractors.
8. **TaskRabbit**: Best for small jobs (under $500). Handyman tasks, furniture assembly, minor repairs.

### Verification Checklist (Before Hiring)
- [ ] **License verified**: Check state licensing board (Tennessee: verify.tn.gov)
- [ ] **Insurance verified**: Request certificate of insurance (COI). Must have general liability AND workers' compensation.
- [ ] **References checked**: Call 2-3 recent clients. Visit completed job if possible.
- [ ] **Written contract**: Detailed scope, timeline, materials, payment schedule, warranty
- [ ] **No "cash only" demand**: Professional contractors accept multiple payment forms
- [ ] **No demand for large upfront payment**: 10-30% deposit is standard. Never 50%+ upfront.
- [ ] **BBB check**: Not definitive but reveals patterns of complaints

## WHEN CONTRACTORS ARE CHEAPEST (SEASONAL PRICING)

### The Off-Season Advantage
Contractors have busy seasons and slow seasons. Slow season = lower prices, faster scheduling, more attention to your job.

| Trade | Busy (Expensive) | Slow (Negotiate) |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|
| HVAC | June-August (cooling), Dec-Feb (heating) | March-May, Sept-Nov |
| Roofing | May-October | November-March |
| Landscaping | April-October | November-March |
| Painting (exterior) | May-September | October-February |
| Painting (interior) | Spring/Fall | January-February, mid-summer |
| Plumbing | No strong season (emergencies year-round) | January-March slightly slower |
| Electrical | Spring-Fall (new construction) | Winter |
| Concrete/masonry | April-October | November-March (weather dependent) |
| Fencing | Spring-Summer | Fall-Winter |
| General remodeling | Spring-Fall | January-March |

### Scheduling Strategy
- Book non-urgent work during the contractor's slow season
- Be flexible on start date: "I'm flexible on timing — when works best for your schedule?" Contractors give better pricing when they can fit you into gaps
- Mid-week starts (Tuesday-Thursday) sometimes preferred over Monday starts

## TRADE-SPECIFIC INTELLIGENCE

### HVAC
- **Get 3 bids minimum** for system replacement. Price varies 30-50% between contractors for the same equipment.
- **Equipment brands**: Carrier, Trane, Lennox = premium. Goodman, Rheem, York = good value. The installer matters more than the brand for longevity.
- **Financing**: Many HVAC companies offer 0% financing promotions. Worth using to preserve cash.
- **Maintenance plans**: $150-300/year for bi-annual tune-ups. Worth it for extending system life and catching issues early. Also keeps warranty valid on many systems.
- **Ductless mini-splits**: For additions or rooms with poor duct access. DIY-friendly kits exist (MrCool) for handy homeowners — saves $2,000-5,000 in labor.
- **Rebates**: Check manufacturer rebates, utility company rebates, and federal tax credits (Inflation Reduction Act provides significant HVAC credits for heat pumps and high-efficiency systems).

### Plumbing
- **Emergency premium**: After-hours plumbing calls cost 50-100% more. For non-emergencies, schedule during normal business hours.
- **Water heater replacement**: Get quotes for both tank and tankless. Tankless upfront is $1,500-3,000 more but saves $100-200/year in energy. Payback: 7-15 years.
- **Fixture upgrades**: Buy your own fixtures from a plumbing supply house (Ferguson, etc.) at better pricing than what the plumber marks up. Many plumbers will install customer-supplied fixtures.
- **Drain cleaning**: Roto-Rooter and Mr. Rooter are expensive but reliable. Independent plumbers often charge 40-60% less for the same work.
- **Sewer line work**: Get a camera inspection ($150-300) before agreeing to any sewer replacement ($5,000-15,000+). Some companies recommend unnecessary replacements.

### Electrical
- **Panel upgrade**: If your home has a Federal Pacific or Zinsco panel, replacement is a safety priority. Get 3 bids — typical range $1,500-3,000.
- **Permit matters**: Electrical work almost always requires a permit. Unlicensed/unpermitted work = liability and insurance issues. Don't skip this.
- **LED retrofit**: Many electricians offer whole-house LED retrofit packages. Calculate ROI based on energy savings — often pays for itself in 2-3 years.
- **EV charger installation**: Level 2 (240V) home charger installation typically $500-1,500 including panel work. Check utility rebates.

### Roofing
- **Storm damage**: After hailstorms, roofers flood the market. Get your own inspector's assessment before accepting a roofer's damage claim. Some inflate damage for insurance claims.
- **Insurance claims**: File your own claim first. Don't let a roofer file on your behalf — this gives them control.
- **Materials**: Architectural shingles (30-50 year warranty, $350-500/square installed) vs. 3-tab shingles (20-25 year, $250-350/square). Architectural is worth the upgrade.
- **Metal roofing**: 2-3x cost of shingles but lasts 50-70 years. Best long-term value for homes you'll stay in long-term.
- **Seasonal pricing**: Fall/winter = 10-20% cheaper. Roofers can work in cold weather (above 40°F for most materials).

### Painting
- **Interior**: $1.50-3.50/sq ft typical for professional painting. Includes prep, primer, 2 coats.
- **Exterior**: $2.00-5.00/sq ft. Includes power wash, prep, 2 coats.
- **DIY savings**: Interior painting is highly DIY-friendly. $50-100 in paint and supplies vs. $2,000-5,000 for professional. Exterior is harder and may justify professional help for multi-story homes.
- **Paint quality**: Spend on premium paint (Benjamin Moore, Sherwin-Williams) — better coverage, longer life. See main KB for paint buying intelligence.

### Landscaping & Lawn Care
- **Lawn mowing**: Weekly service typically $30-60/visit for average suburban lot. Monthly cost: $120-240.
- **DIY vs. service**: If you have a functional mower and 30-60 minutes/week, DIY saves $1,500-3,000/year.
- **Seasonal contracts**: Annual contracts (spring/summer mowing + fall cleanup + winterizing) = 10-20% cheaper than per-visit pricing.
- **Hardscaping (patios, retaining walls)**: Material cost is 30-40% of total. Labor is the majority. Off-season (fall/winter) pricing is significantly better.
- **Irrigation systems**: DIY kits (Rain Bird, Hunter from Home Depot) are genuinely viable for handy homeowners. Professional install is $2,500-5,000+ for average yard.

### Pest Control
- **Annual contracts**: $400-600/year for quarterly treatment (general pest). Better value than per-visit.
- **Termite**: Annual inspection is critical in the Southeast. Termite bonds ($200-400/year) cover treatment if termites appear.
- **DIY general pest**: For ants, roaches, and general pests — Ortho Home Defense ($15-20/gallon at Home Depot) applied quarterly is effective for many situations. YouTube tutorials for application.
- **When to call pros**: Termites, bed bugs, wildlife removal, severe infestations. Don't DIY these.

## NEGOTIATION TACTICS FOR HOME SERVICES

### Leverage Points
- **Off-season scheduling**: "I'm flexible on timing. What's your best price if I schedule during your slow period?"
- **Multiple projects**: "I have the bathroom and the kitchen to do. What's the package price for both?"
- **Referral offer**: "If the work goes well, I have three neighbors interested. Can you factor that into the pricing?"
- **Cash/check discount**: Some contractors offer 3-5% discount for payment by check (avoids credit card processing fees). Ask, but always maintain a paper trail.
- **Materials supply**: "Can I supply the materials myself to save on markup?" Some contractors accept this, some don't. Worth asking on high-cost materials.
- **Scope flexibility**: "Is there a way to reduce cost by simplifying X?" Good contractors will suggest value-engineering options.

### Payment Structure Best Practices
- **10-30% deposit**: Standard and reasonable to secure scheduling
- **Progress payments**: Tied to milestones (e.g., 30% at demolition, 30% at rough-in, balance at completion)
- **Final payment**: Hold 10-20% until final walkthrough and all punch-list items are complete
- **NEVER pay 100% upfront**: This eliminates all your leverage if issues arise
- **Credit card**: Offers chargeback protection if work is not completed or defective

## DIY vs. HIRE DECISION FRAMEWORK

### Always DIY (Save Significantly, Low Risk)
- Interior painting
- Toilet replacement (YouTube + $200 = done)
- Faucet replacement (YouTube + $100-300 = done)
- Light fixture swaps (if existing wiring is in place, no new circuits)
- Caulking and weatherstripping
- Gutter cleaning
- Basic landscaping (mulching, planting, edging)
- Pressure washing (rent from Home Depot, $50-100/day vs. $300+ professional)
- Cabinet hardware replacement
- Minor drywall patches

### Consider DIY (Moderate Skill Required)
- Tile backsplash installation
- Laminate/LVP flooring installation (click-lock)
- Deck staining
- Basic electrical (outlets, switches — with permits where required)
- Fence repair
- Appliance installation (dishwasher, garbage disposal)

### Always Hire a Professional
- Structural work (load-bearing walls, foundations)
- Main electrical panel work
- Gas line work
- Roofing (safety + warranty issues)
- Plumbing rough-in (within walls)
- HVAC system installation
- Asbestos/lead abatement
- Foundation repair
- Tree removal (large trees near structures)
- Any work requiring engineering stamps or complex permits

## HOME WARRANTY vs. EMERGENCY FUND

### Home Warranties (Generally Not Worth It)
- **Cost**: $400-700/year + $75-125 service call fee each time
- **Coverage**: Pre-existing conditions often excluded. Caps on payouts. Replacement with equivalent (not identical) equipment.
- **Reality**: Most claims result in partial coverage, delays, and contractor quality issues
- **Better alternative**: Self-insure. Put $500-1,000/year into a dedicated home repair savings fund. Over 5 years, you'll have $2,500-5,000 available without claim restrictions.

### The Exception
Home warranties can make sense on purchase of older homes (15+ years) with aging major systems. Negotiate for the seller to pay for the first year of coverage.

## GOLDEN RULES: HOME SERVICES

1. **Always get 3 bids.** Minimum. More for jobs over $10,000.
2. **Verify license and insurance** before any work begins. No exceptions.
3. **Written contract for everything** over $500. Scope, timeline, materials, payment schedule, warranty.
4. **Never pay more than 30% upfront.** Hold final 10-20% until completion.
5. **Schedule in the off-season** for 10-20% savings and better service.
6. **DIY interior painting** — it's the highest-return DIY skill for homeowners.
7. **Independent mechanics over chains** for non-warranty auto work. Same for plumbers, electricians over national chains.
8. **YouTube is a valid trade school** for basic home repairs. Watch 3 tutorials before starting any DIY project.
9. **Keep a maintenance calendar**: HVAC filter monthly, dryer vent annually, water heater flush annually, gutters twice yearly.
10. **Emergency fund beats home warranty.** Self-insure with a dedicated repair savings account.
