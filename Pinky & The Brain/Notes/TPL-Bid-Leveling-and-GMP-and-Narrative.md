# TPL-Estimate-Narrative — How to Write a Professional Estimate Narrative

> An estimate without a narrative is a table of numbers. A narrative transforms it into a professional deliverable that builds client confidence and protects the estimator.

---

## Estimate Narrative Structure

### Section 1: Project Overview (2–4 sentences)
State the project, location, scope, and basis for the estimate.

> *"This estimate covers the tenant improvement build-out of a 2,800 SF Chick-fil-A end-cap location at [Address], [City, State]. The estimate is based on schematic design drawings issued [date] and reflects a warm dark shell delivery condition. This is a Design Development-phase estimate with an accuracy range of ±15–20%."*

### Section 2: Scope Inclusions (bullet list or paragraph)
Clearly state what IS included.

> *"The estimate includes all interior construction, MEP systems, drive-thru infrastructure, finishes, and general conditions. It is based on the brand's current prototype specifications."*

### Section 3: Scope Exclusions (bullet list — always use)
Every exclusion protects the estimator from "but I thought that was included."

> *Exclusions from this estimate:*
> *- Owner-furnished equipment (kitchen equipment package, FF&E)*
> *- Exterior signage and sign infrastructure*
> *- Utility connection and development fees*
> *- Permit fees*
> *- Architect/engineer fees*
> *- Technology package (POS, drive-thru communication hardware)*

### Section 4: Key Assumptions (numbered list)
These are the decisions baked into the number. If any assumption is wrong, the number changes.

> *Assumptions:*
> *1. Warm dark shell delivery — HVAC stub-in (2 × 5-ton RTUs), 400A electrical service, plumbing stub-ins at rear wall*
> *2. National average regional cost index applied; Nashville multiplier = 0.95*
> *3. Single drive-thru lane*
> *4. Brand standard finish package (mid-tier)*
> *5. Escalation: 4% annual rate applied for 10 months to construction midpoint*
> *6. Construction start: [date]; duration: 5 months*

### Section 5: Risk Flags (what could change the number)
Be transparent. This builds trust and manages expectations.

> *The following items carry cost uncertainty and should be monitored as design progresses:*
> *- Electrical service upgrade: if existing utility infrastructure cannot support 400A service, a transformer upgrade could add $30,000–$80,000*
> *- Slab conditions: existing slab thickness and reinforcement are unknown; budget assumes standard 4" slab adequate for kitchen loads*
> *- Grease interceptor: AHJ jurisdiction requirement unknown; in-ground interceptor adds $15,000–$35,000 if required*

### Section 6: Accuracy Statement (always include)
> *"This estimate is prepared at the Design Development phase and carries an accuracy range of ±15–20%, reflecting the current state of design documentation. The estimate range is $[low] to $[high]. Further refinement is expected at 100% construction documents."*

### Section 7: Next Steps / Recommended Actions
> *"To improve estimate accuracy, we recommend: (1) Existing conditions survey prior to permit; (2) Utility capacity confirmation letters; (3) AHJ pre-application meeting to confirm grease interceptor requirement."*
