# Project Delivery Methods

> Cross-cutting reference for [[Construction Program Management - Master Reference]]

---

## Overview

The delivery method defines how the owner structures the contractual relationships between design and construction teams. It determines risk allocation, timeline flexibility, and the owner's level of control.

---

## The Six Primary Methods

### 1. Design-Bid-Build (DBB)
**The traditional method.** Three sequential phases: design, bid, build.

- **Contracts:** Owner → Architect (design); Owner → GC (construction). Two separate contracts.
- **AIA Forms:** A101 (Owner-Contractor, Stipulated Sum), B101 (Owner-Architect)
- **Process:** Design 100% → competitive bid → award to lowest qualified bidder → build
- **Risk allocation:** Design risk on architect; construction risk on GC; cost risk largely on GC (fixed price)
- **Owner control:** HIGH over design; LOW during construction
- **Best for:** Projects with well-defined scope, where cost competition is valued, public projects requiring competitive bidding
- **Drawbacks:** Longest timeline (sequential); adversarial designer/builder relationship; change orders common from coordination gaps; no contractor input during design

### 2. Design-Build (DB)
**Single point of responsibility.** One entity handles both design and construction.

- **Contracts:** Owner → Design-Builder (single contract)
- **AIA Forms:** A141 (Owner-Design-Builder)
- **Process:** Owner develops criteria → selects DB team → concurrent design and build
- **Risk allocation:** Design AND construction risk on design-builder
- **Owner control:** LOWER over detailed design; HIGHER over schedule
- **Best for:** Fast-track projects, simple/repetitive projects, owners who want single accountability
- **Drawbacks:** Reduced design control for owner; harder to compare bids (apples-to-oranges); potential quality compromise if cost is prioritized

### 3. Construction Manager at Risk (CMAR / CM/GC)
**Early contractor involvement with guaranteed price.** CM acts as consultant during design, then as builder during construction.

- **Contracts:** Owner → Architect (design); Owner → CM (preconstruction + construction)
- **AIA Forms:** A102 (Cost Plus with GMP), A133 (CM as Constructor with GMP)
- **Process:** CM hired during design → provides cost/schedule input → establishes GMP at ~70-90% CDs → bids trade packages → builds
- **Risk allocation:** GMP risk on CM; savings may be shared
- **Owner control:** HIGH (involved in trade bidding, open-book pricing)
- **Best for:** Complex projects where early builder input adds value; owners wanting cost transparency
- **Drawbacks:** Higher preconstruction costs; GMP disputes over what's "in" vs. "out"; CM may pad GMP

### 4. Construction Management Multi-Prime (CMMP)
**Owner acts as GC.** Holds individual contracts with each trade contractor.

- **Contracts:** Owner → each trade contractor (multiple prime contracts)
- **Process:** Owner (or agency CM) manages all coordination between trades
- **Risk allocation:** HIGH coordination risk on owner; trade risk on each contractor
- **Owner control:** MAXIMUM (but also maximum responsibility)
- **Best for:** Experienced owners with in-house CM capability; some public sector requirements
- **Drawbacks:** Extremely management-intensive; gaps between scopes; difficult dispute resolution

### 5. Integrated Project Delivery (IPD)
**Multi-party collaboration.** All key parties under one agreement with shared risk and reward.

- **Contracts:** Single multi-party agreement (Owner + Architect + Contractor minimum)
- **AIA Forms:** C191 (Multi-Party Agreement for IPD)
- **Process:** All parties selected pre-design → collaborative design → shared target cost → build
- **Risk allocation:** SHARED across all parties; profit pool tied to performance
- **Owner control:** Collaborative (shared decision-making)
- **Best for:** Highly complex projects; innovation-driven projects; parties with trust and collaboration experience
- **Drawbacks:** New and unfamiliar to many; complex contract setup; requires cultural shift; not suitable if parties can't collaborate

### 6. Public-Private Partnership (P3)
**Private entity finances, builds, and often operates.** Used primarily for public infrastructure.

- **Contracts:** Long-term concession or availability payment agreement
- **Process:** Public entity defines need → private consortium proposes, finances, designs, builds, operates
- **Risk allocation:** Significant risk transferred to private partner
- **Best for:** Large infrastructure with long operating horizons (highways, transit, utilities)
- **Drawbacks:** Complex procurement; lengthy negotiation; political risk; requires sophisticated legal/financial structuring

## Decision Guide for the Agent

When advising on delivery method selection, consider:

1. **Timeline pressure?** → DB or CMAR (allow design/construction overlap)
2. **Maximum design control?** → DBB or CMAR
3. **Single point of accountability?** → DB
4. **Complex project with unknowns?** → CMAR or IPD
5. **Public project with bidding requirements?** → DBB (most states) or CMAR (increasing adoption)
6. **Experienced owner with in-house team?** → CMMP may work
7. **Innovation and collaboration valued?** → IPD
8. **Tight budget, simple project?** → DBB with competitive bidding
