## MODULE 1: HISTORY OF MUSIC — TIMELINE & ERAS

### Pre-Classical (Ancient – 1400s)
Music as ritual, prayer, and storytelling. Gregorian chant dominates the Western world. Monophonic — single melody, no harmony. The lute, lyre, and early percussion are primary instruments. Music is oral, passed down and performed live. No notation until the 11th century (Guido d'Arezzo invents staff notation ~1025).

### Renaissance (1400–1600)
Polyphony emerges — multiple independent melody lines. Sacred music gives way to secular songs. Madrigals become popular. Key composers: Josquin des Prez, Palestrina, Thomas Tallis. The printing press (1450s) accelerates music distribution. Instruments expand: lute, harpsichord, early violin family.

### Baroque (1600–1750)
Drama, ornamentation, and emotional intensity. Birth of opera (Florence, 1600s). Key figures: Bach, Handel, Vivaldi, Monteverdi. Counterpoint reaches its apex in Bach's fugues. The harpsichord rules. Tempo marking systems introduced. Music serves courts, churches, and aristocracy.

### Classical Period (1750–1820)
Clean structure, balance, clarity. Sonata form becomes the architectural foundation of Western composition. Key figures: Haydn, Mozart, early Beethoven. The piano replaces the harpsichord. Symphonies and string quartets dominate. Public concerts emerge — music moves from courts to concert halls.

### Romantic Period (1820–1900)
Emotion, nationalism, and individualism explode. Orchestras grow massive. Music becomes a vehicle for storytelling (program music) and national identity. Key figures: Beethoven (late), Schubert, Chopin, Brahms, Wagner, Tchaikovsky, Liszt, Mahler. The virtuoso performer becomes a celebrity. Opera evolves through Wagner's total artwork concept (Gesamtkunstwerk).

### 20th Century Classical / Modernism (1900–1960)
The rules shatter. Debussy introduces impressionism. Stravinsky shocks Paris with The Rite of Spring (1913). Schoenberg abandons tonality (12-tone serialism). Bartok integrates folk music. Shostakovich composes under Soviet oppression. American minimalists (John Cage, Steve Reich) later challenge what music even is.

### American Roots & The Blues (1860s–1930s)
The foundation of virtually all modern popular music. Born from the intersection of African rhythmic tradition, work songs, field hollers, and European harmonic structure. The blues scale — flattened 3rd, 5th, and 7th — becomes the engine of Western pop music for the next century. Key pioneers: Robert Johnson, Bessie Smith, Leadbelly, Ma Rainey. The 12-bar blues progression underpins rock, jazz, soul, and hip-hop. Delta blues (raw, rural) vs. Chicago blues (electric, urban) — both essential lineages.

### Jazz (1890s–Present)
Born in New Orleans from blues, ragtime, and gospel. The most theoretically sophisticated popular music ever created. Evolves in distinct waves:

- **Dixieland / Traditional (1900–1920s):** Louis Armstrong, Jelly Roll Morton
- **Swing / Big Band (1930s–40s):** Duke Ellington, Count Basie, Benny Goodman — jazz as America's dance music
- **Bebop (1940s):** Charlie Parker, Dizzy Gillespie — virtuosic, fast, cerebral, deliberately un-danceable
- **Cool Jazz (1950s):** Miles Davis (Birth of the Cool), Dave Brubeck — laid-back, sophisticated
- **Hard Bop (1950s):** Art Blakey, Horace Silver — reconnects jazz to blues and gospel
- **Modal Jazz (late 1950s–60s):** Miles Davis (Kind of Blue) — scales over chord changes
- **Free Jazz (1960s):** Ornette Coleman, Coltrane (A Love Supreme) — abandons chord structures entirely
- **Fusion (1970s):** Miles Davis (Bitches Brew), Weather Report, Mahavishnu Orchestra — jazz meets rock and funk
- **Contemporary / Neo-Soul Jazz (1980s–present):** Herbie Hancock, Wynton Marsalis, Kamasi Washington, Nubya Garcia

### Country & Folk (1920s–Present)
Country crystallizes in the American South from Appalachian folk, Irish/Scottish ballads, and blues. The Carter Family and Jimmie Rodgers are ground zero (1927). Key eras:

- **Classic Country (1940s–60s):** Hank Williams, Patsy Cline, Johnny Cash, Loretta Lynn
- **Outlaw Country (1970s):** Willie Nelson, Waylon Jennings, Kris Kristofferson — rejecting Nashville polish
- **New Traditionalist (1980s):** George Strait, Randy Travis — back to roots
- **Alt-Country / Americana (1990s–present):** Uncle Tupelo, Wilco, Ryan Adams, Jason Isbell
- **Modern Country-Pop:** Early Taylor Swift, Luke Combs, Kacey Musgraves

Folk diverges into: Pete Seeger and Woody Guthrie (1940s–50s labor/political folk); the Greenwich Village revival with Dylan, Baez, Phil Ochs (1960s); freak folk of Devendra Banhart and Joanna Newsom (2000s).

### Rock & Roll (1950s–Present)
Born from the collision of blues, gospel, and country. Little Richard, Chuck Berry, Bo Diddley, Fats Domino, Elvis Presley — the founding generation (1954–1959). The electric guitar becomes the defining instrument of modern culture.

- **New Wave (1978–1986):** Talking Heads, Elvis Costello, The Police, Devo

- **Heavy Metal (1980s):** Metallica, Iron Maiden, Judas Priest, Slayer

## MODULE 2: MUSIC THEORY FOR THE DJ

### The Basics

**Pitch:** How high or low a note sounds. Determined by frequency (Hz).

**Scale:** A sequence of notes in a defined interval pattern.
- Major scale: W-W-H-W-W-W-H (happy, resolved, bright)
- Minor scale (natural): W-H-W-W-H-W-W (sad, dark, tense)
- Pentatonic scale: 5-note scale — backbone of blues, rock, and folk
- Blues scale: Pentatonic + the blue note (flattened 5th) = aching tension

**Key:** The tonal center of a piece. C major = C is home. Songs can modulate (change keys) for dramatic effect.

**Chord:** Three or more notes played simultaneously.
- Major chord: Root + major 3rd + perfect 5th (bright)
- Minor chord: Root + minor 3rd + perfect 5th (dark)
- Dominant 7th: Major chord + flattened 7th = bluesy tension that wants to resolve
- Suspended chords (sus2, sus4): Ambiguous, floating — used for tension

**The I-IV-V Progression:** The most used chord progression in Western music. In C major: C-F-G.

**The I-V-vi-IV Progression:** C-G-Am-F. Powers thousands of pop songs.

**The 12-Bar Blues:**
```
I - I - I - I
IV - IV - I - I
V - IV - I - V
```

**Modes:** Variations of the major scale:
- Ionian (standard major): bright, resolved
- Dorian: minor feel with raised 6th — jazz, funk
- Phrygian: Spanish, flamenco, dark metal
- Lydian: floating, dreamy — film scores
- Mixolydian: major scale with flat 7th — rock anthems
- Aeolian: natural minor — dark, melancholic
- Locrian: dissonant, unstable — rare

### Rhythm & Feel

**Meter:** 4/4 = universal pop/rock/electronic. 3/4 = waltz. 6/8 = rolling feel. 5/4 = Take Five. 7/8 = Tool, Radiohead, Balkan music.

**Groove:** The feel that makes music move your body. Straight vs. swing feel. On the beat vs. behind the beat (laid-back).

**Syncopation:** Accenting off-beats or weak beats. Reggae guitar, funk rhythm, most jazz.

**The One:** James Brown's concept — emphasizing the first beat above all else.

**Polyrhythm:** Multiple rhythm patterns running simultaneously. West African drumming, Afrobeats, bossa nova, prog rock.

### Song Structure
- Verse, Pre-Chorus, Chorus, Bridge, Outro
- AABA form: Common in jazz standards (32-bar form)
- Through-composed: No repetition — classical, some prog rock

### Harmonic Concepts

**Circle of Fifths:** The map of all 12 keys and their relationships. Adjacent keys share nearly identical notes and transition smoothly.

**Tension and Resolution:** The engine of all music. The dominant chord (V) creates maximum tension and resolves to the tonic (I).

**Voice Leading:** How individual notes in chords move from one to the next. Smooth = flow. Abrupt = drama.

### Timbre and Production

**Timbre:** The color of a sound — what makes a piano sound different from a guitar on the same note.

**Dynamics:** Loud vs. soft. The emotional range of a performance.

**Frequency Spectrum:** Sub-bass (20–60Hz), bass (60–250Hz), low-mids (250–500Hz), mids (500–2000Hz), upper-mids (2–6kHz), highs (6–20kHz).

## MODULE 6: ARTIST DNA PROFILES

### The Black Keys
**Genre:** Blues-rock, garage rock
**DNA:** Ohio two-piece (Dan Auerbach + Patrick Carney). Pure Delta blues filtered through garage rock minimalism. Dirty fuzz guitar, pounding drums, soulful vocals. Heavy Howlin' Wolf and Robert Johnson influence. No bass player — rhythm is everything.
**Top Tracks:** "Lonely Boy," "Tighten Up," "Howlin' for You," "Little Black Submarines," "Gold on the Ceiling," "I Got Mine"
**Start Here:** Brothers (2010) or El Camino (2011)
**Similar Artists:** The White Stripes, Jack White, Gary Clark Jr., JJ Grey & Mofro, Black Joe Lewis & The Honeybears, Vintage Trouble, Seasick Steve, Rival Sons, Blues Pills, Larkin Poe, Marcus King Band, The Heavy, Jon Spencer Blues Explosion

---

### Led Zeppelin
**Genre:** Hard rock, blues rock, folk rock, heavy metal
**DNA:** Architects of rock maximalism. Page's guitar explodes Delta blues with volume. Bonham's drums are the most copied in rock history. Plant's voice is an instrument of excess. Jones is the quiet foundation of genius.
**Top Tracks:** "Whole Lotta Love," "Stairway to Heaven," "Kashmir," "Black Dog," "Immigrant Song," "When the Levee Breaks," "Ramble On"
**Start Here:** Led Zeppelin IV (1971)
**Similar Artists:** Black Sabbath, Deep Purple, Aerosmith, Rival Sons, Greta Van Fleet, Wolfmother, The Sword, Blues Pills

---

### Radiohead
**Genre:** Alternative rock, art rock, post-rock, electronic
**DNA:** Oxford's finest. The defining trajectory of a band that refuses genre. Pablo Honey = grunge-adjacent. The Bends = anthemic alt-rock. OK Computer = paranoid tech-age masterpiece. Kid A = avant-garde electronic rock. Thom Yorke's falsetto is unmistakable. Jonny Greenwood's orchestral textures are the secret weapon.
**Top Tracks:** "Karma Police," "Fake Plastic Trees," "No Surprises," "Paranoid Android," "Everything in Its Right Place," "How to Disappear Completely," "Exit Music (For a Film)"
**Start Here:** OK Computer (1997)
**Similar Artists:** Portishead, Massive Attack, Sigur Ros, Bjork, The National, Doves, Elbow, Thom Yorke (solo)

---

### Bob Dylan
**Genre:** Folk, rock, country, blues — above all, singer-songwriter
**DNA:** The most important figure in popular songwriting of the 20th century. Fused Woody Guthrie folk tradition with Beat poetry and blues. Wrote the rulebook for topical, literary songwriting. Never stayed in one place.
**Top Tracks:** "Blowin' in the Wind," "Mr. Tambourine Man," "Like a Rolling Stone," "The Times They Are A-Changin'," "Tangled Up in Blue," "Knockin' on Heaven's Door"
**Start Here:** Blood on the Tracks (1975) or Highway 61 Revisited (1965)
**Similar Artists:** Phil Ochs, Joni Mitchell, Neil Young, Leonard Cohen, Paul Simon, Bruce Springsteen, Jason Isbell

## MODULE 8: PLAYLIST CONSTRUCTION METHODOLOGY

### The 90-Minute Set Arc

1. **Opening (0–10 min):** Establish the vibe. Slower, atmospheric, hooks without overwhelming.
2. **Rising Action (10–35 min):** Introduce energy, build tension, vary textures.
3. **First Peak (35–45 min):** Most anthemic or emotionally intense track.
4. **Valley (45–55 min):** Purposeful step down. Creates contrast, respects the ear.
5. **Second Build (55–70 min):** New angle on the theme. Genre detour or deeper cut.
6. **Final Climax (70–80 min):** The gut-punch. The track that defines the set.
7. **Outro (80–90 min):** Resolution. Lands the listener softly.

### Key Sequencing Principles
- BPM adjacency: Don't jump from 75 BPM to 145 BPM without a bridge
- Key compatibility: Adjacent keys on the circle of fifths transition smoothly
- Energy arc: Every track has a purpose in the narrative
- Surprise factor: Every set needs one unexpected inclusion
- Callback: A late track that echoes an early one creates cohesion

## MODULE 14: SPOTIFY INTEGRATION LAYER

*What this module enables and how it connects to Spin's intelligence.*

### Security Policy: Command Execution

**CRITICAL RULE:** Spin cannot execute shell commands directly. All Spotify playback commands must be requested through Ziggy using the secured wrapper script.

**Execution Protocol:**
1. When Nathan asks to play music, Spin prepares the command: `~/.local/bin/ziggy-spotify-wrapper play "<search>" [--album|--track|--podcast] --device "<device>"`
2. Spin asks Ziggy: "Please execute this Spotify command for Nathan: [command]"
3. Ziggy validates the request and executes via the wrapper
4. The wrapper (`ziggy-spotify-wrapper`) only allows `ziggy-spotify`, `spogo`, and `spotify_player` commands — all other commands are rejected

**Why This Matters:** The wrapper script is the security boundary. It ensures only Spotify-related commands execute, preventing any accidental or malicious command injection.

### What Spotify Integration Unlocks
- Spin generates a playlist → it gets created in your Spotify library automatically
- Spin can queue the next track without you lifting a finger
- Liked/skipped feedback can be pulled directly from Spotify listening history
- BPM, key, and audio features can be confirmed via Spotify's audio analysis API
- Spin can search Spotify to verify a track exists before recommending it

### Spotify API Capabilities Spin Uses
- `POST /v1/users/{user_id}/playlists` — Create a new playlist
- `POST /v1/playlists/{playlist_id}/tracks` — Add tracks to a playlist
- `GET /v1/search` — Search for a track or artist by name
- `GET /v1/audio-features/{track_id}` — Confirm BPM (tempo), key, energy, danceability, valence
- `GET /v1/me/player/recently-played` — Pull recent listening history for taste learning
- `PUT /v1/me/player/play` — Start playback (requires Spotify Premium)
- `POST /v1/me/player/queue` — Add to queue (requires Spotify Premium)
- `GET /v1/recommendations` — Pull Spotify's own recommendations seeded by artists/tracks/genres

### Audio Feature Reference (Spotify's Audio Analysis Fields)
| Field | What It Means | Range |
|---|---|---|
| tempo | BPM | 0–250 |
| energy | Intensity and activity | 0.0–1.0 |
| valence | Musical positiveness (happy vs. sad) | 0.0–1.0 |
| danceability | How suitable for dancing | 0.0–1.0 |
| acousticness | How acoustic vs. electronic | 0.0–1.0 |
| instrumentalness | Likelihood of no vocals | 0.0–1.0 |
| liveness | Probability of live recording | 0.0–1.0 |
| loudness | Overall loudness in dB | -60 to 0 |
| speechiness | Presence of spoken word | 0.0–1.0 |
| key | Musical key (0=C, 1=C#, 2=D, etc.) | 0–11 |
| mode | Major (1) or minor (0) | 0 or 1 |

### How Spin Uses Audio Features for Taste Learning
When a track is flagged as liked, Spin notes its audio features. Over time, patterns emerge:
- User consistently likes tracks with energy > 0.7 and valence < 0.4 = high-energy but emotionally dark
- User skips tracks with acousticness > 0.8 = avoids acoustic-only recordings
- User likes danceability > 0.65 in evening sessions = wants to move at night

These patterns feed directly into Module 12's Taste Profile.
