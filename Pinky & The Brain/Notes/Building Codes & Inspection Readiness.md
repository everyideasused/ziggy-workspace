# Building Codes & Inspection Readiness

> **Purpose:** The regulatory framework that governs how buildings are constructed — what codes apply, how inspections work, what triggers them, and what inspectors are actually looking for. Understanding codes transforms a builder from someone who "does it the way I was taught" to someone who can defend their work, anticipate requirements, and pass inspections on the first call. For a PM, code literacy means catching non-compliant work before the inspector does.

---

## The Code Framework

### Model Codes

Building codes are developed by national organizations and adopted (with local amendments) by state and local jurisdictions. The major model codes:

**International Code Council (ICC) family:**
- **IBC (International Building Code)** — Commercial and multi-family construction (3+ units, non-residential)
- **IRC (International Residential Code)** — One- and two-family dwellings and townhouses up to 3 stories. This is the primary code for residential builders.
- **IMC (International Mechanical Code)** — HVAC systems
- **IPC (International Plumbing Code)** — Plumbing systems
- **IECC (International Energy Conservation Code)** — Energy efficiency requirements (insulation, air sealing, HVAC efficiency, window performance)
- **NEC (National Electrical Code, NFPA 70)** — Electrical systems. Technically an NFPA standard, not an ICC code, but adopted alongside them.
- **IFC (International Fire Code)** — Fire prevention, fire protection systems

### How Codes Get Adopted

The ICC publishes updated codes on a 3-year cycle (2018, 2021, 2024). States and municipalities adopt a specific edition, often with local amendments. This means the "current code" varies by jurisdiction. Nashville and Davidson County may be on a different code cycle than a rural Tennessee county.

**Always verify which code edition is adopted in the specific jurisdiction before starting design or construction.** Never assume. A call to the building department or a check of their website confirms the adopted code year and any local amendments.

### Prescriptive vs. Performance Paths

The IRC offers two compliance approaches:

**Prescriptive** — Follow the tables and rules exactly. If the code says 2x10 joists at 16" OC can span 15'-4" for a 40 psf live load in Douglas Fir #2, you use that without any engineering. Most residential construction follows the prescriptive path. It's simpler but less flexible.

**Performance / Engineered** — Hire a licensed engineer to design specific elements that deviate from the prescriptive tables. This is required when you exceed prescriptive limits (longer spans, higher loads, unusual configurations) or want to use non-standard materials or methods. The engineer stamps the drawings, and the inspector verifies that the work matches the stamped plans.

## The Inspection Process

### How Inspections Work

1. **Request the inspection** — Call or submit online, typically 24-48 hours before the work is ready. Some jurisdictions allow same-day requests.
2. **Be ready** — The work must be complete, accessible, and visible for the specific inspection being requested. Don't call for a framing inspection if sheathing isn't done or if the work area is cluttered.
3. **Inspector arrives** — They verify the work meets the approved plans and applicable code. They check specific items depending on the inspection type.
4. **Pass or fail** — If it passes, you're cleared to proceed to the next phase. If it fails, you get a correction notice listing the deficiencies. Fix them, then call for a re-inspection.
5. **Document everything** — Keep inspection records, correction notices, and sign-off dates. These matter at final inspection and for the Certificate of Occupancy.

### Standard Residential Inspection Sequence

| Inspection | When | What They're Checking |
|-----------|------|----------------------|
| **Footing** | After excavation, forms, and rebar — before concrete pour | Soil conditions, footing dimensions, rebar size/spacing/cover, form alignment |
| **Foundation** | After walls poured/laid — before backfill | Wall thickness, height, waterproofing, anchor bolt placement and spacing |
| **Under-slab plumbing** | Before slab pour | Pipe material, slope (1/4" per foot for drains), cleanout locations, pressure test |
| **Slab** | Before pour (some jurisdictions) | Vapor barrier, insulation, wire mesh/rebar placement, thickness |
| **Framing** | Walls, floors, and roof complete — before insulation or drywall | Stud spacing, headers, hold-downs, straps, shear wall nailing, blocking, fire stops |
| **Shear wall / lateral** | With framing or separately | Nailing patterns, hold-down installation, strap connections, continuous load path |
| **Plumbing rough** | Pipes installed — before concealment | DWV (drain/waste/vent) layout, pipe sizing, slope, venting, water supply layout, pressure test |
| **Mechanical rough** | Ductwork and equipment — before concealment | Duct sizing, support, return air paths, equipment clearances, combustion air, gas piping test |
| **Electrical rough** | Wiring installed — before concealment | Wire sizing, box fill, circuit layout, GFCI/AFCI protection, smoke/CO detector locations |
| **Insulation** | Installed — before drywall | R-values match code, coverage completeness, vapor retarder (if required), air sealing |
| **Energy / blower door** | After air sealing, before or after drywall | Air leakage rate (ACH50 per IECC — typically 3-5 ACH50 depending on code year and zone) |
| **Drywall nailing** | After hanging, before taping (some jurisdictions) | Fastener spacing, edge distance, proper screws/nails |
| **Final** | Everything complete | All systems operational, smoke/CO detectors working, guardrails/handrails, fixtures, egress, site grading, address visible |

### What Inspectors Actually Look For

Inspectors are checking compliance, not quality. A wall can be ugly but code-compliant. They focus on:

**Life safety items (highest priority):**
- Smoke and CO detector locations and function
- Egress windows in bedrooms (minimum opening: 5.7 sq ft, 24" min height, 20" min width, sill max 44" above floor)
- Stair dimensions and guardrails (36" min guard height at stairs, 42" at landings/balconies, 4" max baluster spacing)
- Structural connections (hold-downs, straps, anchor bolts)
- Fire-stopping (sealing penetrations through fire-rated assemblies and at floor/ceiling intersections in multi-story buildings)

**Structural items:**
- Member sizing matches plans or prescriptive code tables
- Fastener patterns (shear wall nailing is commonly failed — 3" edge nailing, 6" field nailing with specified nail size)
- Headers properly sized and supported
- Load path continuity from roof to foundation
- Bearing on foundations (studs/posts align with anchor points)

**MEP items:**
- Proper GFCI protection (kitchen countertops, bathrooms, garages, outdoors, unfinished basements, laundry — within 6' of water source)
- AFCI protection (bedrooms — most code editions now require it in most habitable rooms)
- Plumbing venting (every fixture needs a vent; the code defines maximum distances and methods)
- HVAC combustion air (gas appliances need adequate air supply)
- Clearances (electrical panels need 36" clear in front, 30" wide; water heaters need clearance per manufacturer)

## Common Code Requirements Every Builder Should Know

### Egress (Getting Out)

Every sleeping room must have at least one operable emergency escape window or door:
- Minimum net clear opening: 5.7 sq ft (5.0 sq ft at grade level)
- Minimum height: 24"
- Minimum width: 20"
- Maximum sill height: 44" above finished floor
- Must open without keys, tools, or special knowledge

Basements used as habitable space need an egress window or door. If below grade, an egress window well is required (minimum 9 sq ft area, 36" minimum dimension).

### Guardrails and Handrails

**Guardrails** (fall protection at edges):
- Required where the walking surface is 30" or more above the grade below
- Minimum height: 36" at stairs, 42" at porches/decks/balconies (check local amendments — some jurisdictions require 42" everywhere)
- Maximum 4" opening between balusters (the 4" sphere rule)
- Must resist 200 lb concentrated load at the top rail

**Handrails** (graspable rail at stairs):
- Required on at least one side of every stairway with 4 or more risers
- Height: 34" to 38" measured from the stair nosing
- Must be graspable — circular cross section 1-1/4" to 2" diameter, or non-circular with a graspable finger recess
- Must be continuous from top to bottom (returns at ends into the wall or newel post)
- Must extend horizontally at least 12" beyond the top riser and the depth of one tread beyond the bottom riser

### Stair Dimensions

- Maximum riser height: 7-3/4"
- Minimum tread depth: 10" (measured from nosing to nosing)
- Maximum variation between any two risers or treads: 3/8"
- Minimum width: 36" (measured above the handrail)
- Minimum headroom: 6'-8" (measured from the sloped plane of the nosings)

### Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Detectors

**Smoke detectors:**
- Required in every sleeping room
- Required outside each sleeping area (in the immediate hallway)
- Required on every level of the dwelling (including basement)
- Must be interconnected (when one sounds, all sound) — hardwired with battery backup in new construction

**CO detectors:**
- Required outside each sleeping area in dwellings with fuel-burning appliances or attached garages
- Interconnected with smoke detectors in new construction

### Electrical Basics

**Receptacle spacing (habitable rooms):** No point along any wall should be more than 6' from a receptacle. This means receptacles at maximum 12' apart along any wall, and any wall section 2' or wider gets a receptacle.

**Kitchen countertop receptacles:** Every countertop space wider than 12" gets a receptacle. Maximum 24" from the end of the countertop to a receptacle. Maximum 48" between receptacles. All must be GFCI-protected.

**Bathroom receptacles:** At least one within 36" of the edge of each lavatory basin. Must be GFCI-protected.

**Exterior receptacles:** At least one at the front and one at the back of the dwelling. Must be GFCI-protected and weatherproof (in-use cover required).

**Garage receptacles:** At least one. Must be GFCI-protected.

## Permit Triggers — When Do You Need a Permit?

General rule: any work that involves structural changes, MEP systems, or changes to the building envelope requires a permit. Specifics vary by jurisdiction, but common thresholds:

**Almost always requires a permit:**
- New construction (any building)
- Additions (adding square footage)
- Structural modifications (removing walls, adding openings, beam installation)
- Electrical work (new circuits, panel upgrades, service changes)
- Plumbing work (new fixtures, re-piping, water heater replacement in some jurisdictions)
- HVAC installation or replacement
- Roofing replacement (some jurisdictions — check local rules)
- Deck construction (especially if attached to the house)
- Fence over a certain height (typically 6'-7')
- Retaining walls over 4' in height

**Typically does NOT require a permit:**
- Cosmetic changes (paint, wallpaper, flooring, trim)
- Cabinet and countertop replacement (same footprint)
- Like-for-like fixture replacement (replacing a toilet, faucet, or light fixture without changing the rough plumbing or wiring)
- Minor repairs (patching drywall, replacing a broken window pane)
- Landscaping (unless grading affects drainage or you're near a waterway)

**When in doubt, call the building department.** An unpermitted structural change can create major problems when selling the property — title companies and home inspectors flag additions and modifications that don't match the permit record.

## Inspection Readiness Checklist

Before calling for any inspection, verify:

- [ ] Work matches the approved plans (or approved plan revisions)
- [ ] Work area is accessible (cleared of debris, ladders available for overhead access)
- [ ] All relevant components are visible (not covered by insulation, drywall, or other trades' work)
- [ ] Permit card is posted and visible on site
- [ ] Approved plans are on site and available for the inspector
- [ ] Previous inspection corrections (if any) have been addressed
- [ ] Safety hazards are mitigated (fall protection in place, no exposed wiring, stable walking surfaces)
