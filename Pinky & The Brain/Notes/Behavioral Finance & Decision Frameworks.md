# Behavioral Finance & Decision Frameworks

> Cross-cutting reference for [[Personal Finance Knowledge Base]]
> **Applies to:** Every financial domain. The biggest risk to your wealth isn't the market — it's your own psychology.

---

## Why This Matters

Behavioral finance studies how psychological biases cause people to make irrational financial decisions. The field was pioneered by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, and expanded by Richard Thaler, Robert Shiller, and others. Their research consistently shows that humans are not the rational actors that classical economics assumes.

Understanding your biases doesn't eliminate them. But naming them gives you a fighting chance of catching yourself before they cost you money.

---

## The Major Biases That Cost Money

### Loss Aversion
**What it is:** Losses feel roughly 2× more painful than equivalent gains feel good. Losing $1,000 hurts more than gaining $1,000 feels rewarding.
**How it manifests:** Holding losing investments too long (hoping they'll recover), selling winners too early (locking in gains before they "disappear"), avoiding the stock market entirely because of fear of loss.
**The fix:** Automate investing. Don't check your portfolio frequently. Set rebalancing rules in advance and follow them mechanically.

### Anchoring
**What it is:** Over-relying on the first piece of information encountered. The "anchor" distorts all subsequent judgments.
**How it manifests:** Refusing to sell a stock until it returns to its purchase price. Valuing a house based on what the seller paid rather than current market value. Salary negotiations anchored to your current (low) salary.
**The fix:** Base financial decisions on current fundamentals and future expectations, not historical prices or arbitrary reference points.

### Confirmation Bias
**What it is:** Seeking information that confirms what you already believe and ignoring information that contradicts it.
**How it manifests:** Reading only bullish analysis when you own a stock. Dismissing warnings about a financial product you've already bought. Surrounding yourself with people who share your financial views.
**The fix:** Actively seek out the bear case for any investment you're considering. Ask "what would have to be true for this to be a bad idea?"

### Recency Bias
**What it is:** Weighting recent events more heavily than historical averages.
**How it manifests:** Assuming the market will keep going up because it has been. Assuming the market will keep going down because it just crashed. Chasing last year's best-performing fund.
**The fix:** Zoom out. Look at 20-30 year returns, not 1-year returns. Stick to your asset allocation regardless of recent performance.

### Herd Mentality
**What it is:** Following what everyone else is doing, assuming the crowd must be right.
**How it manifests:** Buying Bitcoin at $60,000 because "everyone's doing it." Panic selling because the news says everyone else is selling. FOMO-driven investment decisions.
**The fix:** Have a written investment plan (your Investment Policy Statement) and follow it. When the crowd is running, walk.

### Overconfidence
**What it is:** Believing your financial knowledge and forecasting ability are better than they actually are.
**How it manifests:** Day trading. Concentrated stock positions. Believing you can time the market. Ignoring diversification because "I know this company."
**The fix:** Humility. Accept that index funds beat most professional stock pickers. Your edge is time, not skill.

### Present Bias (Hyperbolic Discounting)
**What it is:** Heavily favoring immediate rewards over future rewards, even when waiting is objectively better.
**How it manifests:** Spending instead of saving. Taking Social Security at 62 instead of waiting for a larger benefit. Not contributing to retirement because "I'll start next year."
**The fix:** Automation is the antidote. Auto-enroll in 401k, auto-transfer to savings. Remove yourself from the decision.

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
**What it is:** Continuing a course of action because of past investment (time, money, effort) rather than future expected value.
**How it manifests:** Holding a bad investment because "I've already lost so much." Staying in a bad financial product because of the fees already paid. Continuing to pour money into a failing business.
**The fix:** Only consider future costs and benefits. What's already spent is gone — the only question is "from here, what's the best move?"

### Mental Accounting
**What it is:** Treating money differently based on its source or intended use, rather than recognizing that all money is fungible.
**How it manifests:** Treating a tax refund as "free money" and spending it frivolously (it was your money all along). Keeping a savings account earning 2% while carrying credit card debt at 20%. Refusing to use an emergency fund for an actual emergency because "that's for emergencies."
**The fix:** Money is money. A dollar is a dollar regardless of where it came from. Always optimize the net position.

## Decision Frameworks

### The Pre-Commitment Strategy
Make financial decisions in advance, during calm moments, and bind your future self to them:
- Set your 401k contribution rate once a year and don't touch it
- Write your target asset allocation down and review it annually, not during market swings
- Automate everything possible
- Create "if-then" rules: "If the market drops 20%, I will buy more, not sell"

### The 10-10-10 Framework
For major financial decisions, ask:
- How will I feel about this in **10 minutes?** (Impulse)
- How will I feel about this in **10 months?** (Medium-term impact)
- How will I feel about this in **10 years?** (Long-term consequence)

Most impulsive spending fails the 10-month test. Most panic selling fails the 10-year test.

### The Regret Minimization Framework
Ask: "When I'm 80 and looking back, which choice would I regret more?"
- Not starting investing until I knew enough? Or starting imperfectly but early?
- Not having an emergency fund when I needed one? Or missing out on some stock market gains?
- Paying off the mortgage early for peace of mind? Or investing the difference for potentially higher returns?

### The Opportunity Cost Check
Every dollar has exactly one best use. Before any purchase or allocation:
- What else could this money do?
- What would this money be worth in 20 years if invested instead?
- $100 spent today at 8% return = ~$466 in 20 years. Is this worth $466?

This is not about guilt — it's about intentionality. Some spending is absolutely worth the opportunity cost. But be conscious of the trade-off.

## Stoic Finance Principles

Given the Stoic philosophy influence in this vault (Marcus Aurelius), here are Stoic-aligned financial principles:

**Dichotomy of control:** You control your savings rate, asset allocation, and spending. You do not control the market, interest rates, or the economy. Focus exclusively on what you control.

**Negative visualization (premeditatio malorum):** Imagine losing your job, a market crash, a major medical expense. Then build systems to handle them (emergency fund, insurance, diversification). This is not pessimism — it's preparation.

**Temperance:** Enough is enough. The hedonic treadmill applies to financial goals too. Define "enough" in concrete numbers (net worth target, retirement number, lifestyle cost) and recognize when you've arrived.

**Present over future anxiety:** Plan for the future, but don't sacrifice all present enjoyment. The goal is a life well-lived, not maximum net worth at death.

## The Advisor's Behavioral Toolkit

When the agent recognizes a behavioral bias in a question, it should:
1. **Name the bias** (gently, not condescendingly)
2. **Acknowledge the emotion** behind it — it's natural, not stupid
3. **Present the math** alongside the psychology
4. **Offer both paths** — the mathematically optimal one and the psychologically comfortable one
5. **Respect the choice** — sometimes paying more for peace of mind is the right answer
