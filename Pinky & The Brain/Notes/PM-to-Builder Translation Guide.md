# PM-to-Builder Translation Guide

> **Purpose:** The bridge between the Construction PM Knowledge Base (management layer) and the Carpenter/GC Knowledge Base (craft layer). A PM who understands what's being built makes better decisions about schedule, budget, and risk. A builder who understands the PM's concerns communicates more effectively about problems and progress. This note translates between the two worlds — what to look for on site walks, what questions to ask, and how to interpret what you're seeing through both lenses.

---

## The Two Languages

PMs and builders often talk past each other because they're optimizing for different things:

| PM's World | Builder's World |
|-----------|----------------|
| Schedule milestones and critical path | Work sequence and trade readiness |
| Budget variance and cost-to-complete | Material costs and labor productivity |
| Stakeholder communication and reporting | Getting the work done right and fast |
| Risk registers and mitigation plans | "I know what can go wrong because I've seen it" |
| Document management (RFIs, submittals, ASIs) | "Just tell me what you want built" |
| Percentage complete | Physical progress you can see and touch |

Neither perspective is wrong. The best projects happen when both sides understand each other.

## Site Walk Guide for PMs

### What to Look For (Beyond Schedule Progress)

Most PM site walks focus on schedule — is framing complete, are mechanicals started. That's necessary but insufficient. Here's what to add:

**Structural Integrity Indicators:**
- Are walls plumb? Carry a small torpedo level or use your phone's level app. Check random walls by holding the level against the stud face. More than 1/4" off plumb in 8' is a problem.
- Are floor joists crowned consistently? Walk the subfloor — does it feel flat, or are there bouncy spots and dips? Inconsistent crowning is the cause.
- Are the right connections installed? Look for hold-downs at shear wall ends, hurricane ties at the roof-to-wall connection, and strap ties at multi-story wall-to-wall connections. If the engineering calls for them and they're absent, that's a critical finding.
- Headers over openings — are they there? In bearing walls, every opening needs a header. In non-bearing walls, a flat 2x4 is sufficient. If a bearing wall opening has no header, stop and flag it immediately.

**Moisture Management Indicators:**
- Is the WRB (housewrap) lapped correctly? Upper courses should overlap lower courses. Check at horizontal seams — reverse lapping allows water behind the barrier.
- Window flashing — can you see sill flashing extending out beneath the window unit? If windows are installed and you see only caulk (no metal or membrane flashing), that's a risk.
- Kick-out flashing at roof-to-wall intersections — the single most commonly missed flashing detail. Look where a roof slope terminates against a wall. There should be a small metal diverter directing water away from the wall. If it's missing, flag it.
- Site grading — is the ground sloping away from the foundation on all sides? Minimum 6" drop in the first 10' from the foundation. This prevents the most common source of basement and crawlspace water problems.

**Quality of Care Indicators:**
The overall quality of a GC's work is often visible in the details that aren't explicitly inspected:
- Is the site clean and organized? Debris management correlates strongly with work quality and safety culture.
- Are materials stored properly? Lumber on dunnage (off the ground), protected from rain. OSB and drywall stored flat, covered. Concrete supplies kept dry.
- Are penetrations sealed as they're made? Or is sealing deferred to "later" (which often means "never")?
- Is the framing layout consistent? Random stud spacing, studs not landing under panel edges, and inconsistent blocking suggest a crew that isn't following a plan.

### Questions Worth Asking the GC

**Instead of:** "Are you on schedule?"
**Ask:** "What's your critical path to dry-in, and is anything threatening it?"

**Instead of:** "Is the framing done?"
**Ask:** "Have all the engineered connections been installed? Can I see the hold-downs and shear wall nailing before sheathing goes on?"

**Instead of:** "When will mechanicals start?"
**Ask:** "Have you had a coordination meeting with the plumber, electrician, and HVAC contractor? Who routes first?"

**Instead of:** "Is this up to code?"
**Ask:** "Has the [specific inspection] been called for? What corrections came back from the last inspection?"

**Instead of:** "How's quality looking?"
**Ask:** "Can you walk me through the flashing sequence at the windows? I want to understand your approach for the envelope details."

The goal isn't to catch the GC doing something wrong — it's to demonstrate that you understand what good work looks like, which naturally raises the quality bar.

## Translating Builder Concerns into PM Language

When a builder raises an issue, they often frame it in craft terms. The PM needs to translate that into schedule, budget, and risk impact.

| Builder Says | What It Means in PM Language |
|-------------|------------------------------|
| "The soil is bad — we hit clay" | Potential foundation redesign. May need geotechnical engineer. Schedule impact: 1-3 weeks. Budget impact: $5-15K for redesign and modified foundation. |
| "The trusses don't fit" | Shop drawing error or field measurement error. RFI to architect/engineer. Truss re-fabrication: 3-6 weeks. This is a critical path delay. |
| "We need to sister these joists" | Framing was notched or bored beyond code limits (likely by MEP trades). Structural repair required. Minor cost but indicates coordination failure. |
| "The plumber and HVAC guy are fighting over the same space" | MEP coordination failure. Needs a joint meeting to resolve routing conflicts. If not resolved, expect failed rough inspections and rework. |
| "This wall is bearing — we can't just remove it" | The design intent conflicts with the structural reality. Engineer needed for beam design. Budget: $2-10K for steel beam + installation. Schedule: 2-4 weeks for engineering + fabrication. |
| "The concrete got rained on before it set" | Surface quality may be compromised. May need grinding or re-pouring the top layer. Doesn't usually affect structural integrity if the substrate is sound. |
| "We're waiting on the inspector" | Inspection scheduling is controlling the critical path. PM should contact the AHJ to understand turnaround times and potentially expedite. |
| "The windows are wrong" | Either wrong size, wrong type, or wrong swing direction. If the correct units need to be re-ordered: 4-8 weeks for custom sizes. Stock sizes: 1-2 weeks. This is why submittals exist. |

## Translating PM Requirements into Builder Language

| PM Says | What It Means to the Builder |
|---------|------------------------------|
| "The owner wants to add a window here" | New opening in a potentially bearing wall. Need to verify bearing status, size a header, provide framing labor and material. This is a change order, not a favor. |
| "We need to accelerate the schedule" | Add manpower or work overtime. Both cost money. The PM should specify which milestone they're targeting and what budget is available for acceleration. |
| "The submittal was rejected — revise and resubmit" | The proposed product or detail doesn't match the design intent. The builder needs to understand WHY it was rejected (wrong product? incomplete data? aesthetic mismatch?) to submit correctly the second time. |
| "There's a design conflict — RFI in progress" | Stop work in the affected area until the architect/engineer responds. Don't guess. Don't build it "close enough." Wait for the answer — rework costs more than waiting. |
| "We're over budget on this phase" | The PM is looking for value engineering options. The builder should identify where costs can be reduced without compromising structural integrity or code compliance. Material substitutions, simplified details, and scope reductions are all on the table — but safety never is. |
| "The owner is visiting the site tomorrow" | Clean the site, ensure safety compliance, and have a foreman available to answer questions respectfully. The owner's perception of job site organization directly affects their confidence in the project. |

## Red Flags for PMs — When to Dig Deeper

These observations should trigger further investigation:

**Structural red flags:**
- You can see daylight through the framing where you shouldn't (missing sheathing, gaps at intersections)
- Headers that look small relative to the opening they span (especially wide garage doors, large windows, or open floor plans)
- Anchor bolts that don't align with the plate (plate is offset or bolts are bent to reach)
- Shear wall panels that have been cut around openings (shear panels must be full height — you can't cut a window in a shear panel without re-engineering)

**Moisture red flags:**
- Standing water in the crawlspace or basement during construction
- No gutters installed and soil eroding against the foundation
- Caulk used in place of flashing at window and door heads (caulk is not flashing — it will fail)
- Siding installed tight to the ground (should be minimum 6" above grade to prevent moisture wicking and insect entry)

**Quality red flags:**
- GC unable to explain why something was done a certain way ("that's how we always do it" is not an answer for structural or envelope details)
- Multiple failed inspections on the same item
- Excessive material waste on site (suggests poor planning, which correlates with poor execution)
- Sub crews changing frequently (the regular crew was pulled to another job — your project is getting the B-team)

**Communication red flags:**
- GC avoids direct answers about schedule or budget status
- Inspection records are unavailable or incomplete
- Change orders submitted after work is already complete (fait accompli)
- GC dismisses PM's questions about construction details ("don't worry about it, we know what we're doing")

## The Collaborative Ideal

The best PM-builder relationships are built on mutual respect for each other's expertise:

- **The PM brings:** Schedule discipline, financial tracking, stakeholder management, documentation, risk awareness, and design intent enforcement
- **The builder brings:** Means-and-methods knowledge, trade coordination, material expertise, field problem-solving, and code compliance
- **Both contribute to:** Quality, safety, and a successful project

When a PM understands the builder's craft and a builder understands the PM's constraints, problems get solved faster, communication improves, and the project benefits.
