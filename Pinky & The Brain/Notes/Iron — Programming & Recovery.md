## THE AGENT'S PROGRAMMING WORKFLOW

Before generating any training plan, the agent must gather or infer the following:

1. **Available days this week** (1–7)
2. **Available equipment today** (KB weights available, outdoor access)
3. **Current recovery status** (sleep quality, soreness, stress)
4. **Phase** (recomp, lean bulk, cut, maintenance)
5. **Session goal** (strength, hypertrophy, conditioning, active recovery)
6. **Priority muscles for this session** (based on weekly distribution)

Only after establishing these inputs should the agent generate a session.

## SPLIT SELECTION BY AVAILABLE TRAINING DAYS

### 2 Days Per Week
**Recommended Split**: Full Body A / Full Body B

**Structure**:
- Both sessions hit every major muscle group
- Session A emphasizes different exercises than Session B
- 48+ hours between sessions

**Volume per session**: Higher (20–25 working sets total per session)

**Template**:

**Full Body A (Push + Hip Hinge Dominant)**
- KB Swing 4×15
- Push-Up variation 3×12–20
- KB RDL 3×10
- KB Press 3×8–10 per side
- Core: Dead bug 3×8/side

**Full Body B (Pull + Knee Dominant)**
- Pull-Ups 4×max (or 4×6–10 weighted)
- Goblet Squat 3×15
- KB Single-Arm Row 3×10–12 per side
- Bulgarian Split Squat 3×10 per leg
- Core: Hollow body hold 3×20–30 seconds

---

### 3 Days Per Week
**Recommended Split**: Full Body A / B / C (rotating) OR Push-Pull-Legs (PPL)

**Option 1: Full Body Rotation**
- Best for maximizing muscle frequency (each muscle group hits 3x/week)
- Each session has different primary compound movements

**Option 2: Push / Pull / Legs**
- Monday: Push (chest, shoulders, triceps)
- Wednesday: Pull (back, biceps, rear delts)
- Friday: Legs + Glutes

**Volume per session**: Moderate (15–20 sets per session)

**Recommended Split**: Upper / Lower (most common and effective)

**Monday: Upper — Push Emphasis**

**Tuesday: Lower — Glute Emphasis**

**Thursday: Upper — Pull Emphasis**
- Pull-Ups 5×max / weighted

**Friday: Lower — Quad/Strength Emphasis**
- Goblet Squat 4×15
- Reverse Lunge 3×12 per leg
- KB RDL 4×10

- Calf raises 4×20

### 5 Days Per Week
**Recommended Split**: Upper/Lower + Specialization Day OR PPL + Full Body

**Option: PPL + 2 (Push / Pull / Legs / Upper / Lower)**
- Monday: Push
- Tuesday: Pull
- Wednesday: Legs + Glutes
- Thursday: Upper (hybrid push/pull, moderate volume)
- Friday: Lower (glute specialization day)
- Saturday/Sunday: Rest or active recovery

**Note**: At 5 days, volume per session decreases but frequency increases. Each muscle group hits 2x/week minimum, glutes hit 2–3x.

---

### 6 Days Per Week
**Recommended Split**: PPL × 2 (PPL repeated twice per week)

- Monday: Push
- Tuesday: Pull
- Wednesday: Legs
- Thursday: Push (different exercises/emphasis)
- Friday: Pull (different exercises/emphasis)
- Saturday: Legs + Glutes (glute specialization)
- Sunday: Rest

**Agent caution at 6 days**: At age 44, 6 days requires excellent sleep (8+ hours), nutrition, and stress management. If any of these are suboptimal, drop to 5 days. Signs of overreaching: persistent joint soreness, declining performance, poor sleep, irritability.

## MESOCYCLE DESIGN (4–8 Week Training Block)

### Standard 6-Week Mesocycle Structure

| Week | Focus | Volume | Intensity |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Orientation / technique | Moderate (MEV) | RPE 6–7 |
| 2 | Accumulation | Building | RPE 7–8 |
| 3 | Accumulation | Higher | RPE 7–8 |
| 4 | Peak | Max volume | RPE 8–9 |
| 5 | Peak / Intensification | Lower volume, heavier | RPE 8–9 |
| 6 | Deload | 40–50% volume reduction | RPE 5–6 |

**Week 1 purpose**: Establish baseline, let connective tissue adapt, practice movement quality. Do NOT go to failure in Week 1.

**Week 4–5 peak**: This is where growth happens. Push RPE 8–9 on working sets. Get close to failure.

**Week 6 deload**: Non-negotiable. Fatigue from weeks 1–5 is masking fitness gains. The deload allows adaptation to express itself. Strength records often follow in the next mesocycle.

---

### Progressive Volume Strategy Within a Mesocycle

Start at MEV (minimum effective volume) and add sets weekly:

**Example — Lat volume progression over 6 weeks**:
- Week 1: 12 sets/week
- Week 2: 14 sets/week
- Week 3: 16 sets/week
- Week 4: 18 sets/week
- Week 5: 18–20 sets/week
- Week 6: 8–10 sets/week (deload)

**Next mesocycle**: Begin at 14 sets (slightly above previous start) and repeat the climb.

## GLUTE SPECIALIZATION PROTOCOL (Nathan Priority)

Because glute development is a primary goal, the agent should follow specialized glute programming principles:

### Glute Training Frequency
- Glutes recover relatively quickly for their size due to high mitochondrial density
- Optimal training frequency: **3x per week** (alternating between hip hinge, hip thrust, and abduction-focused sessions)
- Each session targets different movement patterns to allow partial recovery

### Three Glute Sessions Per Week Template

**Session 1: Hip Hinge (Tuesday or paired with lower A)**
- KB Swing 4×15 (explosive glute extension)
- Single-Leg RDL 3×10 per side
- KB RDL 3×12
- Clamshell warm-up 2×25

**Session 2: Hip Thrust (Friday or paired with lower B)**
- KB Hip Thrust 5×20 (primary)
- Frog Pump 3×30
- Bulgarian Split Squat 3×10 per leg
- Side-Lying Hip Abduction 3×20 per side

**Session 3: Single-Leg/Quad Emphasis with Glute Bias (Saturday or standalone)**
- Reverse Lunge 4×12 per leg (glute-biased with slight forward lean)
- Step-Up 3×15 per leg
- Donkey Kick 3×20 per side
- Glute Bridge ISO hold 3×30 seconds

### Glute Activation Protocol (Before Every Lower Session)
1. Clamshell: 2×20 per side
2. Hip Circle: 10 forward, 10 backward per leg
3. Glute bridge: 2×20 (squeeze hard at top)
4. Hip flexor stretch: 60 seconds per side

**Why this matters**: Tight hip flexors inhibit glute activation through reciprocal inhibition. Stretching the hip flexors first and then activating the glutes ensures the glutes are the primary movers in subsequent exercises, not the hip flexors or lower back.

## V-SHAPE SPECIALIZATION PROTOCOL (Nathan Priority)

### Primary Drivers of V-Shape
1. **Lat width**: Pull-ups and rows
2. **Lateral deltoid**: Lateral raises (high frequency, high volume)
3. **Rear delt**: Face pulls, rows with high elbow
4. **Waist**: Body fat reduction (nutrition) + oblique anti-rotation work (does NOT add width)

### V-Shape Weekly Volume Targets
- **Pull-ups/chin-ups**: 15–25 total reps per session, 2–3x per week
- **Rows**: 12–15 sets per week (split across back sessions)
- **Lateral raises**: 15–20 sets per week (lateral delts can tolerate high frequency — 3–4x/week)
- **Rear delt work**: 10–15 sets per week

### Lateral Raise Programming Note
The lateral deltoid responds exceptionally well to:
- High reps (15–30 per set)
- High frequency (can be trained 3–4x/week)
- Constant tension (slow, controlled, no swinging)
- Multiple angles (strict lateral, slight forward lean increases mid-delt recruitment)

**KB Lateral Raise Cue**: "Lead with the elbow, not the hand. Pinky higher than thumb at the top. Stop at shoulder height."

## RUNNING PROGRAMMING

### Running and Resistance Training Interaction
- **Interference effect**: Long-duration running (45+ minutes) performed immediately before or after strength training can blunt muscle protein synthesis
- **Agent rule**: Separate running and heavy lifting by at least 6 hours, or program them on different days
- **Exception**: Short LISS runs (20–30 min) as warm-up or cool-down have minimal interference

### Running Progression for Nathan

**Starting point**: Nathan can likely run continuously — program based on perceived effort

**Weekly running structure (concurrent with lifting)**:
- 2–3 runs per week maximum during heavy lifting phases
- 1 LISS run (easy, 25–40 minutes)
- 1 interval session (sprint intervals or tempo run)
- Optional: 1 active recovery walk/jog (15–20 min easy)

**5K Building Protocol** (if Nathan wants a running performance goal):
- Weeks 1–4: Build easy mileage (3 runs/week, 20–30 minutes each)
- Weeks 5–8: Add 1 tempo run (comfortably hard pace, 20 min)
- Weeks 9–12: Add sprint intervals (6–8 × 30 seconds hard)

## RECOVERY SCIENCE

### The Four Pillars of Recovery

#### Pillar 1: Sleep
Sleep is the most powerful anabolic and fat-loss tool available. During slow-wave sleep:
- Growth hormone secretion peaks (responsible for muscle protein synthesis and fat mobilization)
- Testosterone is produced
- Cortisol is cleared
- Neural consolidation (motor learning from training sessions is solidified)

**Nathan's sleep targets**:
- 7–9 hours of sleep per night (non-negotiable)
- Consistent sleep/wake times (circadian rhythm stability improves hormone profiles)
- Cool dark room (18–20°C / 64–68°F is optimal)
- No screens 30–60 minutes before bed

**Agent sleep protocol**: Ask about sleep quality when programming high-intensity sessions. If Nathan reports poor sleep (< 6 hours), reduce session intensity by 1 RPE point and reduce volume by 15–20%.

#### Pillar 2: Nutrition Timing (see Module 05 for full detail)
- Pre-workout: Carbohydrates + protein 1–2 hours before
- Post-workout: 30–50g protein within 2 hours (anabolic window is real but extended — not urgent)
- Daily protein distribution: 4–6 meals/snacks with 30–50g protein each is optimal for muscle protein synthesis

#### Pillar 3: Stress Management
- Chronic psychological stress elevates cortisol, which:
  - Promotes fat storage (especially abdominal)
  - Impairs muscle protein synthesis
  - Disrupts sleep
- **Agent application**: If Nathan reports high stress, shift from high-intensity training to moderate-intensity training. Reduce volume. Do not pile training stress on top of life stress.
- Recommend: 5–10 minutes of breathwork or meditation post-training (activates parasympathetic nervous system, accelerates recovery)

#### Pillar 4: Active Recovery
- Light movement on rest days increases blood flow to muscles without adding additional stress
- Promotes faster clearance of metabolic byproducts (reduces DOMS duration)
- **Options**: 20–30 min walking, yoga, light cycling, swimming, foam rolling

## SORENESS MANAGEMENT (DOMS)

### What is DOMS?
Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness — muscular discomfort that peaks 24–72 hours after exercise, particularly after eccentric loading or novel movements.

### DOMS vs. Injury — How to Tell the Difference
| DOMS | Injury Signal |
|---|---|
| Diffuse soreness throughout the muscle | Sharp, localized point pain |
| Peaks at 24–48 hours then subsides | Does not improve within 72 hours |
| Symmetric (both sides equally sore) | Asymmetric or joint-centered pain |
| Reduced by light movement | Worsened by movement |
| No swelling or bruising | Swelling, bruising, or instability |

**Agent rule**: If Nathan describes DOMS → continue training (light movement actually helps). If Nathan describes any injury signals → cease training that body part immediately and refer to rest + ice + professional assessment if persistent.

### Managing DOMS
1. **Light active recovery**: Walking or easy movement increases blood flow
2. **Hydration**: Muscles are ~75% water; dehydration worsens DOMS
3. **Protein intake**: Adequate protein accelerates repair
4. **Sleep**: Peak recovery occurs during sleep
5. **Contrast therapy**: Alternating hot/cold shower can reduce perceived soreness
6. **Foam rolling**: Light SMR (self-myofascial release) reduces tissue stiffness

## MOBILITY AND FLEXIBILITY PROGRAMMING

### Why Mobility Matters for Nathan's Goals
1. **Hip flexor mobility** → enables full glute activation (no glute gains without this)
2. **Thoracic mobility** → enables proper overhead pressing and row depth
3. **Shoulder mobility** → injury prevention in pressing and carrying movements
4. **Ankle mobility** → enables deep squatting patterns

### Mobility Routine — Daily (10 minutes, done anytime)
This can be done as a morning routine, pre-workout warm-up, or evening cool-down:

**Hip Complex**:
- Kneeling hip flexor stretch: 60s per side
- Pigeon pose: 90s per side
- Hip circles: 10 per side

**Thoracic Complex**:
- Cat-cow: 10 reps
- Thread the needle: 8 per side
- Thoracic extension over edge of bed: 10 reps

**Shoulder Complex**:
- Doorframe lat stretch: 30s
- Chest opener (arms back, hands clasped): 30s
- Band pull-aparts or arm circles: 20 reps

**Hamstring/Posterior Chain**:
- Standing toe touch reach: 10 reps
- World's greatest stretch: 5 per side

### Post-Training Static Stretch Routine (5–10 min)
Hold each stretch 30–60 seconds. Do not bounce.
- Hip flexors (kneeling)
- Glutes (pigeon or figure-4)
- Hamstrings (lying or standing)
- Lats (doorframe)
- Chest (doorframe or arm-behind-back)
- Quadriceps (standing)

## TRACKING AND FEEDBACK LOOPS

### What the Agent Should Track

**Session Log (every session)**:
- Date, day of week
- Exercises, sets, reps, weight (or difficulty rating for bodyweight)
- RPE for each main exercise
- Session notes (energy level, sleep quality, any pain/discomfort)

**Weekly Check-In**:
- Average sleep hours
- Stress level (1–10)
- Any persistent soreness or pain
- Performance compared to last week (stronger? more reps? better form?)

**Monthly Metrics**:
- Body weight (weekly average, not single measurement)
- Body fat % estimate (tape measurements or calipers)
- Progress photos (front, side, back)
- Performance milestones (max pull-ups, KB weight on key lifts)

### Agent Decision Rules Based on Tracking Data

| Signal | Agent Response |
|---|---|
| Strength consistently increasing | Continue current plan; increase load when reps hit top of range |
| Strength plateauing for 2+ weeks | Check: Is progressive overload being applied? Is sleep/nutrition adequate? Consider deload. |
| Scale weight dropping fast (>2 lbs/week) | Increase calories slightly — too fast = muscle loss risk |
| Scale weight not moving but measurements improving | This is recomp working — celebrate, do not change |
| Persistent joint pain | Deload immediately, address with mobility work, check form |
| Consistently low RPE | Increase load or volume — not training hard enough |
| Consistently RPE 10 every session | Reduce volume — overreaching risk |

## AGE-SPECIFIC PROGRAMMING CONSIDERATIONS (Age 44)

### What Changes After 40
1. **Testosterone decline**: ~1% per year after 30. By 44, Nathan has ~14% less testosterone than at 30. This is real but manageable — it means slower muscle gain, not impossible muscle gain.

2. **Recovery time increases**: The SRA curve is longer. Allow 48–72 hours between heavy sessions targeting the same muscle group (vs. 24–48 hours for younger athletes).

3. **Tendon and ligament adaptation is slower**: Connective tissue adapts at a slower rate than muscle. This is the most common source of overuse injury in athletes over 40. Respect this with:
   - Full warm-ups every session
   - Gradual load increases (never jump more than 10% in a week)
   - Deloads every 4–6 weeks

4. **Joint health**: Prioritize full range of motion and joint-friendly exercise selection. KB exercises are generally joint-friendly compared to barbell lifts because they allow natural movement paths.

5. **Sleep impact amplified**: Sleep quality and muscle recovery are more tightly linked after 40. The agent should consistently flag sleep as the single highest-leverage recovery tool.

### What Doesn't Change After 40
- The muscle building process is fundamentally the same
- Progressive overload still works
- High protein still drives muscle protein synthesis
- The ceiling for aesthetic improvement is still very high
- The body still responds robustly to the right stimulus

**Agent message**: Nathan at 44 can build a physique that most people at 30 would envy. The timeline is slightly longer, the warm-up is slightly longer, and the sleep matters slightly more. Everything else is the same.

## INJURY PREVENTION PROTOCOLS

### The Most Common Injuries in Nathan's Training Style

**1. Lower Back Strain**
- Cause: Poor hip hinge mechanics in swings/RDLs; insufficient core bracing
- Prevention: Always brace TVA before every rep; never round the lower back under load
- Agent check: If Nathan reports lower back soreness, review swing and deadlift form cues

**2. Shoulder Impingement**
- Cause: Internal rotation of the shoulder during pressing; weak rotator cuff; insufficient warm-up
- Prevention: Rotator cuff exercises 2x/week; face pulls and band pull-aparts; full warm-up
- Agent check: Pain on shoulder abduction above 90 degrees = stop pressing, investigate

**3. Knee Pain**
- Cause: Knee valgus (caving in) under load; tight hip flexors causing reduced glute activation
- Prevention: Cue "push knees out" in all squat/lunge movements; glute activation warm-up
- Agent check: Pain at the front of the knee = patellar tendon issue; reduce knee-dominant exercises

**4. Bicep Tendon Irritation**
- Cause: High pull-up volume without adequate warm-up; overuse
- Prevention: Dead hang warm-up, grease-the-groove approach (sub-max volume spread throughout day)
- Agent check: Sharp pain in shoulder at front of joint during pull-ups = rest, assess

### The 10% Rule
Never increase total training volume (sets × reps × weight) by more than 10% per week. This is the most reliable way to avoid overuse injuries while still making progress.
