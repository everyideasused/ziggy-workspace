# Appendix — Restaurant Construction

> Industry appendix for [[Construction PM Knowledge Base]] and [[Sector Profiles — Retail, Commercial, Residential, Civil]]

---

## Overview

Restaurant construction combines the complexity of commercial kitchen infrastructure with the aesthetic demands of a hospitality environment. The kitchen (back of house) is essentially a small industrial facility, while the dining room (front of house) is a brand experience. Managing both simultaneously within tight budgets and aggressive opening timelines is the core challenge.

## Kitchen Design & Equipment

### The Kitchen is the Critical Path

Kitchen design drives the entire restaurant layout because:
- Exhaust hood locations determine structural penetrations and rooftop equipment placement
- Grease trap size and location must be coordinated with plumbing early
- Electrical demand is concentrated in the kitchen (ovens, fryers, walk-ins, dishwashers)
- Gas piping routing depends on equipment layout
- Floor drainage patterns must be designed around equipment placement
- Walk-in cooler/freezer location affects both structural and MEP

### Typical Kitchen Systems
| System | Components |
|--------|-----------|
| Cooking line | Range, grill, fryer, oven, salamander, steam table |
| Exhaust / ventilation | Type I hood (grease-laden), Type II hood (heat/steam), make-up air unit |
| Fire suppression | Ansul or similar wet chemical system under hoods |
| Refrigeration | Walk-in cooler, walk-in freezer, reach-in units, prep tables |
| Dishwashing | Commercial dishwasher, three-compartment sink, pre-rinse |
| Prep | Prep tables, food processors, mixers, slicers |
| Storage | Dry storage shelving, chemical storage (separate per code) |
| Beverage | Soda/beer systems, ice machines, coffee/espresso |

### Kitchen Design Process
```
Menu Concept Defined → Kitchen Consultant Engaged →
Preliminary Equipment List → Kitchen Layout (drives architectural plan) →
MEP Coordination (gas, electric, plumbing, exhaust) →
Equipment Specifications Finalized → Health Department Review →
Equipment Procurement → Installation → Commissioning
```

**Key principle:** The menu drives the equipment, the equipment drives the kitchen layout, and the kitchen layout drives the building design. Start with the food.

## Specialized MEP Requirements

### Grease Management
- **Grease trap / interceptor:** Required by virtually all jurisdictions for any food preparation
- **Sizing:** Based on fixture count, flow rate, and menu type (high-grease menus need larger units)
- **Location:** Typically exterior, in-ground; must be accessible for pumping
- **Maintenance:** Regular pumping schedule required (monthly to quarterly)
- **Interior grease traps:** Point-of-use traps at specific fixtures (in addition to main interceptor)
- **Permit:** Often requires separate grease trap permit from the sewer authority

### Exhaust Systems
- **Type I hoods:** Over all cooking equipment producing grease-laden vapors
- **Type II hoods:** Over dishwashers, steam tables, and heat-producing equipment
- **Make-up air:** Required to replace exhausted air (balanced system prevents negative pressure)
- **Rooftop exhaust fans:** Must be curbed, with grease containment
- **Ductwork:** Welded stainless steel for grease ducts, fire-wrapped per code
- **Fire suppression:** Wet chemical (Ansul-type) under all Type I hoods — automatic and manual activation
- **Kitchen hood cleaning:** Scheduled cleaning required (insurance requirement)

### Plumbing — Restaurant Specific
- Hot water demand is very high (dish machine, hand sinks, mop sinks, prep)
- May require dedicated water heater or booster heater for dish machine (180°F)
- Floor drains throughout kitchen (slope-to-drain critical)
- Indirect waste connections for ice machines, beverage systems
- Hand sinks required at each food prep area AND at kitchen entrance (health code)
- Mop sink / utility sink required
- Backflow prevention on all potable water connections

### Electrical — Restaurant Specific
- High amperage cooking equipment (electric ovens, fryers can be 50-60A each)
- Walk-in cooler/freezer compressors
- Dish machine (often requires dedicated circuit)
- POS systems, music/AV, lighting controls
- Outdoor signage and patio lighting
- Drive-through equipment (menu boards, speaker systems, timers) — QSR only

## Health Department Process

### Pre-Construction
- **Plan review submission** to health department (separate from building permit in most jurisdictions)
- Plans must show: equipment layout, plumbing fixtures, ventilation, finishes, hand sink locations, chemical storage
- Review timeline: 2-6 weeks (varies widely)
- Comments may require design revisions

### During Construction
- Some jurisdictions require health department inspections at rough-in
- Verify hand sink locations, floor drain placement, fixture connections

### Pre-Opening
- **Health department final inspection** — required before serving any food
- Inspect: equipment installation, plumbing connections, temperature control, hand washing stations, chemical storage, pest control measures, employee restrooms, food storage
- **Must pass before opening** — this is independent of the building CO
- Many jurisdictions require a food handler/manager on staff with valid ServSafe or equivalent certification

## Liquor Licensing

If the restaurant will serve alcohol:

- **Application timing:** Apply as early as possible — liquor license approval can take 30-120+ days
- **Types:** Beer/wine only, full liquor, Sunday sales (varies by state/municipality)
- **Requirements vary widely:** Background checks, proximity to schools/churches, public notice periods, hearing before licensing board
- **Landlord consent:** Some leases require landlord approval for liquor license
- **Construction impact:** Bar design, liquor storage, separate POS integration

**Critical:** Liquor license delays do NOT delay construction but CAN delay revenue. Track separately from the construction schedule but manage in parallel.

## Drive-Through (QSR) Specific

### Additional Construction Scope
- Drive-through lane design (stacking, clearance, turning radius)
- Menu board foundations and electrical
- Speaker/microphone posts and wiring
- Preview board and confirmation screen
- Drive-through window (pass-through window, heated shelf)
- Canopy over order point and/or pickup window (some brands)
- Traffic directional signage and pavement markings
- Timer system (speed of service measurement)

### Additional Permits
- DOT/traffic review of drive-through stacking and circulation
- Sign permits for menu boards (often subject to size/illumination limits)
- Some jurisdictions restrict drive-throughs by zoning (check early)

## Construction Sequence — Restaurant Specific Additions

### Before Standard Rough-In
- Grease trap excavation and installation (before exterior paving)
- Walk-in cooler/freezer slab depression (before slab pour)
- Floor drain layout verification (before slab pour — CANNOT be corrected after)
- Underground plumbing for floor drains, floor sinks, grease waste lines

### During Rough-In
- Kitchen hood ductwork (often determines ceiling height and soffit locations)
- Gas piping to all cooking equipment locations
- Electrical rough-in for high-amperage kitchen circuits
- Exhaust fan curbs on roof

### During Finish / Equipment Phase
- Walk-in panel erection and equipment setting
- Cooking line equipment set and connect (gas, electric, plumbing)
- Hood and fire suppression final connection and testing
- Bar construction and undercounter equipment
- POS installation and programming
- AV/music system installation

### Pre-Opening (After CO, Before Revenue)
- Health department final inspection and food permit
- Liquor license active (if applicable)
- Staff training (front and back of house)
- Equipment testing and menu prep
- Soft opening → grand opening

## Common Risks

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|------------|
| Health department rejection at final | Pre-app meeting; involve kitchen consultant; inspect before calling for final |
| Grease trap undersized | Size conservatively; verify local code requirements early |
| Hood system changes after ceiling close-up | Finalize kitchen equipment layout before rough-in |
| Floor drain slope failures | Laser-verify before pour; use experienced concrete contractor |
| Liquor license delay | Apply at lease signing, not at construction start |
| Kitchen equipment lead times | Order at DD/CD phase; 8-14 week lead times typical |
| Menu changes driving equipment changes mid-construction | Lock menu/equipment by end of DD; formalize change order process |
