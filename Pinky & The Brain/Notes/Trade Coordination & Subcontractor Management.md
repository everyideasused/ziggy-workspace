# Trade Coordination & Subcontractor Management

> **Purpose:** The GC's daily operating reality — scheduling subcontractors, managing scope boundaries, resolving conflicts between trades, maintaining quality standards, and keeping the project moving. This is the execution-layer counterpart to the PM KB's stakeholder management. Where the PM sees the big picture, the GC lives in the details of who shows up Monday morning and whether the plumber left enough room for the HVAC duct.

---

## The GC's Core Role

A general contractor is fundamentally a **coordinator of specialists**. On most residential projects, the GC's own crew handles framing and possibly rough carpentry, while everything else is subcontracted: excavation, concrete, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, insulation, drywall, painting, roofing, siding, tile, flooring, cabinets, countertops, landscaping.

The GC's value is not in performing every trade — it's in:
1. **Sequencing** — Getting the right trade on site at the right time with the right predecessors complete
2. **Scope management** — Ensuring every inch of work is covered by someone's contract, with no gaps and no overlaps
3. **Quality control** — Verifying each trade's work meets code and contract standards before the next trade covers it up
4. **Conflict resolution** — When Trade A's work interferes with Trade B's, the GC decides who adjusts
5. **Communication** — Keeping the owner, architect, and all subs informed of schedule, changes, and expectations

---

## Trade Sequencing — The Standard Order

The typical residential trade sequence, with dependencies:

| Order | Trade | Predecessor | Notes |
|-------|-------|------------|-------|
| 1 | **Excavation / Site Work** | Permits, survey | Sets the building pad, utilities, erosion control |
| 2 | **Foundation / Concrete** | Excavation complete, footing inspection | Includes footings, walls, slabs, waterproofing |
| 3 | **Framing** (GC crew or framing sub) | Foundation complete and inspected | Largest single phase — drives the critical path |
| 4 | **Roofing** | Roof sheathing complete | Get the building dried in ASAP |
| 5 | **Windows & Exterior Doors** | Framing + WRB | Must be installed before siding |
| 6 | **Siding / Exterior Cladding** | Windows in, WRB complete | Protects the envelope |
| 7 | **Plumbing rough** | Framing inspected | First MEP trade in — largest pipes, least flexible routing |
| 8 | **HVAC rough** | Framing inspected, plumbing rough started | Ductwork needs space — coordinates with plumber |
| 9 | **Electrical rough** | Framing inspected, plumbing and HVAC mostly placed | Most flexible routing — works around the other two |
| 10 | **Low-voltage** | With or after electrical rough | Data, security, speaker, smart home wiring |
| 11 | **Insulation** | ALL rough inspections passed | Cannot start until every trade that needs cavity access is done and inspected |
| 12 | **Drywall** | Insulation inspected | Hang, tape, mud, sand, prime |
| 13 | **Painting** (prime + first coat) | Drywall complete | Often before cabinets and trim |
| 14 | **Cabinets** | Painting primed/first-coated | Kitchen, bath, laundry |
| 15 | **Countertops** | Cabinets installed | Template after cabinet install; fabrication takes 1-2 weeks |
| 16 | **Tile** | Varies — shower before drywall (pan/backer), floors after cabinets | Complex sequencing — consult tile sub early |
| 17 | **Hardwood / LVP flooring** | Cabinets in (if flooring goes under), or after cabinets (if not) | Discuss with flooring sub — some install before cabinets, some after |
| 18 | **Trim carpentry** | Painting, cabinets, flooring | Doors, baseboard, casing, crown |
| 19 | **Plumbing trim** | Cabinets + countertops (kitchen), drywall + paint (baths) | Fixtures, faucets, toilets, disposals |
| 20 | **Electrical trim** | Paint complete | Devices, covers, fixtures, fans |
| 21 | **HVAC trim** | Drywall + paint | Registers, grilles, thermostat |
| 22 | **Final paint** (touch-up) | All trim trades done | Touch up after every sub has been through |
| 23 | **Cleaning** | All work complete | Construction clean then detail clean |
| 24 | **Landscaping / Hardscape** | Building exterior complete, site grading done | Driveway, walkways, planting, irrigation |

### The Three Bottleneck Zones

**Bottleneck 1: MEP rough-in (trades 7-9).** Three trades competing for the same wall and floor cavities. Plumbing goes first (largest pipes), HVAC second (largest ducts), electrical last (most flexible). If they're not coordinated, you get conflicting routes, overdrilled framing members, and failed inspections. A 30-minute coordination meeting before rough-in starts saves days of rework.

**Bottleneck 2: The drywall-to-trim gap (trades 12-18).** This is where projects stall. Drywall takes 2-3 weeks (hang, tape, three coats, sand, prime). Then painting, cabinets, and countertops must sequence through in order, each with lead times. If cabinets were ordered late, or the countertop fabricator has a 3-week backlog, the project sits idle.

**Bottleneck 3: Finish trade stacking (trades 18-22).** Trim carpenter, plumber, electrician, HVAC tech, and painter all need access to the same spaces in a specific order. Scheduling these trades in the wrong sequence creates damage (painter after electrician), rework (trim before final flooring), or waiting (plumber can't set faucets until counters are in).

## Scope Management — The Gap Problem

The most expensive problems on a construction project are **scope gaps** — work that falls between two subcontractor scopes and doesn't get done until someone notices (usually at the worst possible time).

### Common Scope Gaps

| Between | Gap | Who Should Cover It |
|---------|-----|-------------------|
| Framing and Drywall | Backing/blocking for wall-mounted items (grab bars, TV mounts, heavy cabinets, towel bars) | Framing — but only if the GC coordinates the locations from the cabinet/fixture drawings |
| Plumbing and Tile | Shower pan, waterproofing, backer board installation | Clarify in contracts — some plumbers do the pan, some tilers do. Never assume. |
| Electrical and HVAC | Thermostat wiring, dedicated circuits for HVAC equipment | Electrical typically runs the wire; HVAC connects at the equipment. Verify. |
| Cabinets and Plumbing | Sink cutout, dishwasher hookup, ice maker line | Cabinet installer makes the cutout; plumber hooks up. But who drills the cabinet back for the supply lines? |
| Painting and Everyone Else | Touch-up after each trade damages the paint | Build one final touch-up visit into the painting contract. Don't assume the painter will come back for free. |
| Framing and Concrete | Anchor bolt placement and hold-down embed placement | Framer should provide a bolt plan before the concrete pour. If they don't, the GC must coordinate. |
| Siding and Roofing | Kick-out flashing at roof-to-wall transitions | Both trades point at each other. The GC must explicitly assign this. Missing kick-out flashing is the number one cause of water intrusion at these transitions. |

### How to Prevent Scope Gaps

1. **Write clear scopes of work** in every subcontract — not just what's included, but what's explicitly excluded
2. **Use a scope matrix** for complex projects — a grid showing every work item and which sub is responsible
3. **Hold a pre-construction meeting** with all trades before work starts — walk the plans, identify interfaces, resolve who does what
4. **Walk the job at every trade transition** — before one trade finishes and the next starts, verify the predecessor work is complete and the handoff is clean

## Quality Management

### The GC's Quality Checkpoints

Quality control happens at three levels:

**Level 1: Self-check by the sub.** The sub's foreman should walk their own work before calling it complete. Good subs do this automatically. Unreliable subs don't.

**Level 2: GC verification before concealment.** The GC (or superintendent) walks every trade's work before the next trade covers it. This is the last chance to catch problems. Key verification points:

- After framing, before sheathing/insulation: Check plumb, square, blocking, connections, notching/boring compliance
- After MEP rough, before insulation: Check routing conflicts, test results, proper support and clearances
- After insulation, before drywall: Check coverage, vapor retarder, air sealing at penetrations
- After drywall, before trim: Check flatness, corner quality, screw pops
- After trim, before final paint: Check reveals, coped joints, miter tightness, door swing

**Level 3: Inspection by the building official.** The code inspector verifies minimum code compliance. Passing inspection does not mean the work is good — it means it meets the legal minimum. The GC's standards should exceed code minimums.

### Quality Conversations with Subs

When work doesn't meet standards, the conversation matters:

**Effective:** "The shear wall nailing on the south wall is at 6" on center — the plans call for 3" at the panel edges. Can you correct that before we call for the framing inspection?"

**Ineffective:** "This looks wrong. Fix it."

Be specific about what's wrong, what the standard is, and what the correction looks like. Document with photos. Follow up to verify the correction was made.

### Back-Charges

When a sub damages another sub's work or fails to complete their scope, the GC may need to back-charge. Rules:

1. **Document the issue** with photos, dates, and a written description before any correction work
2. **Notify the responsible sub in writing** that the issue exists and give them a reasonable opportunity to correct it
3. **If they don't correct it**, have another sub (or your crew) fix it, document the cost, and deduct from the responsible sub's payment
4. **This must be addressed in the subcontract** — the right to back-charge should be explicitly stated in the agreement

## Scheduling Subs — Practical Realities

### Lead Times

Every trade has a gap between "when you call them" and "when they show up." These lead times vary by market conditions, but general ranges:

| Trade | Typical Lead Time | Long-Lead Items |
|-------|------------------|-----------------|
| Excavation | 1-2 weeks | None |
| Concrete | 1-3 weeks | Specialty mixes, pump trucks |
| Framing | 1-2 weeks | Engineered trusses (4-6 weeks from order), steel beams (2-4 weeks) |
| Roofing | 1-2 weeks | Specialty materials (copper, slate, tile) |
| Plumbing | 1-3 weeks | Fixtures with long lead (freestanding tubs, commercial-style faucets) |
| Electrical | 1-3 weeks | Panels (may have availability issues), specialty fixtures |
| HVAC | 2-4 weeks | Equipment (furnaces, condensers — can be 4-8 weeks in peak season) |
| Insulation | 1-2 weeks | Spray foam (limited applicators in some markets) |
| Drywall | 1-2 weeks | None (commodity material, readily available) |
| Cabinets | 4-12 weeks | Custom: 8-12 weeks. Semi-custom: 4-8 weeks. Stock: 1-3 weeks. |
| Countertops | 2-4 weeks (after template) | Natural stone: 3-4 weeks. Quartz: 2-3 weeks. Template happens after cabinets are installed. |
| Tile | 1-2 weeks (labor), 1-4 weeks (specialty material) | Imported tile, large-format, mosaic |
| Hardwood flooring | 1-3 weeks (labor), 1-4 weeks (material) | Wide-plank, exotic species, custom finishes |

### The Golden Rules of Scheduling Subs

1. **Confirm three times.** Call when you book, call the week before, call the day before. Subs juggle multiple projects. If you don't confirm, you get bumped.
2. **Have the predecessor 100% complete.** Nothing wastes a sub's time (and destroys your credibility) like calling them to site when the work isn't ready. If the plumber shows up and framing isn't inspected, you've burned a day of their schedule and they'll deprioritize you next time.
3. **Give accurate scope.** When booking, be specific: "3-bedroom, 2-bath, 1,800 sq ft, slab-on-grade, gas furnace, 200-amp panel." Not "just a small house." Accurate scope means accurate scheduling and accurate bids.
4. **Pay on time.** The single most effective thing a GC can do to get priority scheduling from subs is pay invoices promptly. Subs talk to each other. A GC who pays on time gets their calls returned first.
5. **Protect their work.** If the drywall crew finishes and the next three trades damage the walls, the drywall crew won't prioritize your next project. Manage the site so completed work isn't damaged by subsequent trades.

## Communication Protocols

### Daily Minimum

- Know who is on site today and what they're working on
- Walk the site at least once per day (ideally morning and afternoon on active projects)
- Address issues immediately — a 5-minute conversation today prevents a 5-day delay next week

### Pre-Construction Kickoff (Every Project)

Before the first trade mobilizes, hold a meeting with all key subs. Cover:
- Scope boundaries and interfaces (who does what at the transitions)
- Schedule milestones and inspection dates
- Site logistics (material staging, dumpster location, parking, site access hours)
- Safety requirements
- Communication expectations (who to call for issues, how changes are communicated)
- Quality expectations (any specific standards beyond code minimum)

### Change Management

Changes during construction are inevitable. The protocol:
1. **Document the change** in writing — what changed, why, and who requested it
2. **Get pricing** from the affected sub(s) before authorizing the work
3. **Get written approval** from the owner before proceeding (if it affects cost or schedule)
4. **Issue a change order** to the sub, signed by both parties
5. **Update the schedule** if the change affects sequencing or timing

Never authorize extra work verbally and sort out the cost later. This is how budgets explode and relationships deteriorate.
