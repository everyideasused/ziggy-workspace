# PT-Shell-and-Core — Landlord Scope & Base Building Estimating

> Understanding what the landlord delivers — and what it costs — is foundational to every TIA negotiation and tenant-side estimate. This file covers the landlord's scope, shell delivery standards, vanilla box pricing, and how to estimate base building work.

---

## Shell Delivery Standards — Defined

The single biggest source of confusion in TI estimating is "what does the landlord deliver?" These are the standard terms — but every landlord interprets them differently. **Always get the landlord's specific definition in writing.**

### Cold Dark Shell
**What it is:** The bare minimum. Structure, envelope, and slab only.

Landlord delivers:
- Demised space (walls separating from other tenants)
- Concrete slab
- Structural system
- Exterior walls and roof (weather-tight)
- No MEP — no HVAC, plumbing, electrical, sprinkler

Tenant must provide everything else:
- All MEP (HVAC, plumbing, electrical, fire suppression, fire alarm)
- All interior construction
- All finishes

**When you see this:** New construction, first-generation space, landlord building spec space

**Tenant's TI budget impact:** Highest — full MEP from scratch adds $40–$100/SF+ vs. warm shell

---

### Warm Dark Shell
**What it is:** The most common "new space" delivery. Structure + envelope + MEP infrastructure roughed in.

Landlord delivers:
- Everything in cold dark shell PLUS:
- HVAC stub-in (typically: RTU on roof, ductwork stubbed to space)
- Electrical stub-in (panel in space with service; typical 200A)
- Plumbing stub-in (water and drain at back wall)
- Fire suppression rough-in (main line into space, no heads)
- Gas stub-in (in some cases)

Tenant still provides:
- HVAC distribution (ductwork, diffusers, controls)
- Electrical distribution (panels, wiring, devices, fixtures)
- Plumbing distribution (all fixtures, branch lines, slab work)
- Fire suppression heads and distribution
- All interior construction and finishes

**TI budget offset vs. cold dark shell:** $25–$55/SF savings (varies by quality of stub-ins)

---

### Vanilla Box
**What it is:** A "ready to finish" space. Landlord does the base build-out; tenant customizes.

Landlord delivers:
- Everything in warm dark shell PLUS:
- Interior demising walls (insulated, taped/finished, primed)
- Storefront complete (standard aluminum with glass)
- Restrooms (typically 1 or 2, complete with fixtures)
- Ceiling grid (ACT, standard 2×4 white)
- HVAC distribution complete (ductwork, diffusers, thermostats)
- Lighting (basic T-bar fluorescent or LED troffers)
- Electrical panel complete (ready for tenant circuits)
- Sprinkler heads (standard layout, complete)
- Fire alarm devices (basic coverage)
- Flooring: may or may not include (varies — sometimes concrete, sometimes VCT)

Tenant customizes:
- Branded signage and storefront modifications
- Custom finishes (upgraded flooring, custom ceilings, feature walls)
- Kitchen or specialty MEP (restaurants, medical)
- Millwork, casework, fixtures
- Tenant-specific lighting, low voltage, technology

**TI budget offset vs. warm dark shell:** Additional $20–$45/SF savings

---

### As-Is / Second Generation Space
**What it is:** Previously occupied space. Condition completely variable.

Landlord delivers:
- Existing space in its current configuration

Tenant must:
- Survey existing conditions before estimating (non-negotiable)
- Assess existing MEP capacity and layout vs. new program
- Determine demo scope
- Identify code deficiencies (ADA, egress, current code)

**As-is space risk:** Highest — you don't know what you're buying until you survey it.

**Always budget for:**
- Existing conditions survey: $2,000–$8,000
- Potential asbestos/lead survey (pre-1980 buildings): $2,000–$6,000
- Demo contingency: 15–25% extra vs. new space

## Landlord Work Pricing (What It Costs the Landlord)

Understanding landlord-side costs helps negotiate TIA and work letters:

### Vanilla Box Cost Benchmarks (Landlord's Scope)

| Item | $/SF of Tenant Space | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Demising walls (stud + GWB, both sides) | $8–$14/SF | Per SF of tenant space |
| Storefront (standard aluminum, complete) | $65–$100/SF of storefront | $15,000–$35,000 typical storefront |
| Restroom (complete, 2 fixtures) | $15,000–$35,000/RR | Varies by finish level |
| HVAC (RTU + distribution, complete) | $20–$35/SF | Per SF conditioned |
| Electrical (service + distribution, basic) | $15–$25/SF | Basic lighting + power |
| Fire suppression (complete, office layout) | $3–$6/SF | |
| Fire alarm (basic) | $3–$6/SF | |
| Ceiling (ACT, 2×4, standard) | $4–$7/SF | |
| Flooring (VCT or scored concrete) | $2–$5/SF | |
| **Vanilla Box Total (typical)** | **$55–$100/SF** | Typical landlord investment |

**Use this to evaluate TIA:** A $50/SF TIA in a cold dark shell is effectively zero — the landlord is putting all risk on the tenant.

## TIA Negotiation Framework

### What TIA Should Cover

A fair TIA in a warm dark shell environment should cover:
- All tenant-specific TI work above warm shell condition
- Soft costs proportionate to project size
- Not: landlord's base building scope (that's the landlord's cost)

**Negotiating rule of thumb:**
```
Minimum viable TIA = Tenant's hard cost - Landlord's avoided cost
= Tenant TI estimate - What landlord would spend to deliver vanilla box
```

If landlord would spend $75/SF on vanilla box but is instead delivering cold dark shell, the TIA should be $75/SF higher than for a vanilla box deal.

### TIA Benchmarks by Market and Format

| Format | Market | Typical TIA Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| QSR Restaurant | Suburban | $60–$120/SF | Often warm/dark shell delivery |
| QSR Restaurant | Urban / premium | $80–$150/SF | Higher costs; higher TIA |
| Retail (national tenant) | Suburban strip | $40–$80/SF | Vanilla box typical |
| Retail (national tenant) | Lifestyle / power | $60–$120/SF | Better delivery; higher ask |
| Medical office | Class A MOB | $60–$100/SF | Landlord incentivizes medical |
| Restaurant FSR | Mixed-use | $80–$150/SF | Competitive for anchor tenants |

**These TIAs rarely cover full build-out costs** for brands with elevated standards. The gap is the tenant's capital contribution above TIA.

## Landlord Work Letter Checklist

When reviewing or drafting a landlord work letter, confirm:

**MEP Delivery**
- [ ] HVAC: What is the unit capacity delivered? Where does the stub-in terminate?
- [ ] Electrical: What amperage? 1-phase or 3-phase? Panel location? 
- [ ] Plumbing: Hot and cold water stub-in locations? Drain locations?
- [ ] Gas: Is gas available? Stub-in location? Pressure?
- [ ] Fire suppression: Main line only or full heads? Layout standard?
- [ ] Telecom: Is conduit provided? Point of entry location?

**Structural**
- [ ] What floor loading is designed? (100 PSF minimum for most commercial)
- [ ] What roof loading is available for RTUs?
- [ ] Is structural reinforcement available if needed?

**Exterior**
- [ ] Storefront type and size confirmed?
- [ ] Demising wall finish confirmed (both sides)?
- [ ] Exterior signage location(s) confirmed?

**Timing**
- [ ] When will space be ready for tenant work?
- [ ] Is there a landlord work completion guarantee?
- [ ] What happens if landlord is late?

## Common Landlord Scope Mistakes

1. **Assuming "warm shell" means the same thing to every landlord** — it doesn't; always get it in writing
2. **Not confirming electrical service capacity** — 200A is often inadequate for restaurant; verify early
3. **Assuming gas is available** — not all commercial buildings have gas service; check before designing
4. **Taking landlord's HVAC capacity at face value** — confirm tons delivered vs. tons needed
5. **Missing restroom compliance** — existing restrooms may not be ADA-compliant for new occupancy
6. **Not confirming fire suppression design basis** — system designed for office won't work for restaurant
