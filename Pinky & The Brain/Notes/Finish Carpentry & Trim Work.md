# Finish Carpentry & Trim Work

> **Purpose:** The craft layer — doors, windows, baseboard, crown molding, casing, built-ins, and stairs. Finish carpentry is where a building goes from "structure" to "space." It's the most visible carpentry work in a building and the quality indicator most people notice, even if they can't articulate why. For a PM, finish quality is the clearest signal of a GC's standards. For a personal builder, it's the most satisfying and most demanding skill to develop.

---

## The Finish Carpentry Mindset

Rough framing tolerances are measured in fractions of an inch. Finish carpentry tolerances are measured in 1/32" or tighter. The transition from rough work to finish work is a shift in precision, patience, and tool selection. Key principles:

**Scribe, don't force.** Walls aren't flat. Floors aren't level. Corners aren't square. Good finish carpentry adapts the trim to the reality of the building, not the other way around. Scribing means tracing an irregular surface onto the workpiece and cutting to that line so it fits perfectly against the surface.

**Measure twice, cut once — but also dry-fit before nailing.** Hold every piece in place before committing fasteners. Check reveals, check level/plumb, check how joints will close. Once a nail is in, pulling it leaves a hole.

**Work from the most visible areas outward.** Install the focal-point pieces first (fireplace mantel, kitchen trim, main entrance). Cut your best material for the most visible locations. Use shorter, less-perfect stock in closets and secondary spaces.

**Cope inside corners. Miter outside corners.** This is the fundamental rule of trim carpentry. Inside corners get coped joints (one piece butts into the corner, the second is back-cut to the profile and fits over the first). Outside corners get mitered joints. Why? Because walls move seasonally — a coped joint stays tight when materials shrink; a mitered inside corner opens a gap.

## Door Installation

### Pre-Hung Doors (Standard Interior)

1. **Check the rough opening** — should be 2" wider and 1" taller than the door unit (typical). Remove debris and protruding fasteners from the framing.
2. **Set the door unit in the opening** — shim the hinge side first. Check hinge-side jamb for plumb in both planes (face and edge).
3. **Shim behind each hinge location** — plus at the top and bottom of the jamb. Shims should be in pairs (from both sides) for a solid bearing surface.
4. **Fasten through the jamb at shim locations** — use finish nails or screws (screws are stronger and allow adjustment). Start with the hinge side.
5. **Check the reveal** — the gap between the door edge and the jamb should be consistent (typically 1/8" or the thickness of a nickel). Adjust shims to even the reveal on all three sides.
6. **Shim and fasten the strike side** — adjust until the reveal is uniform and the door latches smoothly without binding.
7. **Check the door swing** — open to 45° and release. It should stay put (not swing open or closed on its own). If it drifts, the jamb isn't plumb.
8. **Install strike plate** — chisel a clean mortise, test the latch engagement.
9. **Trim the shims** flush with the jamb — score and snap, don't cut against the jamb.
10. **Install casing** — see Casing section below.

### Exterior Doors

Same principles but with additional considerations: weatherstripping must seal on all four sides, the threshold must be level and properly shimmed, the sill pan (flashing under the door sill) must be installed before the door unit, and the door must be properly flashed to integrate with the WRB.

### Common Door Problems and Fixes

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Door sticks at top corner | Hinge-side jamb not plumb (leaning toward strike) | Re-shim hinge side, plumb the jamb |
| Door won't latch | Strike plate misaligned | Adjust strike plate position (file the opening or re-mortise) |
| Door swings open by itself | Hinge-side jamb out of plumb (leaning away from strike) | Re-shim to plumb |
| Gap visible at top of door | Jamb head not level, or door unit is racked | Re-shim and level the head jamb |
| Hinge screws strip out | Screws too short or framing is soft | Replace with 3" screws that reach the framing stud |

## Window Trim

### Casing Styles

**Butted casing (picture frame)** — All four sides mitered at 45° to form a frame around the window. Clean, modern look. Demands precise miters because all four corners are visible.

**Stool and apron (traditional)** — A horizontal stool (sometimes called a window sill) extends past the casing on each side, with an apron below supporting the stool. Casing runs on the top and sides only, butting into the stool at the bottom. More traditional look, more forgiving of imperfect walls because the stool hides the transition.

**Modern/minimal** — Drywall returns to the window frame with no wood casing. Clean and contemporary. Requires very precise drywall work at the window jamb reveals.

### Window Trim Installation

1. **Mark the reveal** — Set a consistent gap (typically 1/4" or 3/16") between the jamb edge and the inside edge of the casing. Mark this on all three sides of the jamb. A combination square set to the reveal distance makes a quick marking gauge.
2. **Install the head casing first** — Cut miters on both ends. Nail at the reveal mark. The head piece establishes the reference for the side pieces.
3. **Install side casings** — Cut the miter at the top to meet the head piece. The bottom is typically a square cut to the stool (stool-and-apron) or a miter (picture frame).
4. **Check miter fit** — A gap at the front of the miter means the angle is too open. A gap at the back means it's too closed. Adjust the miter saw angle or sand the joint face.
5. **Glue mitered joints** and pin with a 23-gauge pinner across the joint. The glue provides the strength; the pin holds it while it dries.
6. **Fill nail holes** with color-matched wood filler (for stain-grade) or painter's caulk and fill (for paint-grade).

## Baseboard

### Material Options

| Type | Stock | Best For |
|------|-------|----------|
| MDF (medium-density fiberboard) | Pre-primed, smooth, affordable | Paint-grade applications. Won't take stain. |
| Finger-jointed pine | Short pieces joined, pre-primed | Paint-grade. Slightly more stable than MDF near moisture. |
| Clear pine/poplar | Solid wood, smooth grain | Paint-grade (poplar) or stain-grade (pine). More expensive. |
| Oak/maple/cherry | Hardwood, visible grain | Stain-grade. Premium applications. Requires pre-finishing or careful site finishing. |
| PVC/composite | Synthetic, moisture-proof | Bathrooms, laundry, basements. Won't rot. Cuts and nails like wood. |

### Installation Process

1. **Start with the longest walls** — use the best, straightest stock for the most visible runs.
2. **Butt joints on long runs** — where two pieces meet on the same wall, scarf the joint (overlapping 45° cuts) rather than butting end-to-end. Scarf joints stay tight as material shrinks.
3. **Inside corners: cope** — Cut the first piece square into the corner. For the second piece, miter it at 45° as if making an inside miter, then use a coping saw to cut along the profile of the molding, back-cutting slightly. The coped end fits over the first piece like a puzzle. Test-fit before nailing.
4. **Outside corners: miter** — Both pieces cut at 45°. Glue the miter joint and pin through the corner. Pre-drill if near the end to prevent splitting.
5. **Nail at the bottom plate and studs** — Use a stud finder. Bottom nail goes into the plate (horizontal framing member at the base of the wall). Upper nail goes into the stud. For tall baseboards, a third nail into the drywall (with adhesive behind) may be needed for the middle.
6. **Leave a gap at the floor for carpet** or butt to hard flooring. Baseboard sits on top of hard flooring (hardwood, tile, LVP), not under it.

### Common Baseboard Issues

**Gaps at the bottom (floor not level):** Scribe the baseboard to the floor. Set a compass to the widest gap, drag it along the floor while scribing onto the baseboard face, then cut along the scribe line. The baseboard now follows the floor contour.

**Gaps at the wall (wall not flat):** For painted work, caulk the top edge with paintable caulk. This is standard practice — no one expects walls to be perfectly flat. For stain-grade, scribing or strategic placement is the only option.

## Crown Molding

Crown is the most challenging common trim to install because it sits at an angle between the wall and ceiling (typically sprung at 38° or 45° from the wall).

### Key Concepts

**Spring angle** — The angle at which the back of the crown contacts the wall and ceiling. Most stock crown is 38° (also called 52/38). Some is 45° (also called 45/45). The spring angle determines how you set your miter saw for compound cuts.

**Compound miter** — Crown requires both a miter (rotation on the horizontal plane) and a bevel (tilt on the vertical plane). You can either cut the crown flat on the saw with compound angles, or position it upside-down and inverted against the saw fence at its spring angle and use simple miter cuts. The inverted method is more intuitive for most people.

**Coping crown** — Same principle as baseboard but trickier because the profile is more complex. Cut the miter, then carefully back-cut along the profile with a coping saw. A Dremel with a sanding drum helps clean up the cope.

### Installation Tips

- **Find the studs and ceiling joists** before starting. Mark them on the wall and ceiling with tape. Crown needs solid nailing — one nail into the wall stud (bottom edge) and one into the ceiling joist or blocking (top edge).
- **If no blocking exists behind the crown**, install it before starting. Triangular blocking pieces (cut from 2x4 at the spring angle) nailed between studs at the ceiling line give a solid nailing surface at any point along the wall.
- **Start on the wall opposite the entry door** — this is the least visible wall. Your technique improves as you work around the room. The most visible walls get your best work.
- **Test-fit every piece** before applying adhesive or nails.
- **Long runs will bow** — have a helper support the middle while you nail the ends. Work from the center outward for very long pieces.

## Built-Ins and Cabinetry Basics

### Cabinet Installation

**Upper cabinets first, then base cabinets.** This seems counterintuitive, but installing uppers first gives you clear access without reaching over base cabinets.

1. **Find the high point of the floor** — Use a level and measure up from the high point to establish the base cabinet height line (typically 34-1/2" for standard 36" countertop height).
2. **Mark a level line at 54" from the floor high point** — This is the bottom of the upper cabinets (standard 18" gap between counter and upper cabinet bottom).
3. **Install a temporary ledger board** at the 54" line — this supports the upper cabinets during installation so you don't need to hold them.
4. **Start from a corner** and work outward. Shim behind cabinets to plumb and level. Screw through the back rail into wall studs (not just drywall).
5. **Clamp adjacent cabinets together** and screw face frames to each other before permanently attaching to the wall.
6. **Base cabinets:** Set in position, shim level (front-to-back and side-to-side). Screw through the back rail into studs. Scribe filler pieces to wall contours.

### Shelf and Closet Systems

**Adjustable shelving** uses shelf pins in pre-drilled holes (spaced 32mm apart — European system standard). A drilling jig ensures consistent hole spacing. Shelf material: 3/4" plywood with edge banding, MDF, or melamine.

**Fixed shelving** — dadoed into side panels or supported by cleats. Stronger for heavy loads. Used in pantries, laundry rooms, and utility spaces.

## Stair Basics

Stairs are among the most complex finish carpentry tasks and have strict code requirements for safety.

### Key Terminology

- **Rise** — Total vertical distance from finished floor to finished floor
- **Run** — Total horizontal distance the stair occupies
- **Riser** — Vertical face of each step (typical max 7-3/4")
- **Tread** — Horizontal surface you step on (typical min 10" deep, with 1" to 1-1/4" nosing)
- **Stringer** — The angled structural member supporting the treads and risers (typically 2x12)
- **Nosing** — The front edge of the tread that overhangs the riser
- **Baluster** — Vertical members of the railing (max 4" spacing between them — code requirement so a 4" sphere cannot pass through)
- **Newel post** — The main structural post at the top and bottom of the staircase
- **Handrail** — The graspable rail. Must be between 34" and 38" above the stair nosing, continuous from top to bottom, and "graspable" (specific cross-section requirements in code).

### The Riser-Tread Relationship

All risers must be the same height. All treads must be the same depth. Maximum variance between any two risers: 3/8". This is a safety requirement and a code requirement — inconsistent risers cause trips and falls.

To calculate: Divide the total rise by the number of risers. Adjust until you get a riser height between 7" and 7-3/4". Then verify the tread depth meets the 10" minimum with the run available.

The 17-18 rule: Riser height + tread depth should equal approximately 17" to 18" for comfortable stairs. A 7.5" rise with a 10.5" tread = 18" (comfortable). A 7.75" rise with a 10" tread = 17.75" (still comfortable).
