# Tax Planning & Optimization

> Cross-cutting reference for [[Personal Finance Knowledge Base]]
> **Priority:** 🟢 Ongoing — tax awareness should inform every financial decision, but don't let the tax tail wag the investment dog.

---

## The Tax Planning Mindset

Tax planning is not about evasion — it's about using the structures Congress created to minimize your legally owed tax burden. Every dollar saved on taxes is a dollar that compounds in your investment accounts. Over a career, the difference between a tax-aware and tax-ignorant approach is easily six figures.

**Key principle:** Don't make financial decisions *solely* for tax reasons. A bad investment doesn't become good because of a tax deduction. But between two comparable options, always choose the more tax-efficient one.

## How Federal Income Tax Actually Works

### The Bracket System (It's Marginal, Not Flat)
The most common misunderstanding: **you don't pay your top bracket rate on all your income.** Each bracket only applies to the income within that range.

**2025 Tax Brackets (Single Filer):**

| Taxable Income | Rate |
|---------------|------|
| $0 – $11,925 | 10% |
| $11,926 – $48,475 | 12% |
| $48,476 – $103,350 | 22% |
| $103,351 – $197,300 | 24% |
| $197,301 – $250,525 | 32% |
| $250,526 – $626,350 | 35% |
| $626,351+ | 37% |

**Example:** If taxable income is $80,000 (single), you pay: 10% on the first $11,925, 12% on $11,926-$48,475, and 22% on $48,476-$80,000. Your *effective* tax rate is approximately 16-17%, not 22%.

### The Tax Math Chain
```
Gross Income
- Above-the-Line Deductions (401k, HSA, IRA, student loan interest)
= Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)
- Standard Deduction OR Itemized Deductions
= Taxable Income
× Marginal Tax Rates
= Tax Liability
- Tax Credits
= Tax Owed (or Refund)
```

### Deductions vs. Credits
**Deductions** reduce your taxable income. A $1,000 deduction in the 22% bracket saves $220.
**Credits** reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar. A $1,000 credit saves $1,000 regardless of bracket.
Credits are always more valuable than deductions of the same amount.

## Tax-Advantaged Account Strategies

### The Roth vs. Traditional Decision
This is fundamentally a bet on whether your tax rate will be higher or lower in retirement:

| Factor | Favors Roth (Pay Tax Now) | Favors Traditional (Pay Tax Later) |
|--------|--------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Current tax bracket | Low (12-22%) | High (32%+) |
| Expected future income | Higher than now | Lower than now |
| Time horizon | Long (20+ years of tax-free growth) | Shorter |
| Tax rate predictions | Expect rates to increase | Expect rates to stay same or decrease |
| RMD preference | No RMDs on Roth IRA | Subject to RMDs at 73+ |
| Estate goals | Want tax-free inheritance for heirs | Less concerned about estate |

**For current stage (accumulation, moderate income):** Roth contributions are generally favored. You're likely in a lower bracket now than you will be later, and 30+ years of tax-free growth is extremely valuable.

### Roth Conversion Ladder
Converting Traditional IRA/401k money to Roth in low-income years. Each conversion is taxed as ordinary income, but then grows tax-free forever. Particularly useful during career transitions, sabbaticals, or early retirement years when income dips.

### HSA Triple Play
1. Contribute pre-tax (reduces AGI)
2. Invest and grow tax-free
3. Withdraw tax-free for medical expenses

**Advanced move:** Pay medical expenses out of pocket, save receipts indefinitely, let HSA grow invested, and reimburse yourself years later — all tax-free growth in between.

## Investment Tax Efficiency

### Capital Gains Rates
Assets held longer than one year qualify for long-term capital gains rates:

| Taxable Income (Single, 2025) | Long-Term Capital Gains Rate |
|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Up to ~$48,475 | 0% |
| $48,476 – $533,400 | 15% |
| Above $533,400 | 20% |

**Key insight:** If your taxable income (after deductions) is in the 0% bracket, you pay zero tax on long-term capital gains. This is powerful for retirees managing withdrawals or anyone with a low-income year.

### Tax-Loss Harvesting (TLH)
Selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains. The losses can offset gains dollar-for-dollar, and up to $3,000/year of excess losses can offset ordinary income. Remaining losses carry forward indefinitely.

**How to execute:**
1. Identify positions with unrealized losses
2. Sell the losing position
3. Immediately buy a similar (but not "substantially identical") fund to maintain market exposure
4. The loss offsets gains on your tax return

**Wash sale rule:** You cannot buy a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale. Switching between S&P 500 funds from different providers (e.g., Vanguard → Fidelity) is generally considered acceptable.

### Tax-Efficient Fund Placement
See [[Investment Strategy & Portfolio Theory]] for the full tax-location strategy. Summary:
- **Taxable accounts:** Index funds, ETFs, tax-managed funds, municipal bonds
- **Tax-deferred (Traditional 401k/IRA):** Bonds, REITs, actively managed funds
- **Tax-free (Roth):** Highest expected growth assets

## Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing

### 2025 Standard Deduction
- Single: $15,000
- Married Filing Jointly: $30,000
- Head of Household: $22,500

Most people take the standard deduction. You should only itemize if your total itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction.

### Common Itemized Deductions
- **State and local taxes (SALT):** Property tax + state income tax, capped at $10,000
- **Mortgage interest:** On up to $750,000 of mortgage debt
- **Charitable contributions:** Cash donations up to 60% of AGI; appreciated stock donations avoid capital gains
- **Medical expenses:** Only the amount exceeding 7.5% of AGI

### Bunching Strategy
If your itemized deductions are close to the standard deduction, "bunch" deductions into alternating years. For example: make two years of charitable contributions in one year (itemize), then take the standard deduction the next year. Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs) facilitate this — contribute a lump sum, get the deduction now, distribute to charities over time.

## Key Tax Deadlines and Actions

| When | Action |
|------|--------|
| January | Receive W-2s, 1099s. Review prior year tax situation. |
| April 15 | Federal tax return due. IRA contribution deadline for prior year. |
| June 15 | Estimated tax payment Q2 (if self-employed or owing) |
| September 15 | Estimated tax payment Q3 |
| October 15 | Extended tax return deadline |
| November-December | Tax-loss harvesting review. Charitable giving. Roth conversion analysis. 401k contribution adjustment for next year. |
| Year-round | Track deductible expenses. Maximize employer benefit elections during open enrollment. |

## Tennessee-Specific Considerations

Tennessee has no state income tax on wages and salaries. This is a significant advantage:
- No state tax on W-2 income, 401k contributions/withdrawals, or IRA distributions
- No state capital gains tax
- Social Security benefits not taxed at the state level
- Property taxes are assessed — these can be itemized on federal return

**The advantage:** In retirement, Tennessee is extremely tax-friendly. No state income tax on retirement account withdrawals gives more flexibility in withdrawal planning compared to high-tax states.

**Sales tax:** Tennessee has one of the highest combined state + local sales tax rates in the country (~9.25-9.75% in Nashville). This affects the "cost of living" side but not the "income tax" side.

## When You Need a CPA or Tax Professional

- **Income crosses $200k+** and/or multiple income sources
- **Self-employment income** (quarterly estimated taxes, deductions, entity structure)
- **Stock options or RSUs** from an employer
- **Real estate transactions** (buying, selling, rental income)
- **Major life events** (marriage, divorce, inheritance, death of spouse)
- **Roth conversion planning** (especially large amounts)
- **Estate planning** (wills, trusts, gifting strategies)
- **IRS correspondence** (audit, notice, payment plan)
- **You're unsure about anything** — the cost of a CPA ($200-500 for basic preparation) is almost always less than the cost of a mistake
