# 🔍 Food Label & Ingredient Literacy

> **Purpose:** Complete guide to reading food labels as a vegan athlete — identifying hidden animal products, evaluating supplement quality, interpreting nutrition facts for lean bulk, and navigating confusing marketing claims.

---

## Reading the Nutrition Facts Panel

### What Matters Most for Lean Bulk

| Panel Item | What to Look For | Nathan's Target |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Serving size | Always recalculate to actual portion | — |
| Calories | Context: total day target | 2,600–2,800/day |
| Protein | Per 100g — not just per serving | 35–45g per meal |
| Total fat | Composition > total | 70–85g/day |
| Saturated fat | Minimize | <10% of total calories |
| Fiber | Higher = better | 40–60g/day |
| Added sugars | Distinguish from natural sugars | <25–30g added/day |

### The Per-100g Rule

Always compare foods per 100g, not per serving. Serving sizes are marketing tools.

```
Product A: 30g protein / 50g serving = 60g protein per 100g
Product B: 25g protein / 35g serving = 71g protein per 100g
→ Product B has MORE protein per gram despite appearing lower on the label
```

## Hidden Animal Ingredients — Master List

### Always Check For

| Ingredient | Source | Found In |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Casein / Whey | Cow's milk | "Non-dairy" creamers, protein bars, bread |
| Gelatin | Boiled animal bones/skin | Capsules, gummies, marshmallows, some yogurt |
| Carmine / E120 | Cochineal beetles | Red/pink food dyes in juice, candy, yogurt |
| Shellac / E904 | Lac insect secretions | Candy coating, wax on fresh produce, some tablets |
| Isinglass | Fish bladders | Beer and wine clarification — not always labeled |
| Anchovies | Fish | Worcestershire sauce, many pasta sauces, caesar dressing |
| Fish sauce | Fermented fish | Many Asian condiments |
| Lard | Pork fat | Traditional tortillas, canned refried beans, pie crust |
| Tallow | Beef fat | Some restaurant frying oils |
| L-cysteine / E920 | Feathers or hair | Commercial bread dough conditioners, some pretzels |
| Rennet | Calf stomach | Most conventional cheese |
| Lanolin | Sheep wool | Most D3 supplements (get lichen-derived instead) |
| Beeswax / E901 | Honeybees | Candy glaze, some produce wax |
| Bone char | Animal bones | Used to process (whiten) refined cane sugar |
| Collagen | Animal connective tissue | Trending protein powders — never vegan |
| Royal jelly / Propolis | Bees | Some supplements and beauty products |

### The "Natural Flavors" Problem

"Natural flavors" can legally include animal-derived compounds. The FDA definition is broad. When in doubt:
1. Contact the manufacturer directly
2. Use apps: **Is It Vegan?** or **Buycott**
3. Choose products with fully transparent labeling

### Vitamin and Supplement Traps

| Supplement | Common Non-Vegan Form | Vegan Alternative |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------|
| Vitamin D3 | Lanolin (sheep wool) | Lichen-derived D3 |
| Omega-3 | Fish oil | Algae-based EPA/DHA |
| Vitamin A | Retinol (animal liver) | Beta-carotene (plant) |
| Glucosamine | Shellfish shells | Corn-derived or fermented |
| Collagen | Animal skin/bones | No vegan equivalent — ignore |
| Some B12 | Bacterial synthesis (vegan ✅) | Any cyano or methylcobalamin |
| Gelatin capsules | Animal gelatin | HPMC (plant-based) capsules |
| Stearic acid / Magnesium stearate | Can be animal fat | Often plant; check with manufacturer |

## Supplement Quality Standards

### What Third-Party Testing Means

The FDA does not test supplements for purity or potency before sale. Third-party testing closes this gap:

| Certification | What It Verifies |
|-------------|-----------------|
| NSF Certified for Sport | No banned substances, label accuracy, contaminants |
| Informed Sport | Batch-tested for banned substances |
| USP Verified | Label accuracy, purity, dissolution |
| ConsumerLab | Independent testing — subscription-based results |
| Certified Vegan (Vegan Action) | No animal ingredients or byproducts |

**For athletes:** NSF Certified for Sport or Informed Sport is the gold standard.

### Heavy Metal Risk in Vegan Supplements

Plant-based protein powders (especially rice and hemp) can accumulate arsenic, lead, and cadmium from soil. Always choose third-party tested protein powders.

**High-risk products:** Rice protein, brown rice protein, hemp protein, green/superfood powders, seaweed/algae products

**What to look for:** "Heavy metals tested" on label, or ConsumerLab/NSF certification.

## Decoding Marketing Claims

### Claims That Mean Something

| Claim | What It Means |
|-------|-------------|
| USDA Certified Organic | No synthetic pesticides, no GMO (produce/grains). For livestock: pasture access, no growth hormones |
| Non-GMO Project Verified | Product ingredients not genetically modified |
| Certified Vegan (Vegan Action logo) | No animal ingredients, no animal testing |
| Certified B Corp | Social/environmental standards — not specifically vegan |
| Fair Trade Certified | Fair wages and conditions for farmers |
| USDA Organic (supplements) | Ingredients are organic — not a quality/purity indicator |

### Claims That Mean Very Little

| Claim | The Reality |
|-------|------------|
| "Natural" | No FDA definition for food. Means almost nothing. |
| "Plant-based" | Does NOT mean vegan. Could contain honey, dairy. |
| "Made with real [ingredient]" | Trace amount qualifies |
| "No artificial flavors" | Natural flavors can be animal-derived |
| "Superfood" | Marketing term with no regulatory definition |
| "Clean label" | No definition; marketing term |
| "Immune boosting" | Not an FDA-approved health claim; unverified |
| "Hormone-free" | All animals produce hormones; this is always technically false |
| "Humanely raised" | No standardized definition; self-certified |
| "Free-range" | USDA definition is minimal — 5 minutes of outdoor access qualifies for poultry |

### "Vegan" vs. "Plant-Based" — Know the Difference

| Term | What It Means |
|------|-------------|
| **Vegan** | No animal ingredients. In the context of companies/products, often includes commitment to no animal testing. |
| **Plant-based** | Primary ingredients are plants. May still contain honey, dairy, eggs. May be used for marketing without full vegan commitment. |
| **Whole food plant-based (WFPB)** | Minimally processed plant foods. Some WFPB practitioners still use honey or occasional animal products. Not synonymous with vegan. |

**For Nathan:** Only "Certified Vegan" or explicitly labeled vegan products meet the standard. "Plant-based" is insufficient.

## Reading Labels for Protein Powders

### Protein Powder Checklist

Before buying any protein powder:

- [ ] Is it vegan certified? (Not just "plant-based")
- [ ] Third-party tested? (NSF, Informed Sport, or ConsumerLab)
- [ ] Heavy metals tested?
- [ ] Protein source? (Pea + rice blend > hemp alone > single source)
- [ ] Protein per 100g ≥70g? (Calculate: grams protein ÷ serving size × 100)
- [ ] No gelatin capsules or animal-derived fillers?
- [ ] Sweetener? (Prefer stevia, monk fruit, or unsweetened over sucralose/aspartame)
- [ ] Short ingredient list? (Long lists often = cheap fillers and proprietary blends)
- [ ] No "proprietary blends" hiding individual amounts?

### Red Flags in Protein Powders

- Collagen listed as a protein source (animal-derived, incomplete protein)
- "Whey blend" or "casein" in an otherwise vegan product
- "Natural flavors" without clarification
- Proprietary blend (you don't know how much of each ingredient)
- Heavy metal reports available? If not, check ConsumerLab

## Reading Labels at Restaurants

### Questions to Ask

1. "What oil do you cook in?" — Lard in traditional Mexican, butter in French
2. "Is the pasta made with eggs?" — Fresh pasta almost always is
3. "Does that soup have chicken stock?" — Many vegetable soups use chicken base
4. "Is there any dairy in that?" — Often in mashed potatoes, sauces, bread
5. "Does the bread have L-cysteine or eggs?" — Many commercial rolls do
6. "Is the tortilla made with lard?" — Traditional flour tortillas often are

### Cuisines Requiring Most Caution

| Cuisine | Common Non-Vegan Hidden Ingredients |
|---------|-----------------------------------|
| French | Butter in everything, stock in everything |
| Italian | Parmesan as finishing (even "vegan" dishes), pasta made with eggs |
| Mexican | Lard in beans and tortillas, cheese/crema as default |
| Thai | Fish sauce in almost everything traditional |
| Japanese | Dashi (fish stock) in miso soup and many dishes |
| Korean | Fish sauce, salted shrimp in kimchi |
| Indian | Ghee (clarified butter), paneer (dairy cheese) |
| American | Butter in vegetables, honey in dressings |

**Strategy:** Always say "I'm vegan — no meat, fish, dairy, eggs, or honey" rather than just "vegetarian." Many chefs understand vegan; vegetarian can be interpreted loosely.

## Alcohol — Vegan Status

Many alcoholic beverages use animal-derived fining agents. **Barnivore.com** is the definitive resource.

| Drink | Typical Status | Issue |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| Most spirits (vodka, gin, rum, whiskey) | ✅ Usually vegan | Some use isinglass or gelatin — check |
| Most beer | ⚠️ Mixed | Isinglass (fish) used to clarify — check Barnivore |
| Most wine | ⚠️ Mixed | Isinglass, gelatin, or egg whites as fining agents |
| Vegan wines (labeled) | ✅ Vegan | Growing category — many natural wines are vegan |
| Hard cider | ⚠️ Mixed | Often use isinglass — check |
| Kombucha | ✅ Vegan | Always vegan |

**Brands to know as vegan-friendly:** Bonterra (wine), Frey Vineyards (wine), most natural wines are unfined.
