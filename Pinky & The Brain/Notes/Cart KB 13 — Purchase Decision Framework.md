# KNOWLEDGE BASE MODULE 13: PURCHASE DECISION FRAMEWORK

## WHY THIS MODULE EXISTS
Every other Cart module tells you HOW to buy smarter. This module asks the harder question: SHOULD you buy it at all? The most powerful money-saving tool isn't a coupon — it's the ability to recognize when your brain is tricking you into spending. This module gives Cart the frameworks to help Nathan evaluate purchases before the buying process even starts.

---

## THE COST-PER-USE MODEL

### The Only Metric That Matters for Physical Goods

Price means nothing without context. A $300 item used 300 times costs $1/use. A $30 item used twice costs $15/use. The $300 item is the better deal.

**Cost Per Use = Purchase Price ÷ Estimated Lifetime Uses**

### CPu Benchmarks

| CPu Range | Verdict | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Under $0.50/use** | Excellent value | $150 boots worn 400 times = $0.38/use |
| **$0.50-$2.00/use** | Good value | $200 jacket worn 150 times = $1.33/use |
| **$2.00-$5.00/use** | Acceptable for quality experience | $80 concert ticket for a memorable night |
| **$5.00-$10.00/use** | Questionable — evaluate honestly | $120 kitchen gadget used 15 times = $8/use |
| **Over $10.00/use** | Red flag — probably a waste | $250 bread maker used 5 times = $50/use |

### Applying CPu to Common Purchases

| Item | Typical Price | Optimistic Uses | Realistic Uses | Realistic CPu |
|------|-------------|----------------|----------------|---------------|
| Cast iron skillet | $25-35 | Lifetime (5,000+) | Lifetime | < $0.01/use |
| Quality chef's knife | $80-150 | 20 years daily | 5,000+ uses | $0.02-0.03/use |
| Cordless drill | $130-180 | 10 years, weekly | 500+ uses | $0.26-0.36/use |
| Stand mixer | $250-400 | Weekly for years | 200-500 uses (realistic for most) | $0.50-2.00/use |
| Bread maker | $100-250 | Weekly | 5-20 uses (then sits in cabinet) | $5.00-50.00/use |
| Juicer | $100-300 | Daily | 10-30 uses (then sits in cabinet) | $3.30-30.00/use |
| Instant Pot | $80-120 | Weekly | 100-300 uses | $0.27-1.20/use |
| Home gym equipment | $500-2,000 | Daily for years | 50-500 uses (wide variance by person) | $1.00-40.00/use |
| Peloton bike | $1,400+ sub | Daily for years | 200-1,000 rides (depending on commitment) | $1.40-7.00/ride |

**Cart's CPu Rule:** Before recommending any purchase over $50, estimate realistic (not optimistic) uses. Be honest about Nathan's actual habits, not aspirational ones. Past behavior predicts future behavior better than good intentions.

## THE PURCHASE PAUSE SYSTEM

### Cooling-Off Periods by Price

Impulse purchases are the single largest source of wasted spending. A mandatory pause between "I want this" and "I'm buying this" eliminates 60-80% of impulse buys — and you won't miss most of them.

| Purchase Price | Minimum Pause | Method |
|---------------|---------------|--------|
| **Under $25** | None required | Quick mental check: "Do I need this, or does my brain want the dopamine hit of buying it?" |
| **$25-$100** | 24 hours | Add to cart but don't checkout. Come back tomorrow. Still want it? |
| **$100-$500** | 72 hours (3 days) | Write down what you want and why. If the desire survives three days, research begins. |
| **$500-$2,000** | 1 week | Full research cycle: identify need, compare options, check timing, run CPu analysis |
| **Over $2,000** | 2 weeks | Complete research + financial impact analysis. Does this fit the budget? What else could this money do? |

**Exception:** True emergencies (broken appliance you use daily, safety equipment, time-limited deals you've already researched). The pause is for desire-driven purchases, not need-driven ones.

### The "Add to Cart" Test

Online retailers design the checkout flow to minimize friction between desire and purchase. Counteract this:

1. Add the item to your cart
2. Close the browser/app
3. Wait the appropriate cooling period
4. When you come back, ask: "If I didn't already have this in my cart, would I seek it out and add it again today?"
5. If yes → proceed with purchase research
6. If no → remove from cart

## COGNITIVE BIASES IN PURCHASING

### The Biases That Cost You Money

Cart should recognize these biases when evaluating Nathan's purchase decisions and flag them directly.

**Anchoring Bias**
- **What it is:** The first price you see sets your mental reference point. A $200 item "on sale" from $400 feels like a deal, even if the item was never worth $400.
- **How it's exploited:** Inflated MSRPs, "compare at" pricing, the three-tier pricing trick (cheap/target/expensive to make the middle feel reasonable)
- **Cart's counter:** Never evaluate a deal based on the discount percentage. Evaluate based on: Is this item worth $X to me at this price, regardless of what it supposedly costs elsewhere?

**Loss Aversion**
- **What it is:** The pain of losing something feels twice as strong as the pleasure of gaining something equivalent. This is why "limited time" and "only 3 left" messaging works.
- **How it's exploited:** Countdown timers, low-stock warnings, "deal expires at midnight," flash sales
- **Cart's counter:** If the deal expires and you miss it, another deal will come. Retailers create urgency because urgency overrides rational evaluation. Any deal that requires you to skip the pause period isn't a deal — it's a trap.

**Sunk Cost Fallacy**
- **What it is:** Continuing to invest in something because you've already spent money on it, even when cutting losses is the rational choice.
- **How it's exploited:** Subscription services (you keep paying because you "already invested"), repair vs. replace decisions, extended warranties on failing products
- **Cart's counter:** Money already spent is gone. The only question is: "From this moment forward, is spending more money the best use of that money?" Ignore what you've already paid.

**Endowment Effect**
- **What it is:** You value things you own more than identical things you don't own. This makes you overpay for upgrades and overvalue trade-ins.
- **How it's exploited:** "Try before you buy" programs, free trial periods (you start to feel ownership), trade-in programs that undervalue your item
- **Cart's counter:** When evaluating a trade-in or upgrade, ask: "If I didn't already own this, would I buy it at the price I'm valuing it?"

**Bandwagon Effect**
- **What it is:** Buying something because everyone else is buying it, assuming popularity equals quality.
- **How it's exploited:** "Best seller" badges, "most popular" labels, influencer marketing, trending products
- **Cart's counter:** Popular ≠ best for you. Check actual review content (not star ratings), look for negative reviews from experienced users, and evaluate against your specific use case.

**The Diderot Effect**
- **What it is:** Buying one new thing triggers a cascade of "complementary" purchases to match. New couch → new pillows → new rug → new coffee table → new curtains.
- **How it's exploited:** "Frequently bought together," "complete the look," product ecosystems (Apple is the master of this)
- **Cart's counter:** Recognize the cascade before it starts. When buying something new, explicitly ask: "What additional purchases will this create?" Factor the cascade cost into the decision, not just the initial item.

## THE NEED/WANT/ASPIRATION FILTER

Before any purchase, classify it:

### Level 1: Need

Something is a **need** if not having it causes a measurable, concrete problem in your life right now.

- Broken tool required for a project this week
- Replacement for a failed appliance you use daily
- Safety equipment
- Groceries

**Cart's treatment:** Skip the pause for true needs. Focus on finding the best value quickly.

### Level 2: Want

Something is a **want** if it would improve your life or experience, but you're functioning fine without it.

- Upgrade from a working tool to a better one
- New gadget that saves 10 minutes a day
- Better version of something you already own
- Entertainment purchases

**Cart's treatment:** Apply the full pause period. Run CPu analysis. Compare against alternatives including "do nothing." Most wants are fine to fulfill — but on your timeline, not the retailer's.

### Level 3: Aspiration

Something is an **aspiration** if it represents who you want to be, not who you are right now.

- Home gym equipment when you don't currently exercise consistently
- Woodworking tools for a hobby you haven't started yet
- A cookbook for a cuisine you've never attempted
- An instrument you've been "meaning to learn"

**Cart's treatment:** This is the danger zone. Aspirational purchases have the worst CPu because the realistic use count is often near zero. Before buying, require evidence: "Have I done the free/cheap version of this activity at least 5 times in the last 30 days?" If yes, the aspiration is becoming a practice, and the purchase may be justified. If no, the purchase is buying a fantasy, not a tool.

## THE TOTAL COST OF OWNERSHIP (TCO) FRAMEWORK

### Beyond the Sticker Price

Every purchase has hidden costs that extend beyond the purchase price. Cart should calculate TCO, not just the item price.

```
Total Cost of Ownership =
    Purchase Price
  + Sales Tax (9.25% in Nashville)
  + Accessories / Consumables (over lifetime)
  + Maintenance / Repair
  + Operating Cost (electricity, fuel, subscriptions)
  + Storage Cost (does it require space you don't have?)
  + Disposal / Replacement Cost
  - Resale Value (at end of useful life)
  ─────────────────────────────────
  = True Lifetime Cost
```

### TCO Examples

**Inkjet Printer ($60 purchase)**
- Purchase: $60
- Ink cartridges (2/year × 5 years): $50/set × 10 = $500
- Paper: $40/year × 5 = $200
- Total 5-year TCO: **$760**
- Per-page cost: $0.15-0.25/page
- **Alternative:** Laser printer ($200 purchase, $30 toner lasts 2,600 pages). 5-year TCO at same usage: **$320**. Half the cost.

**Robot Vacuum ($400 purchase)**
- Purchase: $400
- Replacement brushes/filters (annual): $30 × 4 = $120
- Battery replacement (year 3): $50
- Electricity: negligible
- 4-year TCO: **$570**
- CPu at daily use (1,460 uses): **$0.39/use**
- **Verdict:** Excellent TCO if used daily. Poor if used twice a week.

**Vehicle (covered in Module 05, but the TCO principle applies everywhere)**

### Cart's TCO Rule

For any purchase over $200, calculate at least a 3-year TCO before recommending. Many items that look cheap to buy are expensive to own, and vice versa.

## THE REPLACEMENT DECISION MATRIX

### Repair vs. Replace

When something breaks, the emotional impulse is to buy new. The rational framework:

| Factor | Lean Repair | Lean Replace |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Age vs. expected lifespan** | Under 50% of expected life | Over 70% of expected life |
| **Repair cost vs. replacement cost** | Repair < 40% of replacement cost | Repair > 50% of replacement cost |
| **Performance after repair** | Returns to full function | Partially restored or band-aid fix |
| **Technology / efficiency gap** | Current model is adequate | Newer model has meaningful efficiency gains |
| **Frequency of repairs** | First or second repair | Third+ repair (pattern of failure) |
| **Availability of parts** | Parts readily available | Parts discontinued or hard to find |

**The 50% Rule:** If the repair cost exceeds 50% of the replacement cost AND the item is past 60% of its expected lifespan, replace.

## SUBSCRIPTION PURCHASE TRAPS

### The Subscription Creep Problem

Subscriptions are purchases that repeat forever. A $15/month subscription is a $180/year purchase, every year. Most people underestimate their total subscription spend by 2-3x.

### The Annual Cost Reframe

When evaluating any subscription, immediately convert to annual cost:

| Monthly | Annual | 5-Year |
|---------|--------|--------|
| $5 | $60 | $300 |
| $10 | $120 | $600 |
| $15 | $180 | $900 |
| $20 | $240 | $1,200 |
| $30 | $360 | $1,800 |
| $50 | $600 | $3,000 |

**Cart's Subscription Rule:** For every subscription, ask: "If this cost $[annual amount] as a one-time purchase today, would I buy it?" If the answer is no, you're paying for it because the monthly amount feels painless, not because it's worth the money.

Also see: [[Cart KB 04 — Subscriptions and Digital]] for subscription audit methodology.

## OPPORTUNITY COST — THE INVISIBLE PRICE TAG

### What Else Could This Money Do?

Every dollar spent on one thing is a dollar that can't be spent on (or invested in) something else. Cart should frame major purchases in terms of alternatives:

**The Investment Alternative:**
- $500 invested in an index fund, growing at 7% annually for 10 years = $983
- That $500 purchase actually costs $983 in future value
- This doesn't mean never spend money — it means be honest about the real cost

**The Experience Alternative:**
- Would you rather have [this item] or [this experience] for the same money?
- Research consistently shows experiences produce more lasting happiness than possessions
- Exception: items that enable experiences (tools enable building; bikes enable riding; kitchen equipment enables cooking)

**The Time Alternative:**
- How many hours of work does this purchase represent?
- At Nathan's effective hourly rate, a $300 purchase = X hours of work
- "Is this item worth X hours of my life?" is a clarifying question

## THE PURCHASE RECOMMENDATION FORMAT

When Cart evaluates a purchase for Nathan, the recommendation should include:

```
PURCHASE EVALUATION: [Item Name]
─────────────────────────────────
Classification: [Need / Want / Aspiration]
Price: $X
Nashville Sales Tax (9.25%): $X
Total Out-the-Door: $X

CPu Analysis:
  Estimated uses: [X uses over Y years]
  Cost per use: $X/use
  CPu Verdict: [Excellent / Good / Acceptable / Questionable / Red Flag]

TCO (3-year):
  Purchase: $X
  Consumables / Maintenance: $X
  Operating Cost: $X
  Total: $X

Timing:
  Best time to buy: [Season / Event / Now]
  Current timing verdict: [Good time / Wait for X / Doesn't matter]

Alternatives Considered:
  1. [Alternative A] — $X, trade-off: [description]
  2. [Alternative B] — $X, trade-off: [description]
  3. Do nothing — [consequence of not buying]

Bias Check:
  [Any detected biases in the decision: anchoring, loss aversion, aspiration, etc.]

Cart's Recommendation: [Buy / Buy but wait for [timing] / Skip / Need more info]
Confidence: [High / Medium / Low]
```

**This format is the default for purchases over $100.** For under $100, a shorter version is fine. For under $25, a single sentence suffices.

## GOLDEN RULES: PURCHASE DECISIONS

1. **Cost per use is the only honest price.** A $300 item used 300 times is cheaper than a $30 item used twice.
2. **Apply the pause.** 24 hours for $25-100. 72 hours for $100-500. 1 week for $500+. If the desire doesn't survive the pause, it wasn't real.
3. **Need, Want, or Aspiration?** Be honest about the category. Aspirational purchases have the worst ROI because the usage rarely matches the fantasy.
4. **Calculate TCO, not sticker price.** The printer is $60; the ink is $500.
5. **Recognize the bias.** When Cart detects anchoring, urgency, sunk cost, or aspirational buying, name it directly.
6. **Opportunity cost is real.** Every purchase competes with every other use of that money. Frame it.
7. **"If I didn't already have this in my cart, would I seek it out today?"** The honest answer to this question eliminates most impulse buys.
8. **Past behavior predicts future behavior.** Don't buy based on who you want to be. Buy based on what you actually do.
9. **Subscriptions are annual purchases in disguise.** Convert to annual cost. Ask if you'd pay that as a lump sum.
10. **The best purchase is often no purchase.** Doing nothing is always an option, and it's free.
