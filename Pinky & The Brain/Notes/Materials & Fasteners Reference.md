# Materials & Fasteners Reference

> **Purpose:** What things are made of and how they're held together. Material selection drives cost, performance, durability, and workability. Fastener selection is equally critical — the wrong fastener in the right material fails just as badly as the wrong material in the right application. This note covers lumber, sheet goods, concrete, masonry, insulation, and fastener systems.

---

## Lumber — Dimensional and Structural

### Nominal vs. Actual Dimensions

This is the most common source of confusion for new builders. Lumber is sold by nominal (name) size, but the actual dimensions are smaller due to milling and drying.

| Nominal | Actual | Common Use |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| 2x4 | 1-1/2" × 3-1/2" | Wall studs, blocking, plates |
| 2x6 | 1-1/2" × 5-1/2" | Exterior wall studs, joists, rafters |
| 2x8 | 1-1/2" × 7-1/4" | Floor joists, headers, rafters |
| 2x10 | 1-1/2" × 9-1/4" | Floor joists, headers, rafters |
| 2x12 | 1-1/2" × 11-1/4" | Stair stringers, headers, joists |
| 4x4 | 3-1/2" × 3-1/2" | Posts (non-structural — undersized for many applications) |
| 6x6 | 5-1/2" × 5-1/2" | Posts (structural), deck posts, timber framing |
| 1x4 | 3/4" × 3-1/2" | Trim, furring, strapping |
| 1x6 | 3/4" × 5-1/2" | Trim, shelving, fence boards |

### Species and Grades

**Species groups** (common in the Southeast):
- **Southern Yellow Pine (SYP)** — Strong, takes pressure treatment well, most common structural lumber in Nashville area. Prone to warping if not properly dried. Check moisture content — 19% or below for framing (stamped "KD" for kiln-dried or "S-DRY" for surfaced dry).
- **Spruce-Pine-Fir (SPF)** — Lighter, easier to work, commonly used for studs and light framing. Less strong than SYP per grade. Standard for many big-box store stud stock.
- **Douglas Fir** — Strong, stiff, excellent for beams and joists. More common in western US but available everywhere. Premium cost.
- **Cedar** — Naturally rot-resistant, lightweight, used for exterior trim, decking, siding, and fence boards. Not structural. Expensive.

**Grades** (lower number = fewer defects = stronger):
- **Select Structural** — Highest grade, fewest knots and defects. Used when appearance and maximum strength both matter.
- **#1** — Few defects, high strength. Used for exposed structural applications.
- **#2** — Moderate defects, standard structural grade. The default for most residential framing.
- **#3 / Stud grade** — More knots and defects. Acceptable for wall studs (short spans, not spanning members). Not suitable for joists, rafters, or headers.
- **Utility** — Non-structural only. Blocking, bracing, temporary work.

### Pressure-Treated Lumber

Wood treated with chemical preservatives (copper-based — ACQ or CA-C since CCA was phased out for residential) to resist rot, decay, and insect damage. Required by code for:
- Bottom plates on concrete (sill plates, basement wall plates)
- Posts or wood within 6" of soil
- Structural members in ground contact
- Deck framing, joists, and posts (if not naturally rot-resistant species)

**Important:** Pressure-treated wood requires specific fasteners — hot-dipped galvanized, stainless steel, or rated for ACQ/CA-C exposure. Standard zinc-plated fasteners will corrode rapidly in contact with treated wood. The copper in the treatment is the culprit.

### Moisture Content

Fresh-cut lumber ("green") contains 30%+ moisture. As it dries, it shrinks, warps, twists, and checks (cracks along the grain). For framing:
- **KD (kiln-dried):** 19% or below at time of surfacing. Standard for big-box store lumber. May still have 15-25% MC at purchase depending on storage.
- **KD-HT (kiln-dried, heat-treated):** Meets phytosanitary requirements. Same moisture standard.
- **S-GRN (surfaced green):** Above 19% MC. Cheaper but will shrink significantly after installation. Acceptable for framing if you expect settlement, but avoid for finish applications.

For finish carpentry, lumber should be acclimated to the interior environment (8-12% MC) before installation. Stacking with spacers ("stickering") in the installation space for 1-2 weeks is standard practice.

## Sheet Goods

### Plywood

Layers of wood veneer glued with alternating grain directions. Stronger than the sum of its parts because the cross-lamination resists splitting and distributes loads. Key grades:

| Grade | Face/Back | Use |
|-------|-----------|-----|
| A-A | Smooth both sides | Cabinetry, furniture, visible applications |
| A-B | Smooth front, patched back | Cabinet backs, shelving |
| B-C | Patched front, utility back | Sheathing, underlayment, concealed use |
| CDX | Rough both sides, exterior glue | Roof and wall sheathing (the "X" means exposure-rated, not exterior-rated) |

**APA ratings to know:**
- **Structural I** — All veneers from the same species group. Required for some shear wall applications.
- **Exposure 1** — Moisture-resistant glue. Suitable for temporary weather exposure during construction but not permanent outdoor use.
- **Exterior** — Fully waterproof glue. For permanent outdoor or wet applications.

### OSB (Oriented Strand Board)

Wood strands compressed with resin. Cheaper than plywood, consistent quality, widely used for sheathing and subfloor. Performance comparison with plywood:

| Property | Plywood | OSB |
|----------|---------|-----|
| Moisture resistance | Better — dries faster, less edge swell | Worse — edges swell when wet and don't recover |
| Stiffness | Slightly less | Slightly more |
| Nail holding | Good | Good |
| Price | 15-30% more | Baseline |
| Availability | Variable | Consistent |
| Weight | Lighter | Heavier |

**The edge-swell issue:** OSB's main weakness. If the edges get wet (from rain during construction, a plumbing leak, or poor flashing), they swell permanently. This creates visible ridges under roofing and bumps under flooring. Plywood handles moisture much more gracefully. If the project will be exposed to weather during construction, plywood is the safer choice.

### ZIP System

OSB panels with an integrated water-resistive barrier (WRB) surface and proprietary tape system. Eliminates the need for separate housewrap. The taped seams create a continuous air and water barrier. More expensive per panel but saves the labor and material cost of housewrap installation. Increasingly popular in residential construction for wall and roof sheathing.

### MDF (Medium-Density Fiberboard)

Wood fibers bonded with resin under heat and pressure. Smooth, flat, consistent, and inexpensive. Excellent for paint-grade trim, cabinet doors, and shelving. Does NOT tolerate moisture — swells irreversibly when wet. Not structural. Produces fine dust when cut (wear a dust mask, always). Available in standard and moisture-resistant (MR) grades.

## Concrete Basics

### Mix Design

Concrete is a mix of Portland cement, sand (fine aggregate), gravel (coarse aggregate), and water. The ratio determines the strength.

**Compressive strength** is measured in PSI at 28 days:
- **2,500 PSI** — Minimum code for residential footings and foundation walls in many jurisdictions
- **3,000 PSI** — Standard for residential slabs, footings, and foundation walls. The most common residential mix.
- **3,500 PSI** — Driveways, garage slabs, areas subject to vehicle loads
- **4,000+ PSI** — Commercial applications, high-strength requirements, freeze-thaw exposed surfaces

**Water-to-cement ratio** is the most important factor controlling strength and durability. More water makes concrete easier to pour and work but dramatically reduces strength. Target w/c ratio for residential: 0.45-0.50. If concrete arrives too stiff, add small amounts of water at the truck — but know that every gallon of water added reduces the compressive strength by approximately 200 PSI.

### Curing

Concrete reaches design strength at 28 days but the first 7 days are critical. Concrete must be kept moist during curing — if it dries too fast, it doesn't reach full strength and develops shrinkage cracks. Curing methods: wet burlap, plastic sheeting, spray-on curing compound, or ponding (flooding) the surface.

**Temperature matters:** Below 50°F, chemical reactions slow dramatically. Below freezing, water in fresh concrete can freeze and destroy the internal structure. Hot weather (above 90°F) accelerates setting and can cause premature cracking. In Nashville, cold pours (November through March) and hot pours (June through August) both require special attention.

### Reinforcement

**Rebar** — Steel bars embedded in concrete to resist tension (concrete is strong in compression, weak in tension). Residential applications: #4 (1/2" diameter) rebar is standard for footings and foundation walls. Placed at mid-depth in footings and at the tension face (outside for below-grade walls, inside for retaining walls). Minimum cover: 3" from soil contact, 2" from formed surfaces, 1.5" in slabs.

**Welded wire mesh (WWM)** — Grid of steel wires used in slabs for crack control. Standard: 6x6-W1.4/W1.4 (6" grid, light gauge). Placed at mid-depth of the slab (support on chairs — it does nothing sitting on the ground under 4" of concrete). Controls crack width but does not prevent cracking.

**Fiber mesh** — Synthetic or steel fibers mixed into concrete to reduce plastic shrinkage cracking. Not a structural reinforcement substitute. Useful as a supplement to WWM in slabs.

## Insulation Materials

| Material | R-Value Per Inch | Air Barrier? | Moisture Behavior | Best For |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|-------------------|----------|
| Fiberglass batts | R-3.1 to R-3.7 | No | Absorbs and holds moisture; dries slowly | Standard wall and attic cavities (budget option) |
| Blown fiberglass | R-2.5 to R-3.7 | No | Same as batts | Attic floors, dense-pack in walls (better coverage than batts) |
| Cellulose (blown) | R-3.2 to R-3.8 | No (but slows air when dense-packed) | Absorbs moisture but borate treatment resists mold | Attic floors, dense-pack retrofit walls |
| Open-cell spray foam | R-3.6 to R-3.8 | Yes | Vapor-permeable; allows drying | Cathedral ceilings, complex cavities, air sealing priority |
| Closed-cell spray foam | R-6.0 to R-7.0 | Yes | Vapor retarder; moisture barrier | Rim joists, crawlspaces, thin cavities needing high R-value, flood zones |
| Rigid EPS (expanded polystyrene) | R-3.8 to R-4.4 | No | Absorbs small amounts of moisture over time | Below-grade insulation, exterior continuous, under-slab |
| Rigid XPS (extruded polystyrene) | R-5.0 | No | Moisture-resistant | Below-grade, exterior continuous (higher moisture resistance than EPS) |
| Rigid polyiso | R-5.7 to R-6.5 | No (but taped seams can serve as air barrier) | Absorbs moisture at cut edges; R-value drops in cold temps | Above-grade exterior continuous insulation (walls, roofs) |
| Mineral wool batts | R-3.7 to R-4.2 | No | Hydrophobic (repels water); fire-resistant | Fire-rated assemblies, exterior continuous, sound control |

## Fastener Systems

### Nail Types and Uses

| Nail Type | Characteristics | Use |
|-----------|----------------|-----|
| Common nails | Thick shank, flat head, smooth or ring-shank | Structural framing — shear walls, plates, connections |
| Box nails | Thinner shank than common (less splitting) | Framing where splitting is a concern, sheathing |
| Sinker nails | Vinyl-coated, slightly thinner, countersink head | Production framing (most framing nailers use sinkers) |
| Ring-shank | Annular rings on shank (400%+ holding power vs smooth) | Subfloor, permanent connections, high-withdrawal applications |
| Finish nails | Small head, thin shank | Trim, casing, baseboard, crown — head is set below surface and filled |
| Brad nails (18-gauge) | Very thin, small head | Lightweight trim, small moldings, temporary holding |
| Pin nails (23-gauge) | Headless, thinnest gauge | Miter joints, delicate trim, nearly invisible |
| Roofing nails | Large flat head, smooth or ring-shank | Roofing shingles, building paper, WRB attachment |
| Galvanized/stainless | Corrosion-resistant coatings | Exterior, pressure-treated lumber, coastal environments |

### Nail Sizing (Penny System)

| Penny Size | Length | Common Use |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| 6d | 2" | Light framing, furring, trim backing |
| 8d | 2-1/2" | Sheathing to studs, subfloor face-nailing |
| 10d | 3" | Framing connections (most common framing nail) |
| 12d | 3-1/4" | Double plates, built-up headers |
| 16d | 3-1/2" | Structural framing connections, toe-nailing studs to plates |

### Screw Types

| Screw Type | Use | Notes |
|-----------|-----|-------|
| Drywall screws | Drywall to studs | NOT for structural use — brittle, snap under shear load |
| Deck screws | Decking to joists, exterior applications | Coated for corrosion resistance. Use ACQ-rated for treated lumber. |
| Structural screws (GRK, SDWS, RSS) | Structural connections, replacing lag bolts in many applications | Engineer-rated, published load values. Game-changer for structural carpentry. |
| Cabinet screws | Cabinets to walls | Washer head for pullout resistance, fine thread for hardwood |
| Pocket screws (Kreg) | Face-frame assembly, cabinet boxes | Self-tapping, fine or coarse thread depending on material |
| Lag screws/bolts | Heavy structural connections, ledger boards | Require pre-drilling. Being replaced by structural screws in many applications. |
| Concrete screws (Tapcon) | Fastening to concrete and masonry | Require pre-drilled hole with specific masonry bit size |

### The Critical Rule: Match the Fastener to the Material

| Material | Required Fastener | Why |
|----------|-------------------|-----|
| Pressure-treated lumber (ACQ/CA-C) | Hot-dipped galvanized, stainless, or ACQ-rated | Copper in treatment corrodes standard zinc-plated fasteners |
| Cedar/redwood | Stainless steel | Tannins cause black staining with regular steel |
| Exterior applications | Hot-dipped galvanized or stainless | Electroplated (silver-colored) fasteners will rust. Hot-dipped (rough gray coating) lasts. |
| Structural connections | Only the fastener specified by the connector manufacturer | Simpson connectors are rated for specific nail/screw types. Substituting reduces load capacity. |
| Engineered wood (LVL, I-joists) | Per manufacturer specification | Some engineered products prohibit certain fasteners or require specific types |

## Adhesives

| Adhesive | Use | Key Properties |
|----------|-----|---------------|
| PL Premium (polyurethane) | Subfloor to joists, structural bonding | Waterproof, gap-filling, strong. The standard construction adhesive. |
| Liquid Nails Heavy Duty | General construction bonding | Cheaper than PL Premium, adequate for most applications |
| Titebond III (wood glue) | Trim joints, cabinet assembly, miter joints | Waterproof wood glue. Stronger than the wood itself when properly clamped. |
| Titebond II | Interior wood joints | Water-resistant (not waterproof). Lower cost than III. |
| PL 300 (foam board adhesive) | Rigid foam insulation to foundation walls | Won't dissolve foam (some adhesives contain solvents that melt EPS/XPS) |
| Construction adhesive caulk | Trim to wall, backerboard, miscellaneous | Flexible, gap-filling, moderate strength |
