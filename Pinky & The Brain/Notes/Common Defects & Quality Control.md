# Common Defects & Quality Control

> **Purpose:** What bad work looks like and how to catch it — from structural failures that compromise safety to cosmetic issues that signal a lack of care. For a PM, this is the field inspection reference: what to look for on site walks and what questions to ask when something doesn't look right. For a personal builder, this is the quality standard to hold yourself to and the checklist for evaluating your own work before an inspector does.

---

## The Defect Severity Framework

Not all defects are equal. Categorizing severity guides the urgency of response:

**Critical (stop work, correct immediately):**
- Structural member undersized, missing, or improperly connected
- Load path broken (missing hold-down, severed strap, inadequate anchor bolt)
- Fire-stopping absent in required locations
- Electrical hazard (exposed live wiring, improper grounding)
- Fall hazard (missing guardrails, unsecured scaffolding)

**Major (correct before next phase proceeds):**
- Framing out of plumb or level beyond tolerance
- Shear wall nailing incorrect (pattern, nail size, edge distance)
- MEP rough conflicts (trades drilled through each other's work)
- Moisture barrier improperly lapped or missing at critical transitions
- Insulation gaps, compression, or missing coverage

**Minor (correct during punch list or before finishes):**
- Cosmetic framing imperfections (minor bow, slight twist in non-structural member)
- Drywall imperfections visible under raking light but not direct light
- Paint touch-up needed
- Trim joints not tight (within repair tolerance — caulk and fill)
- Minor grade settlement away from foundation

## Foundation Defects

| Defect | What It Looks Like | Cause | Severity |
|--------|-------------------|-------|----------|
| Honeycombing | Rough, rocky surface with visible voids where aggregate shows through | Concrete not properly vibrated during pour, mix too stiff, or form leakage | Major — reduces bearing strength and allows water infiltration |
| Cold joint | Visible line in the wall where two pours met | Too much time between successive lifts of the pour | Major — weak plane that can crack under lateral pressure |
| Anchor bolt misplacement | Bolts don't align with plate layout, too close to edge, wrong spacing | No bolt plan coordinated with framer before pour | Major — may require retrofit anchors (epoxy anchors or added bolts) |
| Cracking (structural) | Wide cracks (>1/8"), horizontal cracks, or stair-step cracks in block | Lateral soil pressure, settlement, overloaded wall, premature backfill | Critical — requires engineer evaluation |
| Cracking (shrinkage) | Hairline vertical cracks, typically within first year | Normal concrete curing shrinkage | Minor — cosmetic unless they allow water through, then seal |
| Waterproofing gaps | Water in basement or crawlspace, efflorescence (white mineral deposits) on interior walls | Missing or incomplete exterior waterproofing, poor drainage, no drain tile | Major to Critical — water damage compounds over time |

### Foundation Inspection Tips

- Check anchor bolt spacing (max 6' OC, within 12" of corners and plate ends per IRC)
- Look for minimum 3" rebar cover from soil-contact surfaces
- Verify footing width and depth match plans (or code minimum: typically 12" wide × 6" deep for one-story, 15" × 7" for two-story)
- Check that the top of foundation is level — this affects everything above it
- Verify waterproofing extends from footing to grade (not just a partial coat)

## Framing Defects

| Defect | What It Looks Like | Cause | Severity |
|--------|-------------------|-------|----------|
| Missing header | Opening in bearing wall with no header (just top plate spanning) | Framer didn't identify the wall as bearing, or "expedient" shortcut | Critical — immediate structural concern |
| Undersized header | Header is too small for the span or the load above | Framer used stock on hand instead of checking span tables | Critical — header may deflect or fail under full load |
| Over-notched/over-bored members | Large holes or notches in joists, studs, or rafters beyond code limits | MEP trades needed routing space and cut without checking limits | Major — weakened member may fail under load. Requires sistering or replacement. |
| Missing hold-downs/straps | Shear walls without proper anchorage at base and top | Hardware not specified, not delivered, or skipped by framer | Critical — shear wall cannot function without the complete load path |
| Shear wall nailing errors | Wrong nail size, wrong spacing, nails too close to edge (causes splitting), pneumatic nails over-driven | Production speed, air pressure too high on nailer, lack of awareness | Major — reduces shear wall capacity. Re-nailing or additional panels needed. |
| Walls out of plumb | Walls visibly leaning or measured >1/4" off plumb in 8' | Poor bracing during raising, foundation not level, rushing | Major if caught before sheathing (fixable). Minor to Major after sheathing (much harder to correct). |
| Crown inconsistency in joists | Floor feels bouncy, visible dips | Joists installed with random crown direction (some up, some down) | Minor to Major — causes uneven floors. All joists should be installed crown up. |
| Missing fire-stopping | Gaps at floor/ceiling penetrations where fire could spread between stories | Overlooked during framing, MEP trades created openings that weren't sealed | Major — code requirement and life safety issue |

### Framing Inspection Tips

- Carry a 4' level on every site walk. Check random walls for plumb and random headers for level.
- Count shear wall nails and measure spacing — this is the most commonly failed framing inspection item.
- Look at connections — are straps installed with all fastener holes filled? Are hold-downs bolted (not just placed)?
- Check joist/rafter bearing — minimum 1.5" bearing on wood, 3" on masonry/concrete.
- Look for notches/holes near midspan of joists — that's the worst location structurally.

## Envelope Defects (Moisture & Air Barrier)

| Defect | What It Looks Like | Cause | Severity |
|--------|-------------------|-------|----------|
| Reverse-lapped WRB | Upper course tucked behind lower course (water channels behind the barrier) | Installer worked top-down instead of bottom-up, or didn't lap at horizontal seams | Major — defeats the drainage plane |
| Missing flashing at windows | No sill pan, side flashing not integrated with WRB, head flashing absent | Installer relied on caulk instead of flashing, or didn't follow the flashing sequence | Major — windows are the most common water intrusion point |
| Missing kick-out flashing | No diverter where roof meets wall at the eave (water runs down the roof and behind the siding) | Both roofer and siding sub assumed the other would handle it | Critical — causes severe hidden water damage, often undetected for years |
| Sealed bottom of window flashing | Sill flashing taped or sealed at the bottom (traps any water that enters) | Misunderstanding of how flashing works — the bottom must allow drainage | Major — water accumulates instead of draining |
| Discontinuous air barrier | Gaps at transitions (wall-to-roof, wall-to-foundation, around penetrations) | Air sealing wasn't done systematically, or trades created penetrations after air sealing | Major — air leakage carries moisture and wastes energy |
| Siding installed directly on WRB without rainscreen gap | Siding traps moisture against the WRB with no airspace for drying | Builder followed minimum code (no rainscreen required in all zones) but best practice calls for drainage gap | Minor in dry climates, Major in humid climates |

### Envelope Inspection Tips

- Pull back a section of siding at a window to check flashing integration (if accessible during construction)
- Look for the kick-out flashing at every roof-to-wall intersection — its absence is the number one missed detail
- Check that WRB seams are taped and lapped correctly (upper over lower, like shingles)
- At the foundation-to-frame transition, verify the sill seal is in place and the WRB terminates below the bottom plate
- After air sealing, look for spray foam at every penetration through the building envelope (wires, pipes, ducts, vents)

## MEP Defects

| Defect | What It Looks Like | Cause | Severity |
|--------|-------------------|-------|----------|
| GFCI protection missing | Non-GFCI receptacles within 6' of water sources (kitchen, bath, garage, exterior, unfinished basement) | Electrician missed locations, or owner requested standard receptacles | Major — code requirement and safety issue |
| Plumbing not properly vented | Gurgling drains, slow drainage, sewer gas smell | Vent pipe too far from trap, missing vent, or undersized vent | Major — affects function and indoor air quality |
| HVAC ductwork in unconditioned space without insulation | Condensation on ducts, energy loss, uneven room temperatures | Ducts run through attic or crawlspace without insulation wrap | Major — significant energy waste and moisture risk |
| Gas piping without proper test | No documented pressure test on gas piping system | Test skipped or not documented | Critical — gas leaks are explosive hazards |
| Electrical box overfilled | Too many wires/devices in one box (exceeds NEC box fill calculations) | Electrician added circuits without upsizing the box | Major — fire risk from heat buildup and damaged insulation |
| No combustion air for gas appliances | Gas furnace or water heater in sealed closet without air supply | Closet was sealed for noise or aesthetics without providing required combustion air | Critical — produces carbon monoxide |

## Finish Defects

| Defect | What It Looks Like | Cause | Severity |
|--------|-------------------|-------|----------|
| Drywall nail/screw pops | Small bumps or circles visible on painted walls | Framing shrinkage pulling fasteners through the drywall face | Minor — cosmetic. Fill, sand, touch up. |
| Uneven drywall joints | Visible ridges or depressions at tape joints, especially under raking light | Insufficient coats, improper feathering, or over-sanding | Minor — cosmetic but indicates overall quality level |
| Trim joints opening | Gaps at mitered corners, especially outside corners | Wood shrinkage after installation (MC was too high at install) | Minor — fill with caulk (paint-grade) or color-matched filler (stain-grade) |
| Cabinet faces not aligned | Doors and drawers don't sit in the same plane, gaps uneven | Cabinets not properly shimmed to wall, or wall significantly out of plumb | Minor to Major — adjust hinges and shims. May indicate wall framing issues. |
| Floor squeaks | Audible creaking when walking | Subfloor not glued, nails instead of screws, joist shrinkage | Minor — annoying but not structural. Difficult to fix after flooring is installed. |
| Door won't close properly | Sticks, swings open, latch misaligned | Jamb not plumb, hinges not properly mortised, house settling | Minor — adjust hinges, reposition strike plate, or re-shim jamb |

### Finish Quality Evaluation

The standard for evaluating finish quality is viewing distance and lighting:

- **Level 4 drywall finish** (standard residential): No visible imperfections under normal lighting at normal viewing distance (4-6 feet). Under strong raking light (held parallel to the wall), minor imperfections are acceptable.
- **Level 5 drywall finish** (premium): No imperfections under any lighting. Achieved by skim-coating the entire surface. Specified for high-gloss paint or critical lighting conditions.
- **Trim tolerance:** Joints should be tight (no visible gap from 3 feet). Reveals should be consistent (1/16" maximum variance along a casing run). Corners should be sharp and aligned.

## The Quality Walk — A Structured Approach

When walking a project for quality (as PM or as builder self-checking):

### By Phase

**After framing (before MEP rough):**
- [ ] Walls plumb (check random sample with 4' level)
- [ ] Headers present and properly sized at all bearing wall openings
- [ ] Shear wall nailing correct (nail size, spacing, edge distance)
- [ ] Hold-downs and straps installed with full fastener count
- [ ] Fire-stopping at floor/ceiling penetrations
- [ ] Backing installed for wall-mounted items (cabinets, grab bars, TV mounts)
- [ ] Subfloor glued and fastened (no squeaks when walking)

**After MEP rough (before insulation):**
- [ ] No over-notched or over-bored framing members
- [ ] Plumbing pressure test passed and documented
- [ ] GFCI and AFCI locations correct per code
- [ ] Duct routing doesn't conflict with insulation or structural members
- [ ] Nail plates installed where pipes/wires are within 1-1/4" of stud face
- [ ] Low-voltage wiring pulled (data, security, speakers)

**After insulation (before drywall):**
- [ ] All cavities filled without gaps, compression, or voids
- [ ] Air sealing at every penetration (foam or caulk at all wire/pipe holes through plates)
- [ ] Rim joist insulated and air sealed
- [ ] Attic bypasses sealed (plumbing stacks, duct chases, electrical chases)
- [ ] Vapor retarder (if required) on correct side

**After drywall:**
- [ ] Joints smooth under raking light (hold a flashlight parallel to the wall surface)
- [ ] No visible fastener pops
- [ ] Corners straight
- [ ] All switch, outlet, and fixture boxes accessible (not buried under mud)

**After trim (punch list):**
- [ ] Doors swing freely, latch properly, consistent reveals
- [ ] Baseboard joints tight, consistent height, follows floor contour
- [ ] All paint touch-up complete
- [ ] No visible caulk gaps at trim-to-wall joints
- [ ] Cabinet doors and drawers aligned and functional
- [ ] All fixtures operational (faucets, toilets, lights, outlets)
- [ ] Exterior: flashing visible at all transitions, siding courses straight, no missing caulk

## Documenting Defects

When you find a defect, document it immediately:

1. **Photo** with wide context (which wall/room) and close-up detail
2. **Location** (room, wall orientation, approximate height)
3. **Description** of the defect (what's wrong, what it should be)
4. **Severity** (critical / major / minor)
5. **Responsible party** (which trade)
6. **Required correction** (what needs to happen to fix it)

A shared punch list (spreadsheet, app, or printed list) with photos, locations, and clear correction descriptions is the most efficient way to close out defects. Vague descriptions ("fix the thing in the bathroom") create return trips and frustration.
